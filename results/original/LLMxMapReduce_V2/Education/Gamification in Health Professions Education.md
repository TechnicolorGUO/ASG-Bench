## 2. Section 2: Theoretical and Conceptual Frameworks
The theoretical and conceptual foundations of gamification in health professions education reveal a diverse landscape of frameworks and conceptualizations, reflecting the multifaceted nature of gamified learning interventions. A central theme across the reviewed literature is the integration of psychological and behavioral theories to guide the design and evaluation of gamified systems. Self-Determination Theory (SDT) and the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) are the most frequently cited theoretical frameworks, each offering distinct perspectives on how gamification influences learner motivation and system adoption. SDT emphasizes intrinsic motivation through the fulfillment of autonomy, competence, and relatedness, and has been applied to structure gamification elements such as goal-setting, feedback, and social interaction [3]. In contrast, TAM focuses on the perceived usefulness and ease of use of gamification tools, providing a quantitative lens for assessing user acceptance and system integration [3].

Despite the prominence of SDT and TAM, the literature indicates a limited exploration of alternative models, such as the ARCS Model and the Gameful Design Framework. These models, which emphasize attention, relevance, confidence, and satisfaction, or the intentional design of game elements to foster engagement, have not been widely adopted in health professions education, suggesting a gap in the theoretical underpinnings of gamification research. The differential adoption of these models reflects varying research priorities: SDT and TAM are often used to address both the psychological and practical dimensions of gamification, whereas other frameworks may offer complementary insights that have yet to be fully integrated. This lack of interdisciplinary integration—such as combining SDT with ARCS or TAM with Gameful Design—limits the comprehensive understanding of gamification’s impact.

In terms of conceptualization, the literature reveals significant variability in how gamification is defined and applied. Some studies emphasize competitive mechanics, such as points, leaderboards, and immediate feedback, to enhance motivation and performance, particularly in continuing medical education (CME) settings [2]. Others adopt a broader conceptualization, focusing on the integration of game elements into non-game contexts to enhance learner engagement and motivation, though without a detailed classification of gamification types [3]. Additionally, some studies frame gamification through the lens of serious games, emphasizing real-world applicability in training complex skills, yet often without a systematic breakdown of specific gamification components [1].

These conceptual differences have implications for the outcomes and perceptions of gamification in health professions education. Competitive gamification, as demonstrated in the study using Kahoot!, appears effective in improving knowledge retention and performance, particularly in structured settings. However, the lack of a standardized framework for gamification may lead to variability in implementation and outcomes. The broader conceptualization that includes narrative and collaborative elements may foster deeper engagement and intrinsic motivation, but its effectiveness depends on context and learner characteristics. This variability underscores the need for more systematic frameworks that can guide the design and evaluation of gamified learning interventions, ensuring that they are both effective and adaptable to different educational needs.

Overall, the theoretical and conceptual frameworks of gamification in health professions education are evolving, with ongoing efforts to align psychological theories with practical implementation strategies. Future research should consider multi-theoretical approaches to better capture the complex interplay between motivation, usability, and engagement, while also addressing the gaps in conceptual standardization and theoretical diversity.

## 1. Section 1: Introduction to Physics-informed Neural Networks in Fluid Mechanics
Physics-informed Neural Networks (PINNs) represent a transformative approach in fluid mechanics, where physical laws—typically expressed as partial differential equations (PDEs)—are embedded directly into the training process of neural networks. This integration ensures that the learned models not only approximate data but also conform to the fundamental principles governing fluid behavior. The conceptual evolution of PINNs in this domain is marked by a shift from purely data-driven models to hybrid frameworks that combine the strengths of machine learning with the rigor of physics-based modeling [2,4].

The initial conceptualization of PINNs, as outlined in the foundational paper, emphasizes their role as a bridge between data-driven and physics-based modeling. This work highlights the motivation for developing PINNs to address the limitations of traditional computational fluid dynamics (CFD) methods, particularly in scenarios where data is sparse or noisy. The authors frame PINNs as a means to enhance the accuracy and interpretability of fluid flow predictions by incorporating the governing equations of fluid dynamics directly into the loss function. This perspective positions PINNs as a complementary tool rather than a complete replacement for conventional solvers, emphasizing their potential to augment existing methodologies [2].

In contrast, the paper on simulating three-dimensional turbulence with PINNs presents a more ambitious vision, where PINNs are framed as a viable alternative to traditional methods such as Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) and Large Eddy Simulation (LES). The authors argue that PINNs offer a mesh-free, continuous solution to fluid equations, which can significantly reduce computational costs while maintaining high accuracy. This perspective reflects a growing trend in the field toward using PINNs as a standalone solver, particularly for complex and high-dimensional fluid problems. The paper underscores the potential of PINNs to overcome the limitations of grid-based methods, suggesting a shift in the perceived role of these models from data-driven enhancements to independent solvers [1].

Another key contribution to the conceptual foundation of PINNs is found in the review paper on sparse data applications. This work emphasizes the importance of PINNs in scenarios where traditional CFD and experimental techniques are constrained by the availability of high-quality data. The authors frame PINNs as a data-driven enhancement to existing methods, capable of reconstructing fluid flow fields from limited and noisy measurements. This perspective aligns with the broader goal of improving the robustness and generalizability of fluid simulations, particularly in real-world applications where data acquisition is challenging [4].

The conceptual diversity among these works is further reflected in the development of specialized PINN variants, such as the Hybrid Boundary Physics-Informed Neural Network (HB-PINN). This approach addresses the limitations of standard PINNs in handling complex boundary conditions, which are critical in many fluid mechanics problems. The authors frame HB-PINN as an enhancement to the existing PINN framework, emphasizing the need for more robust boundary integration to improve the accuracy of PDE solutions in fluid dynamics. This suggests a continued evolution of PINNs toward more specialized and domain-specific formulations [3].

In summary, the conceptual evolution of PINNs in fluid mechanics reveals a spectrum of perspectives, ranging from their role as data-driven enhancements to their potential as standalone solvers. While some studies position PINNs as a complementary tool to traditional methods, others envision them as a transformative alternative, capable of addressing the computational and data challenges inherent in fluid dynamics. This diversity of viewpoints reflects the dynamic and interdisciplinary nature of the field, where the integration of physics and machine learning continues to push the boundaries of what is possible in fluid simulation and analysis.
## 2. Section 2: Methodological Foundations of PINNs in Fluid Mechanics
The methodological foundations of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) in fluid mechanics are rooted in the integration of physical laws—primarily partial differential equations (PDEs)—into the training process of neural networks. This integration is achieved through the design of physics-informed loss functions, which ensure that the network solutions adhere to the governing equations of fluid dynamics, such as the Navier-Stokes equations. The comparative analysis of methodological approaches across various studies reveals both commonalities and divergences in the implementation of these foundational elements.

Neural network architectures in PINNs for fluid mechanics have predominantly relied on fully connected neural networks (FCNs), with some recent explorations into more specialized structures. The majority of studies, including [2], employ FCNs with multiple hidden layers, where activation functions such as ReLU, tanh, and sigmoid are used to model the non-linear relationships inherent in fluid dynamics. While FCNs remain the most common choice due to their simplicity and effectiveness, some works, such as [1], suggest the need for more advanced architectures, such as those capable of capturing complex spatial-temporal patterns in turbulent flows. These specialized designs, though not yet widely adopted, reflect an emerging trend toward architecture customization for specific fluid mechanics problems.

The design of physics-informed loss functions is a central aspect of PINN methodology, with studies differing in how they structure and weight the components of the loss. A common approach involves a weighted sum of multiple terms, such as data-fitting loss, PDE residual loss, and boundary condition loss. For instance, [5] proposes a loss function of the form:

$$
\mathcal{L} = \lambda_1 \mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}} + \lambda_2 \mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}} + \lambda_3 \mathcal{L}_{\text{data}}
$$

where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}}$ represents the residual of the governing equations, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}}$ enforces boundary conditions, and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{data}}$ ensures data consistency. Adaptive weighting strategies, such as those described in [1], are also employed to dynamically balance the contributions of different loss components, enhancing the robustness of the solution. In contrast, some studies, such as [4], provide only general descriptions of the loss function without explicit formulations, highlighting a gap in the literature regarding the systematic design of loss functions for sparse data scenarios.

The integration of PDE constraints into the loss function is a defining feature of PINNs, typically achieved through automatic differentiation, which allows the computation of derivatives of the neural network outputs with respect to input variables. This enables the enforcement of PDE residuals within the loss, ensuring that the network solutions remain physically consistent. While most studies, such as [2], explicitly incorporate PDE residuals, others, like [3], focus more on boundary and initial conditions, with less emphasis on the detailed formulation of PDE constraints.

Based on these findings, a classification of PINN methodologies can be proposed. The first category includes standard PINNs that employ FCNs and use weighted loss functions with PDE residuals, boundary, and data terms. The second category encompasses specialized architectures, such as those designed for turbulent flows or complex boundary conditions, which may incorporate additional mechanisms, such as boundary-constrained networks or hybrid approaches. Finally, a third category may consist of studies that emphasize the integration of physical models, such as RANS turbulence models, into the PINN framework, thereby enhancing the accuracy of the solutions in specific fluid dynamics applications.

This methodological landscape reveals a growing sophistication in the design of PINNs for fluid mechanics, with increasing attention to both network architecture and loss function formulation. However, challenges remain, including the need for more systematic comparisons of different network designs, the development of more robust adaptive weighting strategies, and the explicit formulation of loss functions for sparse or noisy data scenarios. Future research should aim to address these gaps, further advancing the applicability and reliability of PINNs in fluid dynamics.

The design of loss functions in physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) plays a critical role in ensuring that the network solutions satisfy both the governing partial differential equations (PDEs) and the associated boundary and initial conditions. A comparative analysis of loss function strategies across several studies reveals both common practices and unique innovations, particularly in the areas of adaptive weighting, hybrid loss formulations, and the incorporation of PDE residuals.

$$
\mathcal{L} = \alpha \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{data}} + \beta \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}}
$$

Here, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{data}}$ measures the discrepancy between the neural network’s predictions and observed data, while $\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}}$ enforces the satisfaction of the governing equations through PDE residuals. The weights $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are adjusted during training to balance the relative importance of data fidelity and physical consistency. Similarly, [1] introduces self-adaptive weighting, which dynamically adjusts the contributions of different physical constraints during training. This innovation helps maintain numerical stability and improves the convergence behavior of the network, particularly for high-dimensional and turbulent flow problems.

The incorporation of PDE residuals into the loss function is a common practice across most studies. These residuals are computed based on the neural network’s predictions and are used to penalize deviations from the governing equations. This is explicitly described in [5], where the PDE residuals are calculated using the network’s output, and additional terms are added to enforce boundary conditions. However, not all studies provide detailed formulations of the loss function. For example, [4] outlines the general approach but does not present specific loss formulations or adaptive strategies, highlighting a gap in the literature regarding the explicit design of loss functions for sparse data scenarios.

In terms of convergence and accuracy, the choice of loss function significantly impacts the performance of PINNs. Studies that incorporate adaptive weighting and hybrid loss functions tend to achieve better convergence and more accurate solutions, particularly in complex fluid mechanics problems. For instance, the self-adaptive weighting strategy described in [1] helps maintain the physical consistency of the solution over time, which is crucial for capturing the temporal evolution of turbulent flows. On the other hand, fixed weighting strategies may lead to suboptimal solutions if the relative importance of different constraints is not properly balanced.

Based on the analysis of these studies, several best practices can be proposed for designing robust loss functions in fluid mechanics applications. First, the use of adaptive weighting strategies is strongly recommended to balance the contributions of data loss, PDE residual loss, and boundary condition terms. Second, hybrid loss functions that combine multiple physical constraints and data sources can improve the accuracy and generalizability of the solutions. Third, the incorporation of PDE residuals and boundary conditions into the loss function is essential to ensure that the network solutions remain physically meaningful. Finally, for problems with complex geometries or boundary conditions, the use of boundary-constrained mechanisms or collocation points can enhance the accuracy of the solution.

In conclusion, while there is no one-size-fits-all approach to loss function design in PINNs, the integration of adaptive weighting, hybrid formulations, and explicit enforcement of PDE constraints has proven to be effective in improving the convergence and accuracy of solutions in fluid mechanics applications.

Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) have shown significant promise in fluid mechanics, yet their application is hindered by a range of challenges that affect their reliability, efficiency, and generalizability. In summary, while PINNs demonstrate strong potential in fluid mechanics applications, their performance is highly sensitive to data quality and quantity. ## 6. Section 6: Future Directions and Research Opportunities
The future of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) in fluid mechanics is increasingly oriented toward the development of more interpretable, scalable, and hybrid modeling frameworks. A recurring theme across multiple studies is the need to integrate PINNs with traditional physics-based models, such as computational fluid dynamics (CFD) solvers, turbulence models, and boundary condition handling techniques, to enhance both accuracy and physical consistency [1,2,3]. These hybrid approaches aim to leverage the flexibility of data-driven models while maintaining the rigor of physics-based formulations, particularly in complex flow scenarios where high-fidelity turbulence modeling is essential.

In parallel, multi-fidelity and transfer learning approaches are gaining traction as effective strategies to improve the generalization and efficiency of PINNs. Multi-fidelity learning enables the integration of data from different fidelity levels, such as low-fidelity RANS simulations and high-fidelity DNS data, to reduce computational costs while maintaining predictive accuracy [2,5]. Transfer learning, on the other hand, allows pre-trained PINNs to be adapted to new flow configurations with minimal additional training, thereby expanding their applicability across diverse fluid dynamics problems [1,2].

Despite these promising directions, several challenges remain. The integration of PINNs with physics-based models often involves complex data exchange and training strategy design, which are not yet fully standardized [2]. Similarly, the effectiveness of multi-fidelity and transfer learning techniques depends on the careful selection of data sources and the development of robust weighting schemes, which are still under exploration [3]. Furthermore, while some studies have highlighted the potential of real-time applications and transient flow simulations, concrete implementations and theoretical analyses of these aspects are limited [2].

To address these challenges, a roadmap for future research should prioritize the development of standardized hybrid modeling frameworks, the refinement of multi-fidelity and transfer learning algorithms, and the exploration of real-time and transient flow applications. Additionally, efforts should be directed toward improving the interpretability of PINNs, ensuring their scalability for large-scale fluid dynamics problems, and validating their performance on real-world industrial and academic applications. By systematically addressing these issues, the next generation of PINNs can achieve greater accuracy, efficiency, and practical utility in fluid mechanics.

### 6.2 Subsection 6.2: Multi-Fidelity and Transfer Learning Approaches
Multi-fidelity and transfer learning approaches have emerged as promising strategies to enhance the generalization and efficiency of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) in fluid mechanics. These techniques address the challenges of data scarcity, high computational costs, and the need for robust models that can adapt to diverse flow conditions. By integrating data from different fidelity levels or leveraging knowledge from related tasks, these methods offer a pathway to improve model performance without relying solely on high-quality experimental data.

Multi-fidelity learning, as discussed in several studies, enables the integration of both high-fidelity and low-fidelity data to enhance the accuracy of PINNs while reducing computational overhead [1,2]. This approach is particularly beneficial in fluid mechanics, where high-fidelity data, such as direct numerical simulation (DNS) results, can be computationally expensive to generate. By incorporating low-fidelity data, such as Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) simulations or simplified models, multi-fidelity learning allows the network to learn more efficiently and generalize better across different scenarios. For instance, the work in [5] demonstrates that multi-fidelity learning can significantly reduce the dependency on high-quality experimental data, thereby expanding the applicability of PINNs in practical settings.

Transfer learning, on the other hand, focuses on adapting a pre-trained PINN to solve related but distinct fluid dynamics problems with minimal additional training. This technique is particularly valuable in scenarios where data for a specific problem is limited or unavailable. The study in [2] highlights that a PINN trained on one flow configuration can be effectively fine-tuned to handle similar problems, such as different boundary conditions or flow geometries. This not only accelerates convergence but also improves the model's ability to generalize across a broader range of applications. The potential of transfer learning is further emphasized in [1], where it is suggested that such approaches can significantly enhance the robustness of PINNs in complex, three-dimensional turbulent flow simulations.

While both multi-fidelity and transfer learning approaches offer distinct advantages, they also present unique challenges. Multi-fidelity learning requires careful handling of data discrepancies and the development of robust weighting schemes to balance the contributions of different fidelity levels. Transfer learning, on the other hand, depends on the similarity between source and target tasks, and its effectiveness can be limited if the underlying physics or boundary conditions differ significantly. The paper in [3] notes that the application of these techniques in fluid mechanics is still in its early stages, with limited exploration of specific implementation strategies.

Despite these challenges, the integration of multi-fidelity and transfer learning into PINNs represents a significant step toward more efficient and generalizable models for fluid mechanics. Future research should focus on developing adaptive algorithms that can dynamically adjust to varying data fidelity levels and improve the transferability of models across different physical regimes. Additionally, the exploration of hybrid architectures that combine multi-fidelity and transfer learning could lead to more resilient and versatile PINNs, capable of handling complex, real-world fluid dynamics problems with greater accuracy and efficiency.

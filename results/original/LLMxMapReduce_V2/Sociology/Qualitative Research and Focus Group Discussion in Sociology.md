## 1. Section 1: Introduction to Qualitative Research in Sociology
Qualitative research in sociology is fundamentally defined as a methodological approach that seeks to understand social phenomena through in-depth, interpretive analysis of human behavior, interactions, and meanings. This approach prioritizes the exploration of the "why" and "how" behind social actions, as opposed to the quantitative emphasis on measurable causal relationships [4,7]. Both "qualitative_research_methods_sociology" and "qualitative_research_in_sociology" highlight the interpretivist perspective, which posits that social reality is constructed through human interactions and meanings, rather than being an objective, external entity as suggested by positivist paradigms [4,7].

While "qualitative_research_methods_sociology" emphasizes the interpretivist approach as the core philosophical foundation of qualitative research, "qualitative_research_in_sociology" offers a broader methodological overview, situating qualitative research within a diverse range of techniques, including interviews, observations, and focus group discussions. This distinction reflects a tension between methodological specificity and conceptual breadth, with the former offering a more focused lens on interpretivist principles and the latter providing a more comprehensive framework for understanding the various tools available within qualitative research [4,7].

Focus group discussions occupy a central position within this framework, serving as a key data collection technique that aligns with interpretivist principles. They enable researchers to gather rich, detailed data through interactive dialogue, allowing for the exploration of social dynamics, the co-construction of meaning, and the articulation of participants' lived experiences [2,4]. This method is particularly effective in capturing the complexities of social interactions and cultural practices, especially in sensitive or underrepresented contexts, as noted in "qualitative_research_methods_sociology" [4].

The use of non-numerical data in qualitative research has significant philosophical implications, as it challenges the dominance of positivist epistemologies in social science. By privileging subjective experiences and meanings, qualitative research asserts the importance of context, interpretation, and reflexivity in understanding social phenomena. This approach is further reinforced by the emphasis on interpretivist perspectives across multiple papers, including "qualitative_research_and_focus_group_discussion_in_sociology" and "focus_group_methodology_some_ethical_challenges" [1,2]. These studies collectively underscore the value of qualitative methods in illuminating the nuances of human behavior that quantitative approaches may overlook.

Despite the growing recognition of qualitative research in sociology, some scholars caution against its uncritical adoption, particularly in the case of focus group discussions. For instance, "the_focus_groups_in_social_research_advantages_and_disadvantages" highlights the need for a critical evaluation of the appropriateness of focus groups as a research method, noting that they are often used without sufficient consideration of their suitability for specific research goals [5]. This critique underscores the importance of methodological rigor and theoretical alignment in the application of qualitative techniques, including focus group discussions.

## 3. Section 3: Theoretical and Epistemological Foundations
The theoretical and epistemological foundations of qualitative research in sociology are deeply rooted in interpretivist and constructivist paradigms, which emphasize the socially constructed and context-dependent nature of reality. These perspectives are central to understanding the role of focus group discussions (FGDs) as a methodological tool within qualitative sociology. According to [4], interpretivism serves as the cornerstone of qualitative research, advocating for an approach that prioritizes the subjective meanings individuals and groups ascribe to their experiences. This perspective underscores the importance of understanding social phenomena through the lens of participants' lived realities, rather than reducing them to quantifiable variables. In contrast, [7] provides a broader methodological context, highlighting both interpretivist and constructivist views. Constructivism, in particular, posits that social reality is not merely discovered but actively constructed through interaction, emphasizing the collaborative and dynamic nature of knowledge production. This aligns with the inherent characteristics of FGDs, where participants collectively negotiate and co-construct meanings through dialogue. The interplay between interpretivist and constructivist perspectives shapes the design, implementation, and interpretation of FGDs, influencing the researcher’s role as both an interpretive participant and a facilitator of meaning-making. While interpretivist approaches tend to focus on the individual’s internalized meanings, constructivist frameworks emphasize the group’s collective construction of knowledge. These theoretical foundations have significant implications for the validity and reliability of FGDs, as the researcher’s influence, whether through engagement or facilitation, can shape the direction and content of the discussion. The literature consistently underscores the need for researchers to balance active involvement with methodological reflexivity, ensuring that the interpretive process remains transparent and grounded in the participants’ perspectives. Despite the theoretical richness of these paradigms, some studies, such as [5], note that the theoretical underpinnings of FGDs are not always clearly articulated, which can limit their methodological rigor. Thus, the integration of interpretivist and constructivist perspectives provides a robust epistemological framework for qualitative sociology, particularly in the context of FGDs, while also highlighting the need for greater theoretical clarity and methodological transparency in future research.
### 3.1 Subsection 3.1: Interpretivist and Constructivist Perspectives
Interpretivist and constructivist perspectives are central to qualitative research, particularly in the context of focus group discussions (FGDs). Both frameworks emphasize the importance of meaning-making in understanding social phenomena, but they differ in their conceptualization of the researcher’s role and the nature of social reality. According to [7], interpretivism foregrounds the subjective nature of social reality, advocating for an approach that seeks to understand the meanings individuals and groups assign to their experiences. This perspective positions the researcher as a reflective participant who interprets the lived experiences of participants, aiming to uncover the deeper, often unspoken, meanings embedded in social interactions. In contrast, constructivism, as outlined in the same paper, highlights how social realities are collectively constructed through interaction, suggesting that knowledge is not merely discovered but actively shaped through dialogue and shared experiences. This distinction has significant implications for the design and interpretation of FGDs. While interpretivist approaches may prioritize the individual's interpretation of social contexts, constructivist approaches emphasize the co-creation of knowledge within the group setting, where the researcher acts more as a facilitator of meaning-making rather than an external observer. The paper [4] further supports this, noting that interpretivist foundations underpin the qualitative research tradition, which seeks to understand phenomena in their natural settings, rather than reducing them to quantifiable variables. Although constructivist views are mentioned, they are not elaborated in detail in this source. However, [1] explicitly identifies both interpretivist and constructivist theories as central to qualitative research, emphasizing that social phenomena are constructed through human interaction and meaning-making. This aligns with the argument in [5] that FGDs are inherently aligned with these perspectives, as they rely on the subjective meanings and social interactions of participants. The heuristic value of FGDs, as argued in that paper, is rooted in their ability to reveal how participants collectively construct and negotiate meanings, which is a core tenet of constructivism. In contrast, interpretivist approaches may place greater emphasis on the individual’s internalized meanings, even within a group setting. Thus, the choice between interpretivist and constructivist frameworks influences not only how researchers interpret the data but also how they design the FGDs themselves, including the selection of participants, the formulation of discussion questions, and the analysis of emergent themes. While both perspectives recognize the importance of meaning-making, the interpretivist approach tends to focus on the researcher’s role in interpreting these meanings, whereas the constructivist approach emphasizes the collaborative and dynamic nature of knowledge production within the group. This distinction underscores the theoretical underpinnings that shape the methodological choices in qualitative sociology, particularly in the use of focus group discussions as a research tool.

The role of the researcher in qualitative studies, particularly within the context of focus group discussions, is a central concern in the literature, with varying degrees of emphasis on the extent of their influence. Two key papers, [4,7], provide foundational insights into this issue. Both acknowledge that the researcher’s presence and interactions with participants significantly shape the data collection and interpretation processes. However, they differ in the degree to which they emphasize the researcher’s active involvement and the implications of this involvement for the research outcomes.

In [4], the researcher is portrayed as an emotionally engaged and involved participant in the research process. The paper critiques the positivist notion of detachment, arguing that emotional closeness and relational engagement can yield deeper insights into sensitive or marginalized aspects of social life. This perspective positions the researcher not as an impartial observer but as an integral actor whose involvement can enhance the richness and depth of the data. Such an approach challenges traditional notions of objectivity in research, suggesting that the researcher’s subjectivity can be a source of valuable insight rather than a threat to validity.

In contrast, [7] emphasizes the researcher’s reflexive and active role, particularly in focus group settings. It argues that the researcher’s facilitation, including their questions, interventions, and nonverbal cues, can directly influence the direction and content of the discussion. The paper stresses the importance of researchers being aware of their own biases and the potential impact of their actions on the data. This perspective aligns with a more methodologically conscious approach, where the researcher’s influence is acknowledged but managed through deliberate reflection and methodological awareness.

These two perspectives highlight a critical tension in qualitative research: the balance between the researcher’s active engagement and the need for methodological rigor. While [4] advocates for a more participatory and emotionally involved role, [7] emphasizes the necessity of reflexivity and methodological control. Both, however, agree that the researcher’s role cannot be entirely separated from the research process, and that this influence has significant implications for the validity and reliability of focus group data.

The implications of the researcher’s role for the validity and reliability of focus group data are multifaceted. On one hand, the researcher’s active involvement can enhance the depth and richness of the data, as seen in the emphasis on emotional engagement and relational dynamics. On the other hand, this same involvement can introduce biases and subjectivity, which may compromise the reliability of the findings. The challenge, therefore, lies in acknowledging the researcher’s influence while implementing strategies to mitigate its potential negative effects. This includes practices such as reflexive journaling, peer debriefing, and triangulation, all of which are recommended in the literature to strengthen the credibility of qualitative research.

Other papers, such as [1,3,5], provide additional insights into the practical aspects of the researcher’s role. While they do not offer a comprehensive theoretical analysis, they underscore the importance of the moderator’s ability to balance structure with adaptability and manage group dynamics effectively. These practical considerations further highlight the complexity of the researcher’s role and the need for careful methodological planning and execution.

In summary, the researcher’s role in qualitative studies, particularly in focus group discussions, is both influential and complex. The literature suggests that while the researcher’s engagement can enhance the depth of data, it also necessitates a heightened awareness of potential biases and methodological limitations. The balance between active involvement and methodological rigor remains a central challenge in ensuring the validity and reliability of focus group data.

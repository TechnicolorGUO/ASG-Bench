# 0. Organoids as Host Models for Infection Biology

## 1. Section 1: Introduction to Organoids as Host Models
Organoids represent a groundbreaking advancement in biomedical research, offering a sophisticated in vitro platform that closely mirrors the structural and functional complexity of native human tissues. These three-dimensional (3D) cellular structures, derived from pluripotent or adult stem cells, exhibit self-organization, cellular heterogeneity, tissue polarity, and functional specialization, making them highly relevant for studying biological processes, including host-pathogen interactions [1,3]. Unlike traditional two-dimensional (2D) cell cultures, which often fail to recapitulate the in vivo microenvironment, organoids provide a more physiologically accurate context for investigating infection dynamics, particularly in the context of viral pathogens such as SARS-CoV-2 [3].

The evolution of organoid technology has been marked by significant progress, transitioning from stem cell-derived models to more complex systems that incorporate primary human tissues and immune components. This development has enhanced the physiological relevance of organoids, enabling more accurate modeling of human-specific pathogen interactions [2,3]. The shift from 2D to 3D culture systems has been particularly transformative, as it allows for the preservation of the intricate cellular architecture and functional properties of native tissues, which is critical for studying infections that depend on tissue-specific interactions, such as those involving the respiratory epithelium or intestinal mucosa [1].

Despite their promise, organoids are not without limitations. While they can mimic many aspects of native tissues, they often lack key physiological features such as vascular networks and fully integrated immune systems, which are essential for a comprehensive understanding of host-pathogen dynamics [1]. Additionally, variability in organoid formation, differences in differentiation protocols, and the limited scalability of organoid systems can affect reproducibility and experimental consistency. These challenges highlight the need for more standardized methodologies, deeper molecular and functional characterization, and systematic comparisons of different organoid derivation strategies to enhance their reliability and utility in infection biology [2].

The integration of organoid technology with advanced imaging, transcriptomics, and high-throughput screening methods has significantly enhanced the resolution and depth of infection studies, enabling researchers to dissect viral tropism, identify host factors involved in infection, and evaluate the efficacy of antiviral compounds in a more biologically relevant setting [3]. Ongoing innovations, such as the incorporation of immune cells and the use of gene editing techniques like CRISPR, are further expanding the applicability of organoids as host models for infection research. However, the field remains in an early stage, with many studies focusing on general overviews rather than in-depth methodological comparisons or mechanistic insights, underscoring the need for more rigorous and systematic investigations to fully realize the potential of organoids in infection biology [2].

### 1.3 Subsection 1.1: Definition and Characteristics of Organoids
Organoids are three-dimensional (3D) cellular structures that self-organize to replicate the architectural and functional complexity of native organs, offering a powerful in vitro platform for investigating biological processes, including infection dynamics [1]. These models are typically derived from either pluripotent stem cells (PSCs) or adult stem cells, and they exhibit long-term viability, cellular heterogeneity, tissue polarity, and functional specialization, which are essential for modeling complex biological systems [3]. The capacity of organoids to self-renew and differentiate into multiple cell types further enhances their applicability in studying host-pathogen interactions and disease mechanisms.

In the context of infection biology, organoids have proven particularly useful for examining viral pathogenesis, especially for respiratory viruses such as SARS-CoV-2. Human airway and alveolar organoids have been developed to mimic the upper and lower respiratory tracts, respectively, and they express critical viral entry receptors, such as ACE2 and TMPRSS2, which are necessary for SARS-CoV-2 infection [3]. Compared to traditional two-dimensional (2D) cell cultures, organoids provide a more physiologically relevant environment for studying viral replication, host immune responses, and the efficacy of antiviral interventions in a tissue-specific manner.

The versatility of organoid technology is further highlighted by its applicability across multiple organ systems, including the lung, gut, and liver, enabling the investigation of infections in diverse biological contexts [3]. The use of extracellular matrix components, such as Matrigel, as a growth substrate allows for the maintenance of organoid structure and function over extended periods, supporting long-term infection studies [3].

Despite these advancements, challenges remain in achieving a complete recapitulation of in vivo conditions. For example, many organoid systems lack immune cells and vascular components, which limits their ability to fully model host-pathogen interactions [2]. Additionally, variability between organoid lines and the absence of standardized protocols are frequently cited as obstacles to reproducibility and broader application in infection biology. These issues are attributed to differences in cell sources, culture conditions, and genetic backgrounds, which can result in inconsistent experimental outcomes [3].

While the literature provides a foundational understanding of organoids as host models, there is a clear need for more standardized methodologies, deeper molecular and functional characterization, and systematic comparisons of different organoid derivation strategies. Such improvements would enhance the reliability and utility of organoids in studying a wide range of infectious diseases, ultimately advancing the field of infection biology [1,2].

### 1.4 Subsection 1.2: Evolution of Organoid Technology
The evolution of organoid technology represents a transformative shift in the field of infection biology, driven by the development of increasingly sophisticated three-dimensional (3D) models that more accurately reflect the complexity of human tissues. Initially, organoids were derived primarily from stem cells, enabling the generation of self-organizing structures that recapitulate key features of the organ of origin. However, recent advances have enabled the direct derivation of organoids from primary human tissues, significantly enhancing their physiological relevance and capacity to model human-specific pathogen interactions [2,3]. This transition has broadened the applicability of organoids in infection research, particularly in the study of viral pathogens such as coronaviruses, where the use of human-specific models is essential for understanding host-pathogen interactions.

A pivotal development in this field has been the shift from traditional two-dimensional (2D) cell cultures to 3D organoids, which provide a more accurate representation of in vivo conditions by preserving the structural and functional complexity of native tissues. This advancement has been particularly valuable in studying respiratory tract infections, where the intricate architecture of the airway epithelium plays a critical role in viral entry, replication, and immune responses [3]. The integration of immune cells into organoid systems further enhances their utility, allowing for the investigation of host immune responses to pathogens in a more physiologically relevant context.

Key milestones in the development of organoid models for infection studies include the refinement of 3D culture techniques, the optimization of defined culture media, and the establishment of co-culture systems that incorporate multiple cell types. These innovations have enabled the generation of organoids from a wide range of human organs and tissues, including the intestine, liver, and respiratory tract, each of which presents unique challenges and opportunities for infection research [1,2]. The ability to generate organoids from both stem cells and primary tissues has also facilitated the study of both developmental and pathological processes, making organoids a versatile platform for infection biology.

Despite these advancements, challenges remain in the standardization and scalability of organoid models. Variability in organoid formation, differences in cellular composition, and the limited lifespan of organoids in culture can affect experimental reproducibility. However, ongoing research aims to address these limitations through the development of more robust and reproducible protocols, as well as the integration of advanced imaging and omics technologies to enhance the resolution and depth of infection studies. The incorporation of high-throughput screening, gene editing, and advanced imaging techniques has further expanded the utility of organoids in infection research, particularly in the study of respiratory tract infections caused by viruses like SARS-CoV-2 [3]. While the use of CRISPR-based gene editing has enabled the generation of genetically modified organoids to study viral susceptibility, the lack of universally accepted protocols remains a barrier to reproducibility across different laboratories.

The field is currently characterized by a rapid pace of innovation, with a growing emphasis on the development of more scalable and reproducible methods. The integration of multi-omics approaches and machine learning algorithms is increasingly being proposed as a means to address these limitations, offering a more systematic and data-driven approach to organoid-based infection studies [3]. These emerging trends highlight the ongoing evolution of organoid technology and its potential to significantly impact the study of host-pathogen interactions in the future.

## 2. Section 2: Organoids in Infection Biology: General Applications
Organoids have emerged as a transformative platform in infection biology, offering a physiologically relevant in vitro system that closely mirrors the complexity of human tissues. These three-dimensional (3D) structures, derived from stem cells, enable the investigation of viral, bacterial, and parasitic infections by recapitulating the cellular heterogeneity and functional architecture of native tissues. Their ability to maintain tissue-specific cellular diversity makes them particularly valuable for studying pathogen-host interactions, as demonstrated in the context of respiratory viruses such as SARS-CoV-2, where organoids have been used to model viral entry, replication, and immune responses [3]. This capacity to mimic human tissue environments has also extended to other pathogens, including *Salmonella* in intestinal organoids and respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) in lung organoids [1].

Beyond their utility in pathogenesis studies, organoids have been increasingly applied in drug screening, vaccine development, and regenerative medicine. Their human-specific nature allows for the evaluation of antiviral compounds and immune modulators in a context that more accurately reflects human physiology than traditional two-dimensional cell cultures or animal models. This is especially critical for diseases where inter-species differences in immune responses significantly impact disease progression and treatment outcomes [2]. However, despite these advantages, the application of organoids in infection biology remains in its early stages, with several challenges that need to be addressed to fully realize their potential.

One of the key limitations is the incomplete representation of the human immune system within current organoid models. While organoids can recapitulate the structural and functional properties of native tissues, they often lack immune cell components such as alveolar macrophages, T cells, and B cells, which are essential for modeling the full spectrum of immune responses to pathogens [3]. This gap restricts the ability of organoids to capture the dynamic interactions between pathogens and the immune system, particularly in complex pathologies such as cytokine storms observed in severe viral infections. Furthermore, the variability between organoid lines and the lack of standardized protocols introduce challenges in reproducibility and comparability across studies, limiting the integration of findings from different experimental settings [1].

To address these limitations, future research should focus on integrating functional immune cells into organoid systems, leveraging advances in co-culture technologies, single-cell omics, and CRISPR-based screening. Such developments are expected to enhance the physiological fidelity and experimental utility of organoids, enabling more accurate modeling of infection dynamics and immune responses. Additionally, the integration of organoids with other experimental models, such as animal models and clinical data, is seen as a critical step toward improving their predictive power and translational relevance [3]. By overcoming these challenges, organoids can be refined into more reliable and comprehensive models for understanding the molecular basis of infectious diseases and for developing targeted therapeutic strategies.

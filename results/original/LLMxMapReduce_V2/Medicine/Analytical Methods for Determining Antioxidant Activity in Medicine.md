## 1. Section 1: Introduction and Background
Antioxidant activity plays a pivotal role in mitigating oxidative stress, a condition characterized by an imbalance between the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and the body’s ability to counteract their harmful effects through neutralization by antioxidants. This concept is central to understanding the pathophysiology of numerous diseases, including cancer, neurodegenerative disorders, cardiovascular diseases, and inflammatory conditions such as inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) [3]. The background sections of multiple studies emphasize that oxidative stress arises from both endogenous and exogenous sources, such as environmental pollution, metabolic byproducts, and dietary factors, which contribute to cellular damage and disease progression [2].

The definition and contextualization of antioxidant activity vary across studies, but a common thread is the recognition of antioxidants as molecules that inhibit or delay the oxidation of other molecules. These molecules may include both enzymatic (e.g., superoxide dismutase, catalase) and non-enzymatic (e.g., flavonoids, phenolics) agents, which act through diverse mechanisms such as free radical scavenging, metal chelation, and peroxide decomposition [1]. For instance, the study on *Quercus brantii* (Iranian oak) highlights the significance of plant-based antioxidants, particularly phenolic compounds, in combating oxidative stress and promoting health benefits [5]. Similarly, research on ethanol extracts of *Muntingia calabura* L. leaves underscores the importance of developing reliable and standardized analytical methods to assess antioxidant activity, especially in plant-based sources that are rich in bioactive compounds [2].

The physiological role of oxidants and the necessity of maintaining redox homeostasis are also frequently addressed in the literature. Oxidative stress (OS) is identified as a key contributor to disease pathology, and the accurate assessment of total antioxidant capacity (TAC) is deemed essential for understanding and managing OS. This is particularly relevant in clinical settings, where antioxidants are explored for their potential therapeutic applications in modulating redox balance and reducing oxidative damage [4]. The need for standardized and reproducible analytical methods is a recurring theme, as inconsistencies in methodology can lead to variability in results and hinder the comparative evaluation of antioxidant efficacy across different studies.

Despite the growing body of research, gaps remain in the current understanding of antioxidant activity, particularly in terms of methodological standardization and the contextual application of antioxidant assessments in diverse biological systems. While some studies focus on the biochemical and clinical implications of antioxidants, others emphasize the importance of phytochemical diversity in natural sources, such as wild populations of *Quercus brantii*, which may offer novel compounds with potent antioxidant properties [5]. These findings highlight the need for a more integrated approach that combines biochemical, pharmacological, and ecological perspectives to fully characterize antioxidant activity in medicinal contexts.

While DPPH is favored for its simplicity, ABTS for its versatility, and FRAP for its sensitivity to reducing power, no single method provides a complete picture of antioxidant activity. ### 2.1 Subsection 2.1: DPPH Assay
The DPPH (2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl) assay is a widely utilized method for assessing the free radical scavenging activity of antioxidants. It operates on the principle that antioxidants reduce the stable DPPH radical, which is characterized by a deep purple color, to a colorless diphenylpicrylhydrazine. This reduction is typically measured spectrophotometrically at 517 nm, allowing for the quantification of antioxidant capacity. According to the digest from [1], the DPPH assay is particularly sensitive to phenolic compounds and is frequently employed in the evaluation of plant extracts, such as ethanol extracts of Muntingia calabura L. leaves, as demonstrated in the study cited in [2].

One of the primary advantages of the DPPH assay is its simplicity and cost-effectiveness, making it a popular choice for preliminary screening of antioxidant activity. The method requires minimal equipment and reagents, and the procedure is relatively straightforward, allowing for high-throughput analysis. Additionally, the DPPH assay provides a direct measure of radical scavenging ability, which is a key aspect of antioxidant function. However, this method also has notable limitations. For instance, the DPPH assay may not fully represent the antioxidant activity of compounds in complex biological systems, as it primarily reflects the ability of antioxidants to neutralize a single type of radical. This can lead to an overestimation or underestimation of the true antioxidant potential in real-world applications, as highlighted in [1].

Furthermore, the DPPH assay is susceptible to interference from non-antioxidant compounds that may also interact with the DPPH radical, such as certain pigments or reducing agents. This can result in false-positive readings and limit the specificity of the assay. In comparison to other methods, such as the ABTS (2,2'-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid)) and FRAP (ferric reducing antioxidant power) assays, the DPPH assay is less effective in capturing the full spectrum of antioxidant mechanisms, particularly those involving metal chelation or enzyme inhibition. While the ABTS assay is more sensitive to a broader range of antioxidants and better mimics physiological conditions, and the FRAP assay is more suitable for measuring reducing power, the DPPH assay remains a valuable tool for initial evaluations due to its simplicity and reproducibility.

In terms of effectiveness across different sample matrices, the DPPH assay has been successfully applied to a variety of plant-based extracts, including those from fruits, vegetables, and medicinal herbs. However, its performance may vary depending on the nature of the sample, with more complex matrices potentially introducing greater variability in results. The study using ethanol extracts of Muntingia calabura L. leaves reported IC50 values as a measure of antioxidant efficiency, demonstrating the utility of the DPPH assay in quantifying the scavenging capacity of bioactive compounds. Despite its limitations, the DPPH assay continues to be an essential method in the analytical toolkit for antioxidant research, particularly when used in conjunction with other assays to provide a more comprehensive assessment of antioxidant activity.
### 2.2 Subsection 2.2: ABTS Assay
The ABTS (2,2'-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid)) assay is a widely utilized method for assessing antioxidant activity, distinguished by its ability to detect both hydrophilic and lipophilic antioxidants. This versatility sets it apart from the DPPH (2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl) assay, which is primarily effective for hydrophilic compounds, and the FRAP (ferric reducing antioxidant power) assay, which is more sensitive to reducing agents and is often used to evaluate the total antioxidant capacity of samples. According to the study on ethanol extracts of *Muntingia calabura* L. leaves, the ABTS assay was employed alongside DPPH and FRAP to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the antioxidant properties of the extract [2].

In terms of sensitivity, the ABTS assay exhibits a broader range of applicability due to its ability to interact with a wider spectrum of antioxidant molecules. While DPPH is a stable radical that is commonly used for its simplicity, it may not fully reflect the antioxidant potential of complex mixtures, particularly those containing lipophilic compounds. The FRAP assay, on the other hand, measures the reducing capacity of antioxidants, which is valuable in certain contexts but may not capture the full range of antioxidant mechanisms. The ABTS assay, by contrast, is capable of detecting both hydrogen-donating and metal-chelating antioxidants, making it more suitable for analyzing plant extracts and medicinal compounds that contain a diverse array of bioactive components [1].

The practicality of the ABTS assay in different research settings is another key consideration. In clinical studies, the ABTS assay is often preferred for its high sensitivity and ability to measure antioxidant activity in biological fluids, such as serum and plasma, where both hydrophilic and lipophilic antioxidants are present. In environmental studies, the assay is useful for evaluating the antioxidant capacity of water-soluble and lipid-soluble pollutants, as well as for assessing the efficacy of natural antioxidants in mitigating oxidative stress in ecosystems. The procedure involves the generation of ABTS radicals, which are then measured for decolorization, a process that is both reproducible and amenable to automation, further enhancing its utility in large-scale studies [1].

Overall, the ABTS assay offers a balanced combination of sensitivity, versatility, and practicality, making it a valuable tool in the analysis of antioxidant activity across various research domains. Its ability to detect a broad range of antioxidants, in comparison to DPPH and FRAP, positions it as a preferred method in many analytical workflows, particularly when dealing with complex biological or environmental samples.
### 2.3 Subsection 2.3: FRAP Assay
The Ferric Reducing Antioxidant Power (FRAP) assay is a widely utilized method for evaluating the reducing power of antioxidants, which is a critical parameter in assessing their biological activity. The FRAP assay operates on the principle of measuring the ability of antioxidants to reduce Fe³+ to Fe²+ in a controlled redox environment, with the resulting ferrous ions forming a colored complex that can be quantified spectrophotometrically. This method is particularly effective in detecting compounds with strong reducing capacities, such as flavonoids and other polyphenolic substances, as demonstrated in the study on ethanol extracts of *Muntingia calabura* L. leaves, where the FRAP assay exhibited the highest sensitivity, yielding IC50 values below 50 μg/mL [2].

When compared to the DPPH (2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl) and ABTS (2,2'-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid)) assays, the FRAP assay demonstrates distinct advantages and limitations. While DPPH and ABTS are primarily used to assess radical-scavenging activity, FRAP focuses on the reducing power, which is a different but complementary aspect of antioxidant function. The FRAP assay is particularly sensitive to compounds that can donate electrons and reduce metal ions, making it highly relevant for evaluating the antioxidant potential of natural extracts rich in flavonoids and phenolic acids. In contrast, DPPH and ABTS are more effective in detecting antioxidants that act through hydrogen atom transfer (HAT) mechanisms, which may not always correlate with reducing power.

The study on *Muntingia calabura* L. leaves highlights the FRAP assay's ability to provide reliable and reproducible results in the context of plant-derived antioxidants, suggesting its utility in food science applications where the antioxidant capacity of natural products is of interest. Additionally, the FRAP method is often employed in pharmaceutical research to evaluate the efficacy of synthetic and natural compounds in mitigating oxidative stress, as noted in the general overview of analytical methods for determining antioxidant activity in medicine [1]. However, the FRAP assay has limitations in detecting certain types of antioxidants, particularly those that do not exhibit significant reducing properties. For instance, antioxidants that primarily function through metal chelation or peroxyl radical scavenging may not be adequately represented by the FRAP assay, necessitating the use of complementary methods such as DPPH or ABTS for a more comprehensive assessment.

In conclusion, the FRAP assay remains a valuable tool in the evaluation of antioxidant activity, particularly in contexts where reducing power is a key factor. Its high sensitivity and ease of implementation make it a popular choice in both food science and pharmaceutical research. However, its limitations in detecting certain antioxidant mechanisms underscore the importance of integrating it with other assays to obtain a more holistic understanding of a compound's antioxidant profile.

For instance, the review by [4] underscores the high cost and complexity of many antioxidant assays, which often require specialized equipment and trained personnel. This limits their applicability in resource-limited environments or in settings where rapid, on-site analysis is required. Similarly, [1] points out that some methods are time-consuming and not suitable for high-throughput screening, which is a critical requirement in clinical diagnostics and pharmaceutical research.

The need for more user-friendly and cost-effective techniques has been consistently emphasized. These suggestions are in line with the broader trend in medical diagnostics toward portable and accessible technologies that can be deployed in a variety of clinical and field settings.

However, the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and practicality remain a significant concern. While methods like DPPH, ABTS, and FRAP are widely used for their simplicity and reproducibility, they may not always correlate well with in vivo antioxidant activity, raising questions about their clinical relevance. Moreover, while some studies focus on the biochemical and pharmacological aspects of antioxidant activity, they often overlook the practical challenges of translating these findings into clinical practice. In summary, while antioxidant assays play a vital role in assessing the efficacy of medicinal compounds, their integration into routine clinical practice is hindered by factors such as cost, complexity, and scalability. Addressing these challenges requires a multidisciplinary approach, combining advancements in analytical chemistry with innovations in device design and protocol optimization. The development of point-of-care technologies and simplified, high-throughput methods represents a promising direction for overcoming these limitations and enhancing the clinical utility of antioxidant assays.

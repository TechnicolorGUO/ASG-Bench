## 2. Section 2: Technical Principles and Sensor Operation
Section 2 provides a comprehensive overview of the technical principles underlying Fiber Bragg Grating (FBG) sensors, focusing on their operational mechanisms and the factors influencing their performance. The section synthesizes the descriptions of sensing mechanisms, sensor fabrication, and interrogation techniques from multiple studies, highlighting both the consensus and the discrepancies in the literature. 

The sensing mechanism of FBG sensors is fundamentally based on the Bragg wavelength shift, which is a function of applied strain, temperature, and other physical parameters. The relationship between these parameters and the wavelength shift is typically described by a linear model, as presented in several papers. For instance, the equation $ \Delta \lambda_B = \lambda_B (1 - p_e) \Delta \epsilon + \lambda_B \left( \frac{d n}{d T} + \alpha \right) \Delta T $, where $ p_e $ is the effective photoelastic coefficient, $ \Delta \epsilon $ is the strain, and $ \Delta T $ is the temperature change, is frequently cited [1,3]. However, the scope of these models is often limited to moderate conditions, with nonlinear effects becoming significant under extreme strain or temperature variations. This limitation is noted in multiple studies, which emphasize the need for more advanced models that account for nonlinear behavior and improved calibration techniques [1,2].

In terms of sensor fabrication, the literature describes two primary methods: ultraviolet (UV) writing and femtosecond laser inscription. UV writing is widely used due to its simplicity and cost-effectiveness, making it suitable for large-scale production and standard applications [1,3]. In contrast, femtosecond laser inscription offers greater precision and flexibility, enabling the fabrication of gratings in non-photosensitive fibers and allowing for more tailored grating parameters. This method is particularly advantageous in specialized or high-precision applications [3].

Interrogation systems, which are essential for reading the Bragg wavelength shifts, include optical spectrum analyzers (OSAs) and wavelength demodulators. OSAs provide high-resolution spectral analysis, making them ideal for applications requiring high accuracy, but they are generally slower and more expensive. Wavelength demodulators, on the other hand, offer faster response times and are more suitable for real-time or dynamic monitoring, although they may sacrifice some resolution. While some studies have explored advanced interrogation techniques such as phase-sensitive optical time-domain reflectometry (OTDR), these methods are less commonly used due to their complexity and higher cost [1,3].

Despite the general agreement on the basic principles of FBG sensor operation, there are notable discrepancies in the interpretation of sensor behavior. For example, some studies propose alternative models for temperature compensation that differ from the standard linear model, reflecting ongoing efforts to refine the technical understanding of FBG sensors [1]. These variations highlight the dynamic nature of the field and the need for continued research to improve sensor accuracy and reliability.

In summary, Section 2 provides a structured synthesis of the technical principles and operational characteristics of FBG sensors, emphasizing the importance of accurate modeling, advanced fabrication techniques, and appropriate interrogation systems. The section also identifies key research gaps, such as the need for more robust models to handle nonlinear responses and the underexplored area of pressure sensing, which should guide future research efforts.

### 2.2 Subsection 2.2: Sensor Fabrication and Interrogation
Sensor fabrication and interrogation are critical components in the development and application of Fiber Bragg Grating (FBG) sensors. The primary fabrication method discussed in the literature is ultraviolet (UV) writing, which is well-established and widely used due to its reliability and simplicity. This technique involves the exposure of photosensitive optical fibers to UV light to induce periodic refractive index modulation, forming the Bragg grating. However, the use of femtosecond lasers has also been highlighted as an alternative, offering higher precision and the ability to inscribe gratings in non-photosensitive fibers, thereby expanding the range of applicable materials and environments [1,3].

The trade-offs between UV writing and femtosecond laser inscription are primarily related to cost, complexity, and flexibility. UV writing is generally more cost-effective and easier to implement, making it suitable for large-scale production and standard applications. In contrast, femtosecond laser writing, while more expensive and technically demanding, allows for greater control over grating parameters, such as period and depth, which can be crucial in specialized or high-precision applications. This distinction underscores the importance of aligning fabrication methods with specific application requirements, particularly in terms of resolution, environmental stability, and long-term reliability.

In terms of interrogation systems, two primary approaches are commonly employed: optical spectrum analyzers (OSA) and wavelength demodulators. OSAs provide high-resolution spectral analysis, making them ideal for applications requiring precise measurement of Bragg wavelength shifts. However, they are typically slower and more expensive, which limits their use in real-time monitoring scenarios. Wavelength demodulators, on the other hand, offer faster response times and are more suitable for dynamic or high-speed applications. Nevertheless, they may sacrifice some resolution compared to OSAs, which can be a critical factor in applications where high accuracy is paramount [1,3].

While some studies have explored advanced interrogation techniques such as phase-sensitive optical time-domain reflectometry (OTDR), these methods are less commonly discussed and often require more complex instrumentation and signal processing. As a result, their adoption remains limited to specialized research environments rather than mainstream engineering applications.

Based on the comparative analysis of fabrication and interrogation methods, a framework for selecting appropriate techniques can be proposed. For applications requiring high precision and stability, such as structural health monitoring in civil engineering, UV writing combined with OSA interrogation is often the preferred choice due to its proven reliability and accuracy. In contrast, for applications demanding real-time monitoring or integration into compact systems, such as aerospace or adaptive structures, femtosecond laser inscription paired with wavelength demodulation may be more suitable. This framework emphasizes the need for a tailored approach, where the selection of fabrication and interrogation methods is guided by the specific requirements of the application, including factors such as accuracy, speed, cost, and environmental conditions.
## 3. Section 3: Applications in Structural Health Monitoring (SHM)
Fiber Bragg Grating (FBG) sensors have been extensively applied in structural health monitoring (SHM) across diverse engineering domains, particularly in civil infrastructure and aerospace systems. In civil infrastructure monitoring, FBG sensors are deployed for detecting structural deformations, assessing the integrity of bridges, buildings, and dams, and enabling predictive maintenance through real-time data acquisition. The placement of FBG sensors in civil structures is often determined by structural complexity and monitoring objectives, with some studies advocating for high-density sensor arrangements to capture localized strain variations, while others propose strategic placement based on structural modeling to optimize cost and performance [1,2]. Data interpretation in civil SHM typically involves integration with finite element analysis (FEA) to validate structural models and predict failure points, although environmental noise and temperature fluctuations pose challenges that require advanced signal processing techniques [3].

In contrast, aerospace applications of FBG sensors focus on high-stress environments, such as adaptive wing structures, turbine components, and pipelines, where traditional sensors may suffer from signal degradation due to temperature and mechanical fatigue. The deployment of FBG sensors in aerospace systems emphasizes their durability, high sensitivity, and compatibility with composite materials, enabling distributed sensing over large areas. For instance, in the monitoring of an adaptive wing, FBG sensors are used in conjunction with shape memory alloy (SMA) actuators to detect structural damage and monitor actuator performance, demonstrating their suitability for real-time SHM in complex systems [4]. However, challenges such as thermal drift and wavelength shift in high-temperature environments necessitate compensation techniques to maintain measurement accuracy [3].

Both civil and aerospace applications highlight the importance of sensor placement strategies, data acquisition methods, and integration with existing monitoring systems. While civil infrastructure monitoring often relies on static or semi-static data collection for long-term structural assessment, aerospace applications demand real-time, high-resolution data acquisition to support dynamic structural analysis. Additionally, the challenges of environmental noise, long-term stability, and signal interference are prevalent in both domains, though addressed through different methodologies. In civil structures, the integration of FBG data with FEA models helps mitigate environmental effects, whereas in aerospace, advanced signal compensation and machine learning-based damage detection algorithms are employed to enhance reliability [4].

Despite these advancements, the literature identifies several research gaps, including the need for standardized data interpretation protocols, improved sensor durability in harsh environments, and the development of cost-effective signal processing techniques. Future research should focus on enhancing the interoperability of FBG sensors with existing SHM systems, expanding their application to more complex and dynamic structures, and addressing the limitations of current sensor deployment strategies to achieve more robust and scalable SHM solutions.

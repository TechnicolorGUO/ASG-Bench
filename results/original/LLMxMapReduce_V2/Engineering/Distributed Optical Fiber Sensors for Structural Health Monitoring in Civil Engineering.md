## 1. Section 1: Introduction and Background of Distributed Optical Fiber Sensors (DOFS)
Distributed Optical Fiber Sensors (DOFS) have emerged as a pivotal technology in the field of Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) within civil engineering, offering a paradigm shift from conventional point-based sensing systems. Unlike traditional sensors that provide localized measurements, DOFS enable continuous, real-time monitoring over extended distances, thereby enhancing the ability to detect and analyze subtle structural deformations, temperature variations, and acoustic activities across large-scale infrastructure such as bridges, tunnels, and high-rise buildings [1,2]. This capability has significantly expanded the scope of SHM, making DOFS an attractive solution for both operational and construction-phase monitoring.

The core principle of DOFS lies in the use of optical fibers as sensing elements, where changes in the physical properties of the fiber—such as strain, temperature, or acoustic vibrations—are detected through variations in the backscattered light signals. This principle has given rise to several key technologies, including Distributed Temperature Sensing (DTS), Distributed Temperature-Strain Sensing (DTSS), and Distributed Acoustic Sensing (DAS), each tailored to specific monitoring requirements. These technologies have demonstrated robust performance in various civil engineering applications, with notable improvements in spatial resolution and data acquisition speed in recent years [2].

Despite their advantages, the integration of DOFS into civil engineering practices has faced several challenges, particularly during the construction phase. Early implementations were constrained by long acquisition times, limited spatial resolution, and sensitivity to environmental factors, which hindered their real-time applicability. However, recent advancements in signal processing algorithms, fiber optic material design, and data interpretation techniques have significantly mitigated these limitations, enabling more reliable and efficient monitoring solutions. These developments have not only enhanced the feasibility of DOFS in construction monitoring but also underscored their potential for broader adoption in the SHM domain [1].

The literature also highlights a critical gap in the contextualization of DOFS within the broader landscape of structural monitoring technologies. While several studies emphasize the capabilities and applications of DOFS, they often lack a comprehensive review of earlier sensor technologies and their limitations, which could provide a more grounded understanding of the technological evolution. This absence of historical and comparative analysis suggests that future research should aim to bridge this gap, offering a more integrated perspective on the development and application of DOFS in civil engineering. Such an approach would not only enhance the theoretical foundation of DOFS but also improve their practical relevance in the context of evolving structural monitoring needs.

## 2. Section 2: Technical Principles and Sensing Mechanisms
The section on Technical Principles and Sensing Mechanisms provides a foundational understanding of the operational mechanisms that underpin distributed optical fiber sensors (DOFS) in structural health monitoring (SHM) within civil engineering. At the core of DOFS technology are the physical phenomena of Rayleigh, Raman, and Brillouin scattering, which enable the measurement of strain, temperature, and acoustic signals along the entire length of an optical fiber. Each scattering mechanism offers distinct advantages and limitations, with Rayleigh scattering providing high-resolution strain sensing, Brillouin scattering enabling dual-parameter monitoring of strain and temperature, and Raman scattering being particularly effective for temperature monitoring due to its strong temperature dependence [1,2].

The evolution of DOFS technology has seen a notable shift from Brillouin-based to Rayleigh-based sensing, driven by advancements in signal processing techniques and the development of more sophisticated interrogation systems. This transition has led to improved sensing accuracy and reliability, particularly in dynamic monitoring scenarios where high spatial resolution is critical. However, the transition also raises questions about the trade-offs between resolution, sensitivity, and the ability to monitor multiple parameters simultaneously, highlighting the need for further research into the integration of multiple sensing mechanisms into a single system [2].

Signal acquisition in DOFS is typically achieved through technologies such as Optical Time Domain Reflectometry (OTDR) and Optical Frequency Domain Reflectometry (OFDR). OTDR is well-suited for long-range monitoring, as it sends short optical pulses into the fiber and measures the backscattered light as a function of time, while OFDR, which uses a continuous wave light source and analyzes the frequency domain response, offers higher sensitivity and resolution in certain applications. Both methods have their unique advantages and limitations, and their selection depends on the specific requirements of the monitoring task, such as the desired spatial resolution, measurement range, and environmental conditions [1].

A critical challenge in DOFS systems is the accurate separation of temperature and strain effects, as both parameters can influence the backscattered signals. Advanced algorithms and multi-parameter sensing techniques are essential for reliable data interpretation, yet the literature often lacks detailed descriptions of the specific methods or software tools used in practice. ## 4. Section 4: Challenges and Limitations

While DOFS have shown reliability in controlled environments, their performance in real-world applications—where factors such as humidity, temperature extremes, and mechanical stress are prevalent—remains an area of ongoing research.

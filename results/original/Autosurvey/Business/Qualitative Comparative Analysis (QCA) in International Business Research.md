## 1 Introduction to QCA and Its Relevance in International Business Research

### 1.1 Definition and Origins of QCA

Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) is a research methodology that has emerged as a powerful tool for analyzing complex phenomena in social sciences, particularly in international business research. At its core, QCA is a configurational approach that enables researchers to explore how different combinations of conditions lead to specific outcomes. Unlike traditional methods that emphasize statistical significance or causal relationships in a linear fashion, QCA focuses on identifying configurations or patterns of conditions that can result in a particular outcome. This method is rooted in set theory and configurational thinking, which allows for a more nuanced understanding of the multifaceted nature of business phenomena.

The origins of QCA can be traced back to the work of Charles Ragin, who introduced the method in the 1980s as a way to analyze social and political phenomena. Ragin's approach was influenced by the philosophy of configurational thinking, which posits that outcomes are the result of multiple, interdependent factors rather than a single cause. This perspective is closely related to the idea of necessary and sufficient conditions, which is a fundamental concept in QCA. Necessary conditions are those that must be present for an outcome to occur, while sufficient conditions are those that, if present, guarantee the outcome. By identifying these conditions, researchers can better understand the complex causal relationships that underlie various business phenomena.

One of the key features of QCA is its use of set theory to model the relationships between different variables. In QCA, data is represented as a set of cases (or observations) and a set of conditions (or attributes). Each case is evaluated on the basis of whether it meets the conditions, and the analysis focuses on identifying the configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes. This approach allows researchers to explore the interplay between different variables and to identify the combinations of factors that contribute to a particular outcome. The use of set theory in QCA is closely related to the work of researchers in formal concept analysis, as discussed in the paper "Introduction to Formal Concept Analysis and Its Applications in Information Retrieval and Related Fields" [1]. This paper provides a comprehensive overview of the theoretical foundations of FCA and its applications, which share similarities with the conceptual framework of QCA.

In addition to its theoretical foundations, QCA has been influenced by developments in configurational thinking, which emphasizes the importance of considering the whole rather than just the parts. This approach is particularly relevant in international business research, where the interplay of multiple factors can significantly influence outcomes. Configurational thinking allows researchers to move beyond simple cause-and-effect relationships and to consider the complex, multi-dimensional nature of business phenomena. This perspective is closely aligned with the work of researchers in the field of quantum cognition, as explored in the paper "Quantum Structure in Cognition, Origins, Developments, Successes and Expectations" [2]. This paper highlights the importance of considering the interplay of multiple factors in understanding cognitive processes, which is a key principle of QCA.

The development of QCA has also been influenced by the growing interest in qualitative research methods, which seek to understand the complexities of social phenomena through in-depth analysis. Unlike quantitative methods that rely on statistical analysis to identify patterns, QCA provides a more flexible approach that allows for the exploration of complex, non-linear relationships. This flexibility is particularly valuable in international business research, where the interactions between different cultural, economic, and institutional factors can be highly complex. The paper "Qualitative Analysis for Human Centered AI" [3] discusses the importance of qualitative methods in understanding the complexities of human behavior and decision-making, which is a key area of application for QCA.

Furthermore, the emergence of new technologies and methodologies has contributed to the evolution of QCA. For example, the integration of QCA with digital technologies and big data analytics has opened up new possibilities for analyzing complex datasets. The paper "Quantum-inspired canonical correlation analysis for exponentially large dimensional data" [4] explores the potential of quantum-inspired methods to enhance the analysis of large-scale datasets. While this paper focuses on canonical correlation analysis, it highlights the broader trend of integrating advanced computational techniques with traditional research methods, including QCA.

In conclusion, the definition and origins of QCA are deeply rooted in set theory and configurational thinking. The method has evolved as a response to the limitations of traditional research approaches, offering a more nuanced and comprehensive way to analyze complex phenomena. By focusing on the configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes, QCA provides valuable insights into the multifaceted nature of international business research. As the field continues to develop, QCA is likely to play an increasingly important role in understanding the complex interactions that shape business outcomes.

## 2 Methodological Foundations of QCA

### 2.1 Theoretical Foundations of Set Theory in QCA

Set theory plays a fundamental role in the methodological foundation of Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA), serving as a conceptual and mathematical basis for understanding and analyzing complex configurations of conditions in international business research. At its core, QCA is rooted in set theory, which allows researchers to conceptualize and analyze phenomena as configurations of multiple conditions that may interact in non-linear and non-additive ways. This theoretical underpinning is essential for capturing the complexity of international business phenomena, where outcomes are often the result of multiple, interdependent factors rather than a single causal mechanism. By leveraging the principles of set theory, QCA enables researchers to move beyond simple cause-effect relationships and instead explore how different combinations of conditions can lead to similar or divergent outcomes [1].

The application of set theory in QCA is closely tied to the notion of "configurations," which refers to the specific combinations of conditions that are associated with an outcome of interest. In international business research, these configurations might include factors such as cultural norms, institutional environments, market characteristics, or organizational strategies. Each of these conditions is treated as a set, and QCA examines how these sets interact to produce particular outcomes. For instance, in the context of cross-cultural studies, set theory can help identify which combinations of cultural dimensions (e.g., power distance, individualism) are associated with successful market entry strategies in different regions. This approach is particularly valuable in international business research, where the same outcome (e.g., market success) may be achieved through different configurations of conditions, depending on the context.

The use of set theory in QCA also supports the concept of "necessary and sufficient conditions," which are critical for understanding causal relationships. A necessary condition is one that must be present for an outcome to occur, while a sufficient condition is one that, if present, guarantees the outcome. In QCA, researchers can identify both necessary and sufficient conditions by examining the relationships between sets of conditions and outcomes. For example, in the analysis of strategic decision-making in multinational corporations, set theory can be used to determine whether certain combinations of resources, capabilities, and market conditions are necessary or sufficient for successful strategy implementation. This approach provides a more nuanced understanding of causality compared to traditional statistical methods, which often focus on the average effect of a single variable while ignoring the complex interactions between multiple factors.

Set theory also underpins the concept of "logical equivalence" in QCA, which refers to the idea that different configurations of conditions can lead to the same outcome. This is particularly important in international business research, where outcomes such as innovation success or organizational performance may be influenced by a variety of factors that vary across regions and cultures. By using set theory, QCA allows researchers to identify these equivalent configurations and understand how they contribute to the same outcome. For instance, in the context of international supply chain management, set theory can be used to identify different configurations of suppliers, logistics networks, and regulatory environments that all lead to efficient and resilient supply chains. This insight is crucial for developing context-sensitive strategies that can be applied across different international settings.

Another key aspect of set theory in QCA is its ability to handle "complex causality," which refers to the interplay between multiple, interacting factors that contribute to an outcome. Traditional methods of analysis, such as regression or factor analysis, often struggle to capture the complexity of these interactions, as they rely on linear assumptions and may overlook non-linear relationships. In contrast, QCA, guided by set theory, can model these complex causal relationships by examining how different conditions co-occur and influence each other. For example, in the study of international collaboration and partnership, set theory can be used to identify the specific combinations of trust, communication, and cultural alignment that are necessary for successful collaboration. This approach not only enhances the understanding of how these factors interact but also provides actionable insights for managing cross-border partnerships.

The role of set theory in QCA is further reinforced by its alignment with the principles of "formal concept analysis" (FCA), a mathematical framework that is closely related to QCA. FCA is based on the idea of concepts as sets of objects and attributes, and it provides a way to systematically analyze and visualize the relationships between different entities. In the context of QCA, FCA can be used to identify and represent the configurations of conditions that are associated with specific outcomes. This is particularly useful in international business research, where data is often complex and multidimensional. By applying FCA, researchers can uncover patterns and relationships that may not be immediately apparent through traditional analytical methods.

The integration of set theory into QCA also enables the analysis of "qualitative data" in a systematic and rigorous manner. Unlike quantitative methods, which rely on numerical data and statistical models, QCA allows for the analysis of qualitative data by treating it as a set of conditions that can be combined and compared. This is especially relevant in international business research, where data often includes rich descriptions of cultural, institutional, and social contexts. For example, in the study of consumer behavior across different cultures, set theory can be used to identify the specific combinations of cultural values, consumer preferences, and marketing strategies that lead to successful brand positioning in different markets.

Furthermore, the theoretical foundations of set theory in QCA provide a framework for addressing the challenges of "data complexity" in international business research. QCA is particularly well-suited for analyzing small to medium-sized datasets, where traditional quantitative methods may struggle to capture the full complexity of the data. By using set theory, researchers can identify the key configurations of conditions that are associated with specific outcomes, even when the data is limited or heterogeneous. This is especially valuable in international business research, where data may be collected from different regions, cultures, and sources, leading to variations in data quality and completeness.

In conclusion, the role of set theory in QCA is central to its ability to understand and analyze complex configurations of conditions in international business research. By providing a mathematical and conceptual framework for modeling causal relationships, set theory enables researchers to move beyond simplistic cause-effect models and instead explore how different combinations of conditions interact to produce outcomes. This theoretical foundation not only enhances the analytical power of QCA but also makes it a valuable tool for addressing the complexities of international business phenomena. ### 3.1 Necessary and Sufficient Conditions

In the context of Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA), the concepts of necessary and sufficient conditions play a central role in identifying configurations of factors that lead to specific outcomes in international business research. These concepts are rooted in logical and methodological frameworks that allow researchers to understand the causal relationships between various elements in complex systems. Necessary conditions are those factors that must be present for an outcome to occur, while sufficient conditions are those that, if present, guarantee the occurrence of the outcome. Understanding these conditions is crucial for developing nuanced theories and practical strategies in international business settings.

Necessary conditions in QCA are identified through the process of necessity analysis, which involves examining whether a particular factor is consistently present in all cases where the outcome occurs. This approach is particularly useful in international business research, where the presence of certain factors may be essential for success in diverse markets. For example, in cross-cultural studies, necessary conditions might include cultural compatibility or market accessibility, which are critical for the successful implementation of international strategies [12]. By identifying these necessary conditions, researchers can better understand the prerequisites for achieving specific outcomes in complex, multifaceted environments.

Sufficient conditions, on the other hand, are those that, when present, are enough to produce the desired outcome. In QCA, the identification of sufficient conditions involves assessing whether the presence of a particular configuration of factors reliably leads to the outcome. This is often done through the use of sufficiency analysis, which helps to determine the extent to which certain combinations of factors can predict the occurrence of an outcome. For instance, in the context of strategic decision-making, a sufficient condition might be the presence of a strong market position combined with effective resource allocation, which together can lead to competitive advantage [36]. By identifying these sufficient conditions, researchers can gain insights into the configurations that are most likely to result in successful outcomes.

### 3.4 The Role of Set Theory in QCA

Set theory forms the foundational framework for Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA), providing a structured approach to understanding and analyzing complex configurations of conditions in international business research. QCA leverages set-theoretic concepts to represent and analyze the presence or absence of various factors and their combinations, which is crucial in understanding the multifaceted and often non-linear relationships that exist in international business contexts. This subsection delves into the role of set theory in QCA, highlighting how it underpins the analysis of configurations and the identification of causal relationships in international business research.

At the core of QCA is the idea that phenomena can be understood through the lens of set membership. In QCA, conditions are treated as sets, and cases are assessed based on their membership in these sets. This approach allows researchers to move beyond traditional statistical methods that often assume linear relationships and instead explore how different combinations of conditions can lead to specific outcomes. Set theory provides the mathematical rigor necessary to define and manipulate these sets, making it possible to identify necessary and sufficient conditions for certain outcomes [49].

One of the key aspects of set theory in QCA is its ability to handle complex configurations. In international business research, the factors that influence business outcomes are often interdependent and multifaceted. Set theory enables the analysis of these configurations by allowing researchers to consider the joint presence or absence of multiple conditions. This is particularly useful in studying phenomena such as strategic decision-making, where the success of a strategy may depend on the interplay of several factors, such as market conditions, organizational capabilities, and cultural dynamics [50]. By treating these factors as sets, QCA can identify the configurations that are most likely to lead to successful outcomes.

Another important aspect of set theory in QCA is its role in identifying causal relationships. QCA is rooted in configurational thinking, which posits that outcomes are the result of multiple, interdependent factors rather than single, isolated causes. This aligns with the principles of set theory, which emphasizes the relationships between sets and their elements. In QCA, causal relationships are identified through the analysis of set memberships, where the presence of certain conditions in a set is associated with the occurrence of a particular outcome. This approach allows researchers to uncover the complex causal pathways that contribute to specific outcomes in international business contexts [51].

Set theory also provides a framework for understanding the concept of necessity and sufficiency in QCA. A condition is considered necessary if its presence is required for the occurrence of an outcome, while a condition is considered sufficient if its presence alone is enough to produce the outcome. By applying set-theoretic concepts, QCA can identify these necessary and sufficient conditions, which is essential for understanding the causal mechanisms that underlie business phenomena [52]. For example, in cross-cultural studies, set theory can help identify the necessary conditions for successful business practices in different cultural contexts, as well as the sufficient conditions that may lead to success in those contexts.

The application of set theory in QCA is further enhanced by the use of Boolean logic, which provides a systematic way to analyze the interactions between different conditions. Boolean logic allows researchers to represent the relationships between sets using logical operators such as AND, OR, and NOT, which is essential for identifying the configurations that lead to specific outcomes. This logical framework is particularly useful in international business research, where the relationships between variables are often complex and non-linear [53].

Moreover, set theory in QCA is supported by the use of empirical data and case studies, which are essential for validating the theoretical framework. By applying set-theoretic concepts to real-world data, researchers can test the validity of their configurations and causal relationships. This empirical approach is crucial for ensuring that the findings of QCA are grounded in actual business practices and can be applied to real-world scenarios [54]. For instance, in analyzing global market dynamics, set theory can help identify the configurations of economic, political, and cultural factors that contribute to market success, providing valuable insights for international business strategies [55].

In addition, the use of set theory in QCA allows for the integration of qualitative and quantitative methods, enhancing the comprehensiveness of the analysis. By treating conditions as sets, QCA can combine the strengths of qualitative case studies with the rigor of quantitative analysis, providing a more nuanced understanding of business phenomena. This integration is particularly beneficial in international business research, where the complexity of the phenomena often requires a multi-method approach [56].

The role of set theory in QCA is also evident in the development of advanced analytical techniques such as fuzzy set QCA (fsQCA) and multi-value QCA (mvQCA). These methods extend the traditional binary set theory to allow for degrees of membership, providing a more flexible and realistic representation of the conditions that influence business outcomes. This flexibility is crucial in international business research, where the relationships between variables are often not binary but exist on a spectrum [56].

Overall, set theory provides the theoretical foundation for QCA, enabling the analysis of complex configurations and the identification of causal relationships in international business research. ### 3.6 Comparing QCA with Traditional Causal Analysis

Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) offers a distinctive approach to causal analysis that differs significantly from traditional causal analysis methods. Traditional causal analysis methods, such as regression analysis or structural equation modeling, typically assume linear relationships between variables and emphasize identifying single, dominant causal factors that explain an outcome. These methods often rely on statistical techniques that prioritize quantifiable, measurable variables and are designed to uncover average effects across a population. In contrast, QCA is rooted in configurational thinking, which posits that outcomes emerge from multiple, interdependent conditions that interact in complex ways. One of the key advantages of QCA over traditional causal analysis is its ability to handle causal complexity. Instead, they often depend on a combination of conditions that interact in non-linear and sometimes contradictory ways. Traditional methods, which often rely on linear models, may struggle to capture these complex interactions, leading to oversimplified or misleading conclusions. QCA, on the other hand, uses set-theoretic logic to explore multiple causal pathways and configurations. This allows researchers to identify both necessary and sufficient conditions for an outcome, providing a more nuanced understanding of the factors that contribute to business phenomena [59].

Traditional causal analysis methods also tend to prioritize generalizability and statistical significance over context-specific insights. While this is valuable for identifying broad trends, it can sometimes obscure the unique combinations of factors that lead to specific outcomes in particular contexts. QCA, by contrast, is particularly well-suited for studying cases where the interplay of multiple factors is critical. For example, in cross-cultural studies, QCA can reveal the configurations of cultural, economic, and institutional factors that contribute to successful international market entry or strategic decision-making [7]. This contextual sensitivity is a major strength of QCA, especially in international business research, where the same factor may have different effects in different settings.

Another important distinction lies in the way QCA and traditional causal analysis handle data. Traditional methods often require large datasets to achieve statistical power and generalizability. While this is useful for identifying trends, it can be a limitation when dealing with complex or rare phenomena that are difficult to measure quantitatively. QCA, by contrast, is more flexible in terms of data requirements and can work effectively with small to medium-sized datasets. This makes it particularly useful in international business research, where data availability may be limited, and where the focus is often on understanding specific, context-rich cases [27].

Moreover, traditional causal analysis methods are often limited in their ability to capture the multiplicity of causes that contribute to an outcome. For instance, in strategic decision-making, a successful business strategy may result from a combination of factors such as market conditions, organizational capabilities, and leadership decisions. ## 5 Comparative Analysis of QCA with Other Research Methods

### 5.1 Comparative Overview of QCA and Statistical Analysis

Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) and statistical analysis are two distinct approaches to data analysis, each with its own strengths and limitations in the context of international business research. While statistical analysis is rooted in quantitative methods, relying on numerical data and mathematical models to identify patterns and relationships, QCA is a configurational method that focuses on understanding complex causal relationships through the examination of multiple conditions and their interactions. This subsection provides a comparative overview of QCA and statistical analysis, highlighting their respective strengths in handling complex data and uncovering relationships in international business research.

Statistical analysis is a well-established method in the social sciences, including international business research. It involves the use of numerical data and mathematical techniques to identify trends, correlations, and causal relationships. Statistical methods, such as regression analysis, factor analysis, and cluster analysis, are widely used to examine large datasets and draw inferences about populations based on sample data. One of the key strengths of statistical analysis is its ability to handle large datasets and provide generalizable results. This makes it particularly useful in international business research, where data from multiple countries and regions can be analyzed to identify common patterns and differences. However, statistical analysis can be limited in its ability to capture the complexity and multifaceted nature of international business phenomena. For instance, statistical methods often assume linearity and independence of variables, which may not always hold true in real-world scenarios where multiple factors interact in non-linear ways [31].

In contrast, QCA is a configurational method that emphasizes the identification of multiple causal pathways and the analysis of complex configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes. Unlike statistical analysis, which typically focuses on the average effect of variables, QCA allows researchers to explore how different combinations of conditions can lead to similar or different outcomes. This makes QCA particularly suitable for studying complex phenomena in international business research, such as strategic decision-making, cross-cultural dynamics, and global market trends. QCA is also well-suited for handling small to medium-sized datasets, which is a common scenario in international business research where data collection can be challenging due to the diversity of contexts and the difficulty of obtaining comprehensive data [31].

One of the key advantages of QCA over statistical analysis is its ability to capture the context-specific nature of international business phenomena. While statistical analysis often seeks to identify universal patterns and generalizable results, QCA acknowledges that the same set of conditions may lead to different outcomes in different contexts. This is particularly relevant in international business research, where cultural, economic, and institutional factors can significantly influence business outcomes. For example, a study using QCA to analyze the impact of cultural dimensions on consumer behavior in different countries may reveal that the same cultural factor has different effects in different regions, which would be difficult to capture using traditional statistical methods [31].

Another strength of QCA is its ability to handle non-linear and interactive relationships between variables. Statistical analysis often relies on linear models, which may not be appropriate for capturing the complex interactions between variables in international business research. QCA, on the other hand, uses Boolean logic and set theory to analyze the configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes. This allows researchers to identify multiple causal pathways and understand how different combinations of conditions can lead to similar or different outcomes. For instance, a study using QCA to analyze the success factors of multinational corporations may reveal that different combinations of resources, strategies, and market conditions can lead to successful outcomes in different contexts [31].

However, QCA also has its limitations, particularly in terms of generalizability. While QCA provides a detailed understanding of specific cases, it may not be as effective as statistical analysis in identifying generalizable patterns across a large number of cases. This is because QCA focuses on the configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes, which may vary significantly across different contexts. In contrast, statistical analysis can provide more generalizable results by identifying common patterns and relationships across a large dataset. For example, a statistical analysis of consumer behavior across multiple countries may reveal that certain demographic factors consistently influence purchasing decisions, which may not be as evident in a QCA analysis of a smaller number of cases [31].

The integration of QCA with statistical analysis can also enhance the understanding of complex phenomena in international business research. While QCA provides a detailed, context-specific analysis of the configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes, statistical analysis can provide a broader, more generalizable understanding of the relationships between variables. ### 5.2 QCA versus Case Study Methods

Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) and case study methods are both widely used in social science research, but they differ in their approaches, objectives, and applicability, especially in the context of international business research. While both methods aim to explore complex phenomena, they differ significantly in their underlying philosophical foundations, data collection and analysis techniques, and the types of insights they generate. Understanding these differences is essential for researchers in international business who are seeking to select the most appropriate method for their studies.

QCA is a configurational approach that emphasizes the identification of multiple causal pathways leading to a particular outcome. It is rooted in set theory and Boolean logic, allowing researchers to analyze complex configurations of conditions and their relationships to outcomes. QCA is particularly useful for studying phenomena that involve multiple, interdependent factors, such as strategic decision-making, cross-cultural dynamics, and global market trends [12]. The method enables researchers to uncover patterns and relationships that may not be apparent through traditional quantitative methods, making it well-suited for exploring causality in complex, multi-faceted phenomena [18].

In contrast, case study methods focus on in-depth, detailed exploration of a specific context or phenomenon. They are often used to generate rich, descriptive insights into particular cases, allowing researchers to understand the nuances of the studied phenomenon in its natural setting. Case studies are particularly valuable when the research aims to explore the unique characteristics of a specific context or when the phenomenon under investigation is not well understood. This approach is often employed in international business research to analyze the strategic decisions of multinational corporations, the dynamics of cross-cultural interactions, and the challenges of operating in diverse market environments [36].

One of the key differences between QCA and case study methods lies in their approach to generalizability. QCA is designed to identify configurations that are common across cases, allowing for the development of generalizable insights about the conditions that lead to specific outcomes. This is achieved through the use of Boolean logic and set-theoretic principles, which enable researchers to identify necessary and sufficient conditions for the occurrence of an outcome. In contrast, case study methods prioritize depth over breadth, often focusing on a limited number of cases to generate detailed insights that are specific to the studied context. While this approach provides a rich understanding of the particular case, it may not be as effective in identifying broader patterns or generalizable principles [71].

Another important distinction is the role of data in each method. QCA typically involves the analysis of a relatively small number of cases, often ranging from 10 to 30, and relies on qualitative or mixed methods data to identify the configurations of conditions that lead to the outcome of interest. The method is particularly useful when the dataset is small or when the research question involves complex causal relationships. Case study methods, on the other hand, often involve a more extensive and varied data collection process, including interviews, observations, and document analysis. This allows for a more comprehensive understanding of the case, but it can also make the method more time-consuming and resource-intensive.

The analytical approach of QCA is also distinct from that of case study methods. QCA employs a systematic, structured approach to analyzing configurations, using set-theoretic and Boolean logic to identify the relationships between conditions and outcomes. This approach allows for the identification of multiple causal pathways and the exploration of the interdependencies between different factors. In contrast, case study methods rely on a more interpretive, subjective approach, where the researcher's insights and understanding play a crucial role in the analysis. While this can provide valuable insights into the specific case, it may also introduce biases and limitations in the interpretation of the findings.

Despite these differences, both QCA and case study methods have their own strengths and limitations. QCA is particularly effective in identifying generalizable patterns and causal relationships, making it well-suited for research that seeks to understand the conditions that lead to specific outcomes. However, it may not be as effective in capturing the richness and complexity of individual cases. Case study methods, on the other hand, excel at providing in-depth insights into specific contexts, but they may lack the generalizability and systematic approach of QCA.

In the context of international business research, the choice between QCA and case study methods depends on the research objectives and the nature of the phenomenon being studied. If the goal is to identify the configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes, QCA may be the more appropriate method. However, if the research aims to explore the unique characteristics of a specific context or to generate a detailed understanding of a particular phenomenon, a case study approach may be more suitable.

In addition to these differences, the integration of QCA with other research methods can enhance the depth and breadth of international business research. Mixed-methods approaches that combine QCA with case study methods can provide a more comprehensive understanding of complex phenomena, allowing researchers to leverage the strengths of both methods. For example, QCA can be used to identify the configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes, while case study methods can provide in-depth insights into the processes and mechanisms that underlie these configurations [72].

The integration of QCA with digital technologies and emerging methodologies also offers new possibilities for international business research. As the field of artificial intelligence and machine learning continues to evolve, there is potential for QCA to be combined with these technologies to enhance the analysis of complex datasets and to uncover new insights into international business phenomena. For instance, machine learning algorithms can be used to identify patterns in large datasets, which can then be analyzed using QCA to identify the configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes [4].

In conclusion, while QCA and case study methods differ in their approaches, objectives, and applicability, both methods offer valuable insights into the complexities of international business research. The choice between these methods depends on the research goals, the nature of the phenomenon being studied, and the resources available to the researcher. By understanding the strengths and limitations of each method, researchers can make informed decisions about the most appropriate approach for their studies. ### 5.4 Limitations of QCA Compared to Other Methods

While Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) offers a powerful framework for analyzing complex causal relationships in international business research, it is not without limitations. Compared to other research methods such as statistical analysis, case studies, and mixed-methods approaches, QCA presents several challenges that can hinder its effectiveness in certain contexts. These limitations primarily relate to issues of generalizability, data requirements, and the complexity of configuration analysis, which are critical factors in the application of QCA in international business research. 

One of the most significant limitations of QCA is its restricted generalizability. Unlike quantitative methods such as statistical analysis, which rely on large sample sizes and probabilistic inferences, QCA is designed to work with smaller datasets and focuses on configurational logic rather than statistical significance. This makes it less suitable for studies that aim to generalize findings across a broad population or across different cultural and economic contexts. For instance, while QCA can effectively identify specific configurations of conditions that lead to a particular outcome, these findings may not be easily transferable to other settings due to the unique contextual factors that shape each case [74]. This lack of generalizability is a major constraint, especially in international business research, where cross-cultural and cross-regional differences are often significant.

Another limitation of QCA is its data requirements. QCA requires a high level of data quality and careful selection of cases, which can be challenging in international business research. Unlike statistical methods that can handle large, diverse datasets, QCA demands a more rigorous and systematic approach to data collection and case selection. This can be particularly problematic when dealing with international data, where variations in data availability, accessibility, and consistency across different regions and cultures can complicate the analysis. For example, the availability of comprehensive and reliable data on international business practices, cultural norms, and market trends may be limited, making it difficult to apply QCA effectively. Furthermore, QCA's reliance on binary or fuzzy-set data can be restrictive, as it may not capture the nuanced and continuous nature of many variables in international business contexts [75].

The complexity of configuration analysis is another key limitation of QCA. While QCA's configurational approach allows for the identification of multiple causal pathways, it also introduces a level of complexity that can be difficult to manage, especially for researchers unfamiliar with the methodology. The process of identifying and analyzing configurations requires careful selection of conditions, as well as the interpretation of complex relationships between variables. This can lead to challenges in ensuring the reliability and validity of findings, as the outcomes of QCA are often sensitive to the choices made during the analysis. For instance, the selection of conditions and the way in which they are aggregated can significantly affect the results, making it essential for researchers to have a deep understanding of the theoretical and methodological foundations of QCA. Additionally, the interpretation of configuration results can be subjective, as different researchers may arrive at different conclusions based on the same data, which can limit the reproducibility of findings [37].

Moreover, QCA's reliance on specific assumptions and its sensitivity to data quality can further complicate its application in international business research. QCA assumes that the relationships between variables are deterministic, which may not always be the case in real-world business settings where variables are often interdependent and subject to change over time. This can lead to challenges in capturing the dynamic and evolving nature of international business phenomena, which are often influenced by a wide range of factors such as political, economic, and technological changes. Furthermore, QCA's focus on configurations can sometimes overlook the role of individual-level variations, which may be important in understanding the nuances of international business processes [36].

In comparison to other research methods, QCA also faces limitations in terms of its ability to handle large-scale datasets and complex interactions between variables. While statistical methods such as regression analysis and machine learning techniques are well-suited for dealing with large, high-dimensional data, QCA is typically more suited for smaller datasets with a limited number of cases. This can be a significant disadvantage in international business research, where the complexity of global markets and the diversity of business practices often require the analysis of extensive and heterogeneous datasets. Additionally, the computational demands of QCA can be high, particularly when dealing with complex configurations and multiple causal pathways, which can make it less efficient compared to other methods that are optimized for large-scale data processing [4].

In conclusion, while QCA offers a valuable approach for analyzing complex causal relationships in international business research, it is important to recognize its limitations in comparison to other research methods. The challenges related to generalizability, data requirements, and the complexity of configuration analysis highlight the need for careful consideration when choosing QCA as a research method. Researchers must weigh the strengths and weaknesses of QCA against the specific needs of their study, ensuring that the methodology aligns with their research objectives and the characteristics of their data. By doing so, they can maximize the effectiveness of QCA and enhance the validity of their findings in the context of international business research.

### 7.3 Interpretation and Causal Complexity

Interpreting the results of Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) poses significant challenges, especially within the context of international business research, where causal relationships are often complex and multifaceted. QCA is designed to identify configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes, which makes it particularly useful in analyzing complex, non-linear causal relationships. However, the very nature of these relationships can complicate the interpretation of QCA results, as researchers may encounter multiple pathways and overlapping conditions that are difficult to disentangle. This complexity can lead to potential misinterpretations or overgeneralizations if not carefully managed.

One of the primary difficulties in interpreting QCA results lies in the identification of causal pathways. Unlike traditional quantitative methods that often assume a single, linear causal relationship, QCA recognizes that multiple configurations of conditions can lead to the same outcome. This multiplicity of pathways, while a strength of QCA, can also be a source of confusion. For instance, in the context of international business, different combinations of cultural, economic, and institutional factors can lead to similar outcomes, such as successful market entry strategies [7]. Researchers must carefully consider these multiple pathways and avoid the trap of overgeneralizing the results, which can lead to misleading conclusions about the causal mechanisms at play.

Moreover, the interpretation of QCA results requires a deep understanding of the theoretical framework and the context in which the analysis is conducted. The theoretical underpinnings of QCA, such as configurational thinking and the use of set theory, provide a robust foundation for analyzing complex phenomena. However, the application of these theoretical concepts in real-world international business scenarios can be challenging. For example, the use of set theory to analyze the interplay of various factors in international market dynamics may require a nuanced understanding of how these factors interact and influence each other [15]. Without a solid theoretical foundation, researchers may misinterpret the results, leading to an incomplete or inaccurate understanding of the causal relationships being studied.

Another challenge in interpreting QCA results is the potential for misinterpretation due to the complexity of the data. QCA often involves a large number of conditions and configurations, which can make it difficult to discern the most relevant factors contributing to the outcome. For instance, in the study of international collaboration in science, researchers may encounter a multitude of variables that influence the success of collaborative projects, such as institutional support, funding, and cultural compatibility [73]. The complexity of these variables can make it challenging to determine which factors are most influential, leading to potential misinterpretations of the results.

Furthermore, the interpretation of QCA results can be influenced by the researcher's biases and assumptions. Researchers must be aware of their own preconceptions and how these might affect their interpretation of the data. For example, in the analysis of consumer behavior across cultures, researchers may inadvertently impose their own cultural perspectives on the data, leading to a biased interpretation of the findings [37]. This bias can result in overgeneralizations that do not accurately reflect the diversity of consumer behavior in different cultural contexts.

The potential for overgeneralization is a significant concern in the interpretation of QCA results. While QCA is well-suited for analyzing complex, multi-faceted phenomena, the results can sometimes be overly generalized, leading to an incomplete understanding of the specific conditions and contexts that contribute to the outcomes. For example, in the study of international market analysis, researchers may identify certain configurations of conditions that lead to successful market entry strategies, but these findings may not be applicable to all markets due to variations in local conditions [41]. Overgeneralization can undermine the validity of the findings and limit the practical applications of the research.

To mitigate these challenges, researchers must employ a rigorous and transparent approach to interpreting QCA results. This includes clearly defining the research questions, selecting appropriate conditions and configurations, and using a combination of qualitative and quantitative methods to validate the findings. For instance, in the study of QCA in strategic decision-making, researchers may use both qualitative interviews and quantitative data analysis to ensure that the findings are robust and reliable [36]. By adopting a mixed-methods approach, researchers can enhance the validity of their interpretations and reduce the risk of misinterpretation.

Additionally, the use of advanced analytical techniques, such as machine learning and data mining, can help researchers navigate the complexity of QCA results. These techniques can assist in identifying patterns and relationships that may not be immediately apparent through traditional methods. For example, in the study of financial data analysis, researchers can use machine learning algorithms to uncover hidden patterns in the data and improve the accuracy of their interpretations [40]. By leveraging these advanced techniques, researchers can enhance their ability to interpret QCA results and draw meaningful conclusions.

In conclusion, the interpretation of QCA results in international business research is a complex and challenging process that requires careful consideration of the theoretical framework, contextual factors, and potential biases. The multiplicity of causal pathways and the complexity of the data can lead to misinterpretations or overgeneralizations if not properly managed. By employing a rigorous and transparent approach to interpretation, using advanced analytical techniques, and being aware of their own biases, researchers can enhance the validity and reliability of their findings. Ultimately, the successful interpretation of QCA results depends on the researcher's ability to navigate these challenges and draw meaningful conclusions from the data.

### 7.4 Generalizability and Context-Specificity

Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) has emerged as a powerful method for analyzing complex configurations in international business research. However, its application is not without limitations, particularly concerning generalizability and context-specificity. Generalizability refers to the extent to which findings from a specific study can be applied to other contexts, while context-specificity emphasizes the need for interpretations that are tailored to the unique characteristics of the studied environment. In the context of international business research, these two aspects are closely intertwined, as the cultural, economic, and institutional differences across regions can significantly influence the validity and applicability of QCA findings.

One of the primary challenges in achieving generalizability with QCA is the reliance on case-specific data. QCA typically requires a small to moderate number of cases, which can limit the ability to draw broad conclusions that apply across different markets or regions. For instance, a study that analyzes the success factors of multinational corporations in a specific country may not be directly applicable to other countries with different cultural norms, regulatory environments, or market dynamics [12]. The context-specific nature of QCA results means that findings must be interpreted within the particular framework of the studied cases, which can make it difficult to extrapolate these insights to other settings.

Moreover, the interpretive nature of QCA can contribute to its context-specificity. Unlike quantitative methods that often provide statistically significant results, QCA focuses on identifying configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes. This approach is inherently subjective, as the selection of conditions and the interpretation of their interactions can vary depending on the researcher's perspective and the context of the study. For example, a study examining the role of cultural dimensions in consumer behavior might yield different insights when applied to a Western versus an Eastern market, as the underlying cultural values and norms can significantly influence the outcomes [12]. Therefore, QCA findings must be interpreted with caution, recognizing that they are shaped by the specific cultural and institutional contexts in which they are generated.

Another factor that affects the generalizability of QCA is the potential for sampling bias. Since QCA often involves a small number of cases, the selection of these cases can significantly impact the results. If the cases are not representative of the broader population or if they are selected based on convenience or availability, the findings may not be generalizable to other contexts. For instance, a study that focuses on a limited number of successful firms in a particular industry may not capture the diverse strategies and conditions that exist across different sectors or regions [36]. This limitation highlights the importance of careful case selection and the need to consider the diversity of the studied cases to enhance the generalizability of QCA findings.

In addition to these challenges, the complexity of international business environments further complicates the generalizability of QCA. The global business landscape is characterized by a multitude of variables, including economic fluctuations, political instability, and cultural differences, which can interact in non-linear and unpredictable ways. QCA is well-suited to analyzing these complex configurations, but its ability to generalize findings is constrained by the need to account for these multifaceted interactions. For example, a study that identifies a specific configuration of factors leading to successful market entry in one region may not be applicable to another region where the economic and cultural conditions are different [41]. This underscores the importance of context-specific interpretations and the need to adapt QCA findings to the unique characteristics of the target environment.

The integration of QCA with other methodologies can also influence its generalizability and context-specificity. While QCA provides a configurational perspective, it may benefit from being combined with quantitative methods to enhance the robustness of the findings. For instance, a mixed-methods approach that combines QCA with statistical analysis can help validate the configurations identified by QCA and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the factors influencing business outcomes [21]. However, this integration requires careful consideration of the data and the research questions, as the combination of different methodologies can introduce additional complexities and challenges.

Furthermore, the use of QCA in international business research must take into account the evolving nature of global markets. As markets become increasingly interconnected and dynamic, the configurations of conditions that lead to successful outcomes may change over time. QCA findings that are based on historical data may not be relevant to current or future contexts, highlighting the need for continuous updates and adaptations. For example, a study that identifies successful strategies for entering a specific market may need to be revisited as new technologies, consumer behaviors, or regulatory changes emerge [35]. This dynamic nature of international business environments underscores the importance of context-specific interpretations and the need to remain vigilant about the limitations of QCA in capturing the evolving landscape.

In summary, the generalizability and context-specificity of QCA in international business research are critical considerations that must be addressed to ensure the validity and applicability of its findings. The inherent subjectivity of QCA, the potential for sampling bias, and the complex interactions of variables in international business environments all contribute to the challenges of achieving generalizability. By recognizing these limitations and adopting a context-specific approach, researchers can enhance the relevance and impact of their QCA studies, ensuring that the insights gained are both meaningful and applicable to the unique challenges of international business.

### 7.5 Methodological Limitations

One of the significant methodological limitations of Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) in international business research lies in its reliance on specific assumptions. QCA is fundamentally based on set theory, configurational thinking, and Boolean logic, which requires that the researcher clearly defines the conditions and outcomes of interest. However, the assumptions underlying these theoretical foundations may not always align with the complexities of international business phenomena, which are often characterized by dynamic, multi-dimensional, and context-specific factors. For example, the assumption that causal relationships are configurational and non-linear may not hold in all cases, particularly when dealing with highly deterministic or simple relationships. This can lead to challenges in interpreting results, especially when the conditions and outcomes do not perfectly align with the expected configurations. Such assumptions also raise questions about the generalizability of QCA findings, as the method is primarily designed for small to medium-sized datasets, which may not capture the full complexity of global business environments [96].

Another critical limitation of QCA is its sensitivity to data quality. The accuracy and reliability of QCA results depend heavily on the quality of the data used, including the consistency, completeness, and validity of the data. In international business research, data collection can be particularly challenging due to differences in data availability, access, and representation across countries and cultures. For instance, the reliance on qualitative data, such as interviews or case studies, may introduce biases and inconsistencies, as the interpretation of data can vary depending on the researcher's perspective and the cultural context. Moreover, the lack of standardized data collection and measurement procedures can further complicate the application of QCA in cross-cultural or multinational settings. These issues can lead to a higher risk of misinterpretation, which in turn affects the validity and reliability of the findings [7].

Additionally, QCA faces significant limitations in handling large-scale or highly complex datasets. While QCA is well-suited for analyzing small to medium-sized datasets, it can struggle with the computational demands of larger, more complex datasets. This is particularly relevant in international business research, where the datasets may involve numerous variables, multiple cases, and intricate interactions. The method's reliance on Boolean logic and set-theoretic approaches may not be efficient or effective in capturing the nuances of such complex datasets. Furthermore, the computational complexity of QCA increases rapidly with the number of cases and conditions, making it less feasible for large-scale analyses. This limitation is compounded by the fact that many international business datasets are often characterized by high levels of heterogeneity and variability, which further complicates the application of QCA [97].

The methodological constraints of QCA are also evident in its ability to handle uncertainty and ambiguity. In international business research, uncertainty is a common characteristic of many phenomena, such as market dynamics, cultural differences, and regulatory environments. QCA, with its deterministic and configurational approach, may not adequately account for the inherent uncertainties and ambiguities that are often present in these contexts. This can result in a limited ability to capture the full range of possibilities and variations that may exist in international business scenarios. For example, in cases where the causal relationships are not clearly defined or are subject to change, QCA may struggle to provide a comprehensive analysis. This limitation highlights the need for additional methodological approaches that can better accommodate the complexities and uncertainties of international business research [7].

Moreover, the methodological limitations of QCA extend to its application in dynamic and evolving business environments. International business research often involves studying phenomena that are subject to rapid changes, such as technological advancements, market shifts, and regulatory changes. QCA, with its focus on static configurations, may not be well-suited for analyzing these dynamic contexts. The method is typically used to identify stable configurations of conditions that lead to specific outcomes, which may not capture the evolving nature of many international business phenomena. This can limit the applicability of QCA in contexts where the relationships between conditions and outcomes are fluid or changing over time [7].

In addition, the methodological limitations of QCA include challenges related to the interpretation of results. While QCA provides a systematic approach to analyzing complex configurations, the interpretation of the results can be subjective and influenced by the researcher's biases and assumptions. This is particularly relevant in international business research, where cultural, linguistic, and contextual factors can significantly influence the interpretation of data. The reliance on expert judgment and qualitative insights can introduce variability in the analysis, making it difficult to ensure consistency and objectivity. This issue is further exacerbated by the lack of standardized criteria for evaluating the quality and validity of QCA results, which can lead to discrepancies in the interpretation of findings across different studies [7].

Finally, the methodological limitations of QCA also extend to its integration with other research methodologies. While QCA offers a unique approach to analyzing complex configurations, it may not always be compatible with other qualitative or quantitative methods. This can pose challenges in the development of integrated research designs that combine multiple methodologies. For instance, the use of QCA alongside statistical analysis or case study methods may require careful consideration of how the data and findings from each method are interpreted and synthesized. This limitation highlights the need for further research into the integration of QCA with other methodologies to enhance its applicability and effectiveness in international business research [7].

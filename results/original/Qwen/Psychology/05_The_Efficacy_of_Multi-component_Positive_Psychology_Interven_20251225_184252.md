## Introduction and Background

The field of positive psychology, conceptualized by Martin Seligman in 1998, represents a significant paradigm shift within the discipline of psychology, moving beyond a singular focus on pathology and mental illness to systematically study the factors that contribute to human flourishing [[12]]. This scientific inquiry into positive subjective experiences, individual character traits, and societal virtues has given rise to a diverse array of interventions designed to enhance well-being and mitigate psychopathology. Within this domain, multi-component positive psychology interventions (MPPIs) have emerged as a prominent approach, integrating multiple evidence-based techniques to foster psychological growth. An MPPI is broadly defined as an intervention that combines two or more distinct, theoretically-grounded positive psychology modules, such as gratitude exercises, strengths use, optimism training, mindfulness, and savoring practices [[11,12]]. These interventions are often structured around overarching theoretical models, most notably Seligman's PERMA model, which posits that well-being is composed of five core elements: Positive Emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, and Accomplishment [[7,11]].

The rationale for employing multi-component approaches is rooted in the understanding that well-being is a multifaceted construct, and targeting a single component may be insufficient to produce meaningful change. By simultaneously cultivating several pathways to well-being, these interventions aim to create synergistic effects that lead to more robust and sustainable outcomes. The development of MPPIs can be traced through a series of landmark studies and meta-analyses. Early work by Fordyce (1983) on happiness programs laid some of the groundwork, but it was the foundational articles by Seligman and Csikszentmihalyi (2000) and Seligman (2002) that formally launched the modern field [[12]]. Subsequently, comprehensive meta-analyses by Sin and Lyubomirsky (2009) and Bolier et al. (2013) provided quantitative evidence for the efficacy of PPIs, finding small-to-moderate effect sizes for enhancing well-being and reducing depression [[6,8,9]]. These findings established a baseline of efficacy, prompting researchers to explore how different intervention characteristics, particularly their complexity, might influence outcomes.

Despite the growing body of research, the precise effectiveness of MPPIs remains a subject of intense scrutiny. Initial meta-analytic results were promising but also highlighted significant methodological limitations in the primary literature. For instance, the Bolier et al. (2013) review noted that only one of the 39 included studies was rated as high quality, with many lacking adequate randomization concealment or intention-to-treat analysis [[9,14]]. Furthermore, subsequent reanalyses have raised concerns about the inflation of reported effect sizes due to publication bias and methodological flaws in earlier syntheses [[10]]. This context underscores the critical need for a systematic review and meta-analysis that not only quantifies the current state of evidence for MPPIs but also critically evaluates the methodological rigor of the contributing studies. Such an analysis is essential for distinguishing between genuine therapeutic effects and artifacts of poor research design. This review aims to address this need by synthesizing the findings from recent randomized controlled trials (RCTs) on MPPIs, providing a comprehensive overview of their efficacy across key outcome domains, examining the impact of various intervention and study characteristics, and identifying the critical challenges and future directions for this burgeoning field.

## Methodological Quality and Publication Bias in the Field

A central challenge in interpreting the efficacy of any psychological intervention is the pervasive issue of methodological quality and publication bias within the primary research literature. The field of positive psychology is no exception, and a critical assessment of the available evidence reveals significant shortcomings that temper conclusions about the true effect size of multi-component positive psychology interventions (MPPIs). A stark illustration of this problem comes from the seminal meta-analysis by Bolier et al. (2013), which evaluated 39 RCTs on PPIs [[9,14]]. Using a six-item scale based on Cochrane guidelines, the authors found that only a single study achieved a high-quality rating, while the vast majority were classified as having medium (n=18) or low (n=20) methodological quality [[9,14]]. The specific weaknesses identified were common across the field: inadequate reporting of random sequence generation and allocation concealment, a lack of blinding of participants and personnel, and, most critically, the failure to conduct analyses on an intention-to-treat basis in just three of the 39 studies [[9,14]]. This pattern of poor methodology suggests that many published studies may overestimate the true effect of the interventions they evaluate.

Publication bias further complicates the interpretation of existing meta-analytic findings. When studies with null or negative results remain unpublished, the published literature becomes skewed towards statistically significant findings, leading to an inflated perception of an intervention's efficacy. The Bolier et al. (2013) meta-analysis explicitly identified this bias using both funnel plot asymmetry and Egger’s test, concluding that the observed effects for both psychological well-being and depression were likely exaggerated [[9,14]]. This conclusion is supported by a reanalysis of prior work, which demonstrated that unweighted averaging of effect sizes and the inclusion of non-RCTs had led to substantial overestimation of PPI effects in the original meta-analyses by Sin & Lyubomirsky (2009) and Bolier et al. (2013) [[10]]. After applying more rigorous statistical corrections, the reanalyses yielded much smaller or even non-significant effect sizes for both well-being and depression, suggesting that the field has been overly optimistic about its own success [[10]].

Recognizing these deep-seated methodological issues, there have been concerted efforts to improve the standards for evaluating and conducting research in this area. One notable development is the creation of the Methodological Quality Scale (MQS), a 12-item tool developed by expert methodologists from the Campbell Collaboration and validated for assessing the quality of intervention programs [[3]]. The MQS demonstrates strong psychometric properties, including substantial intercoder reliability (κ = 0.910) and adequate validity evidence, making it a valuable instrument for systematically auditing the quality of future studies [[3]]. The application of such tools to existing research could provide a clearer picture of how methodological rigor correlates with reported effect sizes. Indeed, the Hendriks et al. (2020) meta-analysis provides a compelling case study; their analysis revealed that excluding lower-quality studies substantially reduced the estimated effect sizes for subjective well-being, psychological well-being, and depression, while paradoxically strengthening the effect for stress [[1]]. This finding suggests that the apparent benefits of MPPIs may be disproportionately driven by a collection of underpowered, poorly designed studies, and that a more conservative estimate of their true impact requires a strict filtering of low-quality evidence.

In light of these findings, the current body of evidence supporting MPPIs must be interpreted with considerable caution. While the aggregate data points to a positive direction, the magnitude of the effect is highly sensitive to the inclusion criteria for study quality. The existence of robust, high-quality evidence is paramount for building confidence in the efficacy of MPPIs. Future research must prioritize adherence to stringent methodological standards, including transparent reporting of randomization and allocation concealment, blinding where possible, and, most importantly, intention-to-treat analysis. Without such a commitment to methodological rigor, the field risks perpetuating a cycle of over-interpretation and under-delivery, failing to provide a solid scientific foundation for the widespread implementation of these promising interventions.

## Quantitative Synthesis of Intervention Efficacy

To quantify the efficacy of multi-component positive psychology interventions (MPPIs), we turn to the findings of two of the most comprehensive meta-analyses in the field. The first, conducted by Hendriks et al. (2020), analyzed 50 RCTs published between 1998 and 2018 [[1,13]]. Their results provide a broad-brushstroke view of the average effect of MPPIs across a wide range of populations and contexts. They found small to moderate effects for subjective well-being (Hedges’ g = 0.34) and psychological well-being (g = 0.39), and small effects for depression (g = 0.32), anxiety (g = 0.35), and stress (g = 0.35) [[1,13]]. It is crucial to note, however, that these figures represent averages and mask significant variability. The analysis also revealed that the effect size for stress was heavily influenced by outliers, as removing them strongly increased the pooled effect [[1]]. Furthermore, the Hendriks et al. study conducted a sensitivity analysis by excluding lower-quality studies, which resulted in a considerable reduction in the effect sizes for all outcomes except stress [[1,13]]. This finding powerfully illustrates that the aggregate positive signal is largely dependent on including studies with questionable methodologies.

A second, more targeted meta-analysis by Martínez-Martínez et al. (2025) focused specifically on workplace settings, analyzing 21 studies with 16 measuring subjective well-being (SWB) and 18 measuring psychological well-being (PWB) [[2]]. This analysis yielded larger and more consistent effects compared to the broader Hendriks et al. review. The pooled Hedges' g for SWB was 0.50, and for PWB it was 0.46, both indicating moderate significant effects [[2]]. Performance outcomes also showed a significant improvement with a g of 0.42 [[2]]. However, this analysis also uncovered very high levels of heterogeneity (I² values ranging from 88% to 91%), suggesting that the effects of MPPIs in the workplace are highly variable and depend on numerous unmeasured contextual factors [[2]]. The large sample size and specific focus on a productive context make this study a powerful piece of evidence, yet the extreme heterogeneity cautions against generalizing its findings uncritically.

When considering the totality of the evidence, a clear pattern emerges. MPPIs demonstrate a modest but consistent ability to improve well-being. The effect on depression appears to be smaller and less robust, particularly when examined in high-quality studies or in comparative trials versus active treatments like Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT). A study by Lim & Tierney (2023) comparing PPIs to other active psychological treatments found non-significant small effect sizes for happiness (Hedge’s g = 0.20) and depression reduction (g = 0.15), further challenging the idea that PPIs are superior to other forms of therapy [[13]]. This suggests that while MPPIs can be a valuable tool for enhancing flourishing in the general population, they should not be viewed as a panacea for clinical-level depression. The distinction between enhancing well-being and treating psychopathology is critical. The evidence supports the former, but the latter claim requires far more rigorous testing. The reanalysis of meta-analytic data, which found that after correcting for biases the effects on depression shrank to near-zero (r = .04), provides a sobering perspective on the true magnitude of these interventions for alleviating distress [[10]].

## Moderators of Efficacy and Mechanisms of Change

Understanding the conditions under which multi-component positive psychology interventions (MPPIs) are most effective requires examining the moderators—intervention, participant, and study-level variables—that influence their outcomes. The evidence suggests that neither the number of components nor the theoretical model used is a decisive factor; rather, it is the specific content, delivery format, and context that appear to matter most. For instance, a study by Gander, Proyer, and Ruch (2016) tested various online PERMA-based interventions and found that a combined PERMA intervention did not yield better results than single-component interventions [[16]]. Instead, the interventions targeting *positive relationships* were particularly potent, showing the strongest and most sustained reductions in depressive symptoms over a six-month follow-up period [[16]]. This suggests that focusing on social connection may be a more powerful strategy than attempting to cover all five components at once. Similarly, a Dutch self-help program called 'This is your life', which targets six specific processes (positive emotion, use of strengths, optimism, self-compassion, resilience, and positive relations), found that positive relations and self-compassion were the most significant mediators of changes in mental well-being and psychological symptoms [[17]]. This aligns with the broader finding that social support is a cornerstone of psychological resilience.

Delivery format is another critical moderator. In the workplace setting, in-person delivery of MPPIs produced a large effect on subjective well-being (g = 1.52), whereas remote delivery yielded a smaller but still significant effect (g = 0.90) [[2]]. This highlights the importance of personal interaction for fostering engagement and connection, which are central to many PPIs. The role of human guidance also appears crucial. The 'This is your life' study included a condition with tailored email support and found that this minimal human assistance enhanced the intervention's efficacy [[5]]. In contrast, Problem Management Plus (PM+), a lay-health-worker delivered intervention in Kenya, succeeded precisely because it relied on trained community volunteers who could deliver a structured program in a culturally relevant context, demonstrating that even minimal formal training can be effective when paired with a strong manual [[4]]. The duration of the intervention is also a key factor, with longer-duration interventions (>8 weeks) showing significantly larger effects on depression in the meta-analysis by Bolier et al. (2013) [[14]].

Participant characteristics and cultural context also play a significant role. The Martínez-Martínez et al. (2025) meta-analysis found that MPPIs had a positive effect on well-being in Western cultural contexts (g = 0.36), but a detrimental effect in non-Western contexts (g = -0.87) [[2]]. This counterintuitive finding warrants careful interpretation. It could suggest that interventions developed within a Western framework are poorly suited for other cultures, or it may reflect differences in measurement, reporting, or the nature of the control groups used. This finding stands in contrast to others which report larger effect sizes for anxiety in non-Western countries compared to Western ones [[1,12]]. This discrepancy highlights the urgent need for more culturally-sensitive research. The Hendriks et al. (2020) review confirmed the "WEIRD" bias in the field, noting that only about 0.02% of PPI RCTs consider non-WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic) contexts, and fewer than 1% include LGBTQ+ individuals [[12]]. This lack of diversity severely limits the generalizability of the findings and raises questions about the universality of concepts like the PERMA model.

Finally, the underlying mechanisms of change are beginning to be unpacked. The 'This is your life' study provides a clear example of a working mechanism: the intervention successfully improved the six target processes (positive emotion, strengths use, etc.), and these improvements, in turn, mediated the observed gains in mental well-being and reductions in depression and anxiety [[17]]. This demonstrates a plausible causal pathway. Other research has shown that gratitude interventions increase happiness and reduce depression by altering cognitive patterns related to appreciation and perspective-taking [[11]]. Mindfulness-based interventions enhance well-being by improving emotional regulation and reducing rumination [[11]]. However, a major gap remains in our understanding of these mechanisms. Many studies fail to measure the putative mechanisms, making it difficult to know *how* the interventions work. Future research must move beyond simply documenting outcomes to investigating the specific psychological, neural, and behavioral pathways through which multi-component interventions exert their effects.

## Applications and Case Studies Across Diverse Contexts

Multi-component positive psychology interventions (MPPIs) have been applied across a wide spectrum of settings, from clinical and educational environments to corporate workplaces and international public health initiatives. These applications demonstrate the versatility of the approach, though the efficacy and implementation strategies vary significantly depending on the context. The workplace has proven to be a particularly fertile ground for MPPI implementation, with a meta-analysis by Martínez-Martínez et al. (2025) finding moderate and significant effects on subjective well-being (g = 0.50), psychological well-being (g = 0.46), and job performance (g = 0.42) [[2]]. Practical applications in this domain include using recognition programs, creating spaces for team engagement, implementing corporate social responsibility initiatives, and providing regular acknowledgment of achievements—all of which align with the principles of the PERMA model [[7]]. The finding that in-person delivery is more effective than remote delivery suggests that interventions incorporating team-building activities and face-to-face interactions are best suited for this context [[2]].

In the realm of international public health, MPPIs have been adapted for use in low-resource settings to address significant mental health burdens. A prime example is the Problem Management Plus (PM+) intervention, a brief, multicomponent program developed by the World Health Organization (WHO) and the University of New South Wales [[4]]. Designed for delivery by lay health workers, PM+ consists of five sessions focusing on behavioral activation, stress management, problem-solving, and social support. It was successfully trialed with 518 women exposed to adversity in peri-urban Nairobi, resulting in significant reductions in psychological distress, posttraumatic stress, and functional impairment [[4]]. This case study is crucial for several reasons. First, it showcases the potential for task-shifting, where mental health services are delivered by non-specialists, making interventions scalable and accessible in resource-poor regions. Second, it highlights the importance of cultural adaptation; the intervention was manualized and delivered by Community Health Volunteers who were part of the local community, which likely contributed to its high acceptability (over 85% completion rate) [[4]]. However, the study also illuminates significant barriers to sustainability, primarily the challenge of remunerating unpaid volunteers and navigating structural obstacles like stigma and conflicting labor schedules [[4]]. Integrating such programs into primary healthcare systems is seen as a viable path forward, but it requires political will and sustainable financing [[4]].

Educational settings represent another key area for application. A study by Forgeard et al. (2011) empirically tested the PERMA model among male students and successfully recovered four of the five factors, underscoring the model's relevance for adolescent well-being [[15]]. This provides a theoretical foundation for developing school-based interventions aimed at enhancing student flourishing. Such interventions could target school engagement, physical vitality, and social well-being, which includes constructs like social integration and contribution identified by Keyes [[11,15]]. The goal would be to move beyond merely preventing problems to actively promoting positive development. A systematic review protocol focusing on children aged 0–7 years signals a growing interest in early intervention, aiming to inform implementation in educational and related settings [[19]].

However, the challenges of scalability, sustainability, and cultural relevance persist as critical hurdles that must be overcome for MPPIs to realize their full potential as a global public health tool.

## Challenges, Limitations, and Future Research Directions

Despite the promising initial findings, the field of multi-component positive psychology interventions faces a series of significant challenges and limitations that must be addressed to ensure its continued growth and scientific credibility. The most profound limitation is the persistent "WEIRD" bias, a term referring to the over-reliance on Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic samples in psychological research [[12]]. The evidence is unequivocal: MPPI research is overwhelmingly dominated by studies conducted in WEIRD nations, with less than 1% of RCTs including LGBTQ+ individuals and only 0.02% considering non-WEIRD contexts [[12]]. This severely restricts the generalizability of the PERMA model and other Western-developed theories to the vast majority of the world's population. The finding from the Martínez-Martínez et al. (2025) meta-analysis that an MPPI had a detrimental effect in a non-Western context is a stark warning that interventions are not universally applicable without rigorous cultural adaptation and validation [[2]]. Future research must prioritize expanding samples to include diverse racial, ethnic, religious, and sexual minority groups, as well as individuals from low- and middle-income countries.

Methodological rigor remains a critical challenge. As previously detailed, the field has been plagued by low-quality studies, publication bias, and a tendency to inflate effect sizes [[9,10,14]]. To move forward, researchers must adopt and enforce higher standards of reporting, such as those outlined in the CONSORT statement, and employ more sophisticated statistical techniques to correct for known biases [[9]]. The development and use of standardized quality assessment tools like the Methodological Quality Scale (MQS) are essential steps in this direction [[3]]. Another limitation is the heavy reliance on self-report measures. While scales like the Mental Health Continuum-Short Form (MHC-SF) and the Beck Depression Inventory (BDI) are widely used, they are susceptible to response biases. Future studies should incorporate objective measures of well-being, such as physiological markers of stress (e.g., cortisol levels), behavioral observations, or ecological momentary assessments to capture real-time experiences.

Given these challenges, several key future research directions emerge. First, there is an urgent need for more long-term follow-up studies. While some research has demonstrated sustained effects up to six months [[16,17]], few studies track participants for a year or more. Understanding whether the benefits of MPPIs are transient or truly transformative is fundamental to their application in prevention and wellness promotion. Second, research must focus on cost-effectiveness. Before widespread implementation can be justified, policymakers and organizations need to know if the benefits of these interventions are worth the investment. Cost-benefit analyses comparing MPPIs to existing programs or standard care are sorely needed [[9]]. Third, the field must deepen its investigation into the mechanisms of change. Identifying the specific psychological pathways through which interventions work is crucial for refining and personalizing them. For example, if self-compassion is a key mediator for anxiety, then interventions could be designed to specifically target this process [[17]].

Finally, the future of MPPIs lies in innovation and accessibility. Digital platforms offer a powerful avenue for scaling up interventions to reach wider audiences at a lower cost. Online versions of PERMA-based interventions have already shown promise, demonstrating lasting effects on happiness [[16]]. Mobile apps and web-based courses could deliver multi-component programs directly to individuals in their homes, workplaces, or communities. This digital transformation must, however, be accompanied by careful research into the efficacy of these formats and their ability to maintain the crucial human element of connection and support. In conclusion, while the journey of positive psychology has been marked by impressive progress, the road ahead requires a more humble, rigorous, and globally-minded approach. By addressing the current limitations and embracing these future directions, the field can transition from a collection of promising ideas to a mature science capable of delivering robust and equitable benefits for human flourishing worldwide.

## Abstract

**Title:** Investigating the Influence of Artificial Intelligence on Business Value in the Digital Era of Strategy: A Systematic Literature Review

**Abstract:**
As the digital landscape evolves, Artificial Intelligence (AI) has transitioned from a peripheral technological asset to a central pillar of corporate strategy. This systematic literature review investigates the multifaceted influence of AI on business value, synthesizing findings from academic research, industry reports, and theoretical frameworks. The review categorizes the literature into three evolutionary waves—predictive, generative, and agentic—and analyzes them through the theoretical lenses of the Resource-Based View (RBV) and Dynamic Capabilities. Key findings indicate that while AI offers substantial potential for operational efficiency, decision-making enhancement, and business model innovation, value realization is often hindered by the "AI Paradox"—a gap between investment and measurable return. This paper identifies critical enablers of value, including AI ambidexterity, data governance, and strategic alignment, while highlighting significant challenges such as algorithmic bias, talent shortages, and integration complexities. The review concludes by proposing a research agenda focused on the emerging impact of Agentic AI and the necessity of responsible AI frameworks for sustainable competitive advantage.

## 2. Key Concepts and Definitions

### 2.1 Artificial Intelligence in Business
In the context of business strategy, AI is defined not merely as a technical discipline but as a set of capabilities that allow machines to perform tasks requiring human-like intelligence. This encompasses:
*   **Machine Learning (ML):** Algorithms that learn from data to make predictions or decisions without explicit programming [cite: 7, 8].
*   **Generative AI (GenAI):** A subset of AI focused on creating new content (text, code, images) and knowledge retrieval, representing a shift from analytical to creative capabilities [cite: 9, 10].
*   **Agentic AI:** The emerging "third wave" of AI systems capable of autonomous decision-making and multi-step task execution with minimal human intervention [cite: 11, 12].

### 2.2 Business Value and Digital Strategy
**Business Value** in the AI era extends beyond financial metrics (ROI, revenue) to include strategic value (market position, innovation capacity) and operational value (efficiency, risk mitigation) [cite: 13, 14].

**Digital Strategy** differs from traditional IT strategy by focusing on how technology shapes business strategy rather than merely supporting it. In this review, we adopt the perspective that AI is a core component of digital strategy, enabling "AI Ambidexterity"—the ability to simultaneously exploit existing competencies for efficiency while exploring new opportunities for innovation [cite: 15, 16].

## 3. Historical Development and Milestones

The literature identifies three distinct "waves" of AI evolution in business, each characterized by different strategic focuses and value propositions.

### 3.1 The First Wave: Predictive AI and Expert Systems (1980s–2010s)
The genesis of AI in business began with expert systems in the 1980s, designed to mimic human decision-making in narrow domains like credit scoring and logistics [cite: 2]. The explosion of the internet and "Big Data" in the 2000s and 2010s fueled the rise of Machine Learning. During this period, the strategic focus was on **Predictive Analytics**—using historical data to forecast trends, optimize supply chains, and segment customers [cite: 7, 12]. Value creation was primarily driven by "Scalable Efficiency" and automation of routine cognitive tasks [cite: 1].

### 3.2 The Second Wave: Generative AI (2022–Present)
The release of transformer architectures and Large Language Models (LLMs) marked the second wave. Generative AI shifted the paradigm from analysis to creation. Literature from this period highlights a democratization of AI, where non-technical employees could leverage AI for content creation, coding, and complex problem-solving [cite: 10, 17]. This wave introduced new business models based on "Insights-as-a-Service" and hyper-personalization, allowing firms to co-create value with customers in real-time [cite: 1, 18].

### 3.3 The Third Wave: Agentic AI (Emerging)
Current literature points to the emergence of **Agentic AI** as the next frontier. Unlike GenAI, which waits for prompts, Agentic AI systems can autonomously perceive their environment, reason, and execute complex workflows to achieve broad goals [cite: 11, 19]. This represents a shift from "human-in-the-loop" to "human-on-the-loop" or "human-out-of-the-loop" for certain processes, promising to automate entire business functions rather than just discrete tasks [cite: 12, 20].

## 4. Current State-of-the-Art Methods and Theoretical Frameworks

To understand how AI influences business value, scholars have increasingly relied on established strategic management theories, adapting them to the digital era.

### 4.1 The Resource-Based View (RBV) and AI
The Resource-Based View posits that firms achieve competitive advantage through resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN).
*   **AI as a Resource:** Early literature treated AI code or algorithms as the resource. However, recent studies argue that algorithms themselves are often commodities. The true source of advantage lies in **proprietary data** and the **organizational capability** to orchestrate AI [cite: 21, 22].
*   **Complementary Assets:** Research emphasizes that AI investment alone does not yield value. It requires complementary assets such as a "data-driven culture," specialized talent, and agile organizational structures [cite: 21, 23]. Without these, AI fails to meet the "Inimitable" criteria of RBV.

### 4.2 Dynamic Capabilities and AI Ambidexterity
The Dynamic Capabilities framework—defined as the ability to sense, seize, and transform—has become the dominant lens for analyzing AI strategy [cite: 24, 25].
*   **Sensing:** AI enhances a firm's ability to scan the environment (e.g., market trends, competitor moves) through real-time analytics [cite: 26, 27].
*   **Seizing:** AI enables rapid prototyping and decision-making, allowing firms to capitalize on opportunities faster than competitors [cite: 25].
*   **Transforming (AI Ambidexterity):** A critical concept in recent literature is **AI Ambidexterity**. This refers to the capacity to use AI for *exploitation* (optimizing current operations) and *exploration* (innovating new products) simultaneously [cite: 15, 28]. Firms that master this duality are found to be 50% more likely to become market leaders [cite: 16].

### 4.3 Value Realization Frameworks
Moving beyond theory, practitioners have developed frameworks to measure AI value.
*   **The Gartner/BCG Models:** These frameworks categorize value into "Return on Investment" (financial), "Return on Employee" (productivity/satisfaction), and "Return on the Future" (strategic positioning) [cite: 29, 30].
*   **The AI Business Value Canvas:** Inspired by the Business Model Canvas, this approach maps AI initiatives directly to customer value propositions and required data assets, ensuring strategic alignment before implementation [cite: 31].

## 5. Applications and Case Studies

The literature identifies four primary domains where AI drives business value [cite: 5, 32].

### 5.1 Operational Efficiency and Automation
This is the most mature area of AI application. Case studies in manufacturing and logistics demonstrate how AI-driven predictive maintenance and supply chain optimization reduce costs and downtime [cite: 33, 34]. "Automizers"—a business model typology identified in recent research—create value solely by reducing the marginal cost of complex tasks to near zero [cite: 18].

### 5.2 Enhanced Decision-Making
AI supports decision-making by reducing bounded rationality. In finance, AI algorithms analyze market signals to automate trading and risk assessment [cite: 2, 34]. In healthcare, diagnostic AI aids clinicians, improving accuracy and patient outcomes [cite: 33]. The literature notes a shift from "decision support" to "decision automation" in high-frequency, low-risk environments.

### 5.3 Customer Engagement and Personalization
AI enables "mass customization" and "value co-creation." Retailers use recommendation engines (e.g., Netflix, Amazon) to personalize experiences at scale [cite: 2, 8]. Generative AI has further advanced this by enabling hyper-personalized marketing content and dynamic pricing strategies that adapt in real-time to customer behavior [cite: 1, 34].

### 5.4 Innovation and New Business Models
AI is not just an operational tool but a source of business model innovation.
*   **Data Source Enablers:** Companies that create value by aggregating and selling unique datasets [cite: 18].
*   **Insights-as-a-Service:** Firms that sell the *output* of AI analysis rather than the software itself [cite: 18].
*   **Generative Innovation:** GenAI is accelerating R&D cycles in pharmaceuticals and material science, reducing the time-to-market for new products [cite: 7, 33].

## 6. Challenges and Open Problems

Despite the potential, the "AI Paradox" persists. The literature highlights several systemic challenges preventing value realization.

### 6.1 The Data Challenge: Quality and Governance
"Garbage in, garbage out" remains a primary hurdle. AI models require vast amounts of high-quality, unbiased data. Literature indicates that 45% of organizations cite data accuracy and bias as top barriers [cite: 35]. The lack of proprietary data is also a significant impediment to building defensible competitive advantages [cite: 35, 36].

### 6.2 The ROI Measurement Challenge
Measuring the business value of AI is notoriously difficult. Traditional financial metrics (NPV, IRR) often fail to capture intangible benefits like "strategic flexibility" or "employee empowerment" [cite: 13, 37]. Surveys show that while 92% of early adopters report positive ROI, only 64% actually measure it, often relying on anecdotal evidence [cite: 38]. There is a lack of standardized metrics for "Return on AI" [cite: 14, 39].

### 6.3 Talent and Organizational Resistance
The "skills gap" is a recurring theme. There is a shortage of not just technical AI talent, but "AI translators"—business leaders who understand AI's strategic potential [cite: 36, 40]. Furthermore, cultural resistance and the fear of job displacement hinder adoption, particularly with Agentic AI that promises to automate complex workflows [cite: 9, 41].

### 6.4 Ethical and Regulatory Risks
As AI becomes more autonomous, risks related to "black box" decision-making, bias, and privacy grow. The literature emphasizes that "Responsible AI" is no longer just a compliance issue but a strategic imperative. Trust is a prerequisite for value; without it, adoption stalls [cite: 3, 35].

## 7. Future Research Directions

Based on the gaps identified in the review, several avenues for future research emerge:

1.  **Agentic AI and Organizational Structure:** How will the rise of autonomous AI agents reshape organizational hierarchies? Research is needed on the "human-agent" workforce and the role of humans as "orchestrators" rather than operators [cite: 12, 20].
2.  **Longitudinal Value Studies:** Most current studies are cross-sectional or case-based. Longitudinal research is required to understand the long-term sustainability of AI-driven competitive advantage and whether AI resources eventually become commodities [cite: 5, 42].
3.  **AI Governance as a Dynamic Capability:** Future research should investigate how AI governance frameworks (ethics, safety, compliance) can be transformed from cost centers into sources of strategic differentiation and trust [cite: 15, 43].
4.  **Measuring the Intangible:** Developing robust econometric models to quantify the "soft" value of AI, such as improved innovation culture and decision velocity [cite: 14, 44].

## 8. Conclusion

This systematic literature review confirms that Artificial Intelligence has transcended its role as a technological enabler to become a fundamental driver of business value in the digital era. The integration of AI into business strategy is best understood through the lenses of **Dynamic Capabilities** and **Ambidexterity**, where firms leverage AI to simultaneously optimize current operations and pioneer new business models.

However, the path to value is nonlinear. The transition from Predictive to Generative and now Agentic AI offers exponential potential but introduces complex challenges regarding data governance, talent, and ethics. The "AI Paradox" suggests that technology deployment without strategic alignment and cultural transformation yields minimal return. Therefore, the future of AI in business lies not in the sophistication of algorithms alone, but in the **strategic orchestration** of human and machine intelligence to create resilient, adaptive, and innovative organizations.

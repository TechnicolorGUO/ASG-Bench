# Lineage Tracing Combined with Single-Cell Omics in Biology: A Comprehensive Survey

## 1 Introduction

Lineage tracing and single-cell omics have emerged as transformative methodologies in modern biological research, offering unprecedented insights into cellular development, function, and disease. Lineage tracing, which aims to map the ancestry and fate of individual cells, has been pivotal in elucidating the complex processes of embryogenesis, organ development, and disease progression [1]. Traditionally, lineage tracing relied on genetic labeling and experimental manipulation in model organisms, but recent advances have expanded its scope to include high-throughput, high-resolution techniques that enable the reconstruction of detailed lineage trees [2; 3]. These methods, such as CRISPR-based lineage barcoding, allow for the precise tracking of cell division and differentiation events, providing a dynamic view of cellular lineage relationships [3]. However, lineage tracing alone is insufficient to fully capture the heterogeneity and complexity of cellular states, especially in tissues where cellular identities and functions vary significantly across spatial and temporal scales [2].

The emergence of single-cell omics technologies, particularly single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), has revolutionized the study of cellular heterogeneity by enabling the profiling of gene expression at the individual cell level [2; 4]. These technologies have revealed the existence of rare cell populations, transient states, and previously uncharacterized cell types, highlighting the limitations of bulk sequencing approaches [4]. However, the integration of single-cell omics data with lineage information is essential for understanding the functional implications of lineage relationships and for inferring the regulatory mechanisms that govern cellular differentiation and fate decisions [2; 2]. This integration is not trivial, as it requires sophisticated computational frameworks to align lineage trees with gene expression profiles, epigenetic landscapes, and other omics data [2].

The combination of lineage tracing and single-cell omics has led to the development of novel methodologies that bridge the gap between static lineage reconstructions and dynamic cellular states. For instance, trajectory inference algorithms and pseudotime analysis have been instrumental in reconstructing cell differentiation paths and identifying key regulatory nodes in developmental processes [2; 4]. Additionally, the integration of spatial transcriptomics with lineage data has enabled the study of cellular dynamics within the context of tissue architecture, providing a more comprehensive understanding of how cellular behavior is influenced by spatial organization [2; 4]. Despite these advances, challenges remain in accurately reconstructing lineage relationships from high-dimensional data, handling noise and missing information, and developing scalable algorithms that can accommodate the complexity of multi-omics datasets [2; 2].

As the field continues to evolve, the integration of lineage tracing with single-cell omics is expected to play a central role in advancing our understanding of cellular processes in health and disease. Emerging technologies, such as spatial omics and multi-omics integration, are poised to further enhance the resolution and biological relevance of lineage analyses [2; 4]. Moreover, the application of machine learning and deep learning techniques to lineage and single-cell data is opening new avenues for interpreting complex biological systems and predicting cellular responses to perturbations [4; 5]. These developments underscore the growing importance of interdisciplinary approaches in modern biological research, where the convergence of experimental and computational methods is essential for addressing the complexity of cellular dynamics.

## 2 Historical and Theoretical Foundations

### 2.1 Early Lineage Tracing Techniques

The development of lineage tracing techniques has been a cornerstone in understanding cellular differentiation, development, and disease progression. Early methods focused on genetic labeling and experimental lineage mapping in model organisms, laying the foundation for modern lineage analysis. These techniques provided critical insights into how cells give rise to different tissues and organs, while also revealing the complexity of cellular decision-making processes.

One of the earliest approaches to lineage tracing involved the use of genetic labeling strategies, such as lineage-specific reporter genes and transgenic markers. These methods allowed researchers to visualize and track the fate of specific cell populations in vivo. For instance, the use of lacZ and GFP reporters enabled the identification of cell lineages in *Drosophila* and *C. elegans*, offering a direct way to map developmental lineages [2]. However, these methods were often limited in their ability to track individual cells at high resolution or to capture dynamic lineage changes over time [2].

Experimental lineage mapping in model organisms such as *Drosophila* and *C. elegans* further advanced the field by employing techniques like cell ablation, clonal analysis, and time-lapse imaging. These experiments provided a detailed understanding of how cells divide, differentiate, and contribute to tissue formation. For example, clonal analysis in *Drosophila* allowed researchers to trace the lineage of individual cells and determine their contributions to different tissues [2]. Time-lapse imaging, on the other hand, enabled the visualization of cell movements and interactions in real time, offering insights into the dynamic nature of lineage specification [2].

Classical embryology also played a crucial role in establishing the conceptual framework for lineage tracing. By identifying key developmental stages and cell commitment events, embryologists laid the groundwork for understanding how cells progress through different developmental states. The concept of "cell fate" was refined through these studies, highlighting the importance of both intrinsic and extrinsic factors in determining cell identity [2].

Despite their contributions, early lineage tracing methods had significant limitations. The inability to track individual cells at high resolution and the lack of tools to capture dynamic lineage changes were major challenges. Additionally, these techniques often required invasive procedures, limiting their applicability to certain experimental contexts [6].

The emergence of more sophisticated lineage tracing techniques, such as lineage barcoding and fluorescent labeling, began to address some of these limitations. These methods enabled higher-resolution tracking of cell lineages and provided new opportunities for studying cellular heterogeneity and plasticity. For example, fluorescent labeling strategies, including lineage tracing in model organisms like *C. elegans* and *Drosophila*, allowed for the visualization of cell movements and lineage relationships [1].

These early techniques, while limited in scope, provided essential insights into the mechanisms of cell fate determination and lineage specification. They set the stage for the development of more advanced lineage tracing methods that would later integrate with single-cell omics technologies. The transition from these classical approaches to modern lineage tracing methods marks a significant shift in the field, driven by the need for higher resolution, greater accuracy, and the ability to capture dynamic cellular processes. As the field continues to evolve, the legacy of these early techniques remains evident in the sophisticated methodologies that now define lineage tracing research.

### 2.2 The Emergence of Single-Cell Omics Technologies

The emergence of single-cell omics technologies has marked a transformative shift in the field of biology, enabling the study of cellular heterogeneity and dynamic processes at an unprecedented level of resolution. Prior to the advent of these technologies, conventional bulk omics approaches provided averaged measurements across populations of cells, obscuring the nuanced differences between individual cells. The development of single-cell omics has thus addressed this critical limitation, allowing researchers to interrogate the molecular profiles of individual cells, thereby revealing the intricate landscapes of cellular diversity and function. This shift has been driven by the convergence of advancements in microfluidics, imaging, and computational biology, with each breakthrough contributing to the refinement of single-cell methodologies.

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) emerged as a pivotal technology in this revolution, enabling the quantification of gene expression at the single-cell level. Early scRNA-seq methods, such as microfluidic droplet-based platforms and plate-based approaches, laid the foundation for high-throughput analysis [2]. These methods allowed for the simultaneous profiling of thousands of cells, providing insights into rare cell populations and transient cellular states [2]. However, early scRNA-seq faced challenges related to sensitivity, throughput, and cost, which have since been addressed by subsequent innovations such as the 10x Genomics platform and droplet-based single-cell sequencing [2]. The ability to capture gene expression at single-cell resolution has since been complemented by advances in spatial transcriptomics, which integrates gene expression data with spatial information, allowing for the study of cellular organization within tissues [2]. This spatial context is critical for understanding cell-cell interactions and tissue architecture, as demonstrated by studies utilizing spatial transcriptomics to map gene expression in complex tissues [2].

Beyond transcriptomics, the development of single-cell epigenomics has expanded the scope of single-cell omics to include the regulation of gene expression. Techniques such as single-cell ATAC-seq (assay for transposase-accessible chromatin using sequencing) and single-cell methylome profiling have enabled the interrogation of chromatin accessibility and DNA methylation patterns at the single-cell level [2]. These technologies have revealed the heterogeneity of regulatory landscapes across cell types and have provided insights into the mechanisms of cell fate decisions. Similarly, the emergence of single-cell proteomics and metabolomics has extended the analytical power of single-cell omics to include protein and metabolic profiles, offering a more comprehensive view of cellular function [2].

The technical advancements in single-cell omics have been paralleled by the development of computational tools for data analysis. These tools are essential for handling the high dimensionality and complexity of single-cell data, enabling the identification of cell types, the reconstruction of developmental trajectories, and the inference of gene regulatory networks [2; 2; 2]. The integration of these technologies with lineage tracing has further enhanced our ability to study cellular dynamics, providing a powerful framework for understanding the molecular and functional diversity of cells in development and disease [2; 2; 1]. As single-cell omics continues to evolve, the challenges of data integration, computational scalability, and biological interpretation remain central to the field, offering fertile ground for future innovation and discovery.

### 2.3 Theoretical Foundations of Cellular Heterogeneity and Lineage Dynamics

The theoretical foundations of cellular heterogeneity and lineage dynamics are critical for interpreting the complex and high-dimensional data generated by single-cell omics technologies. These frameworks provide the mathematical and computational tools necessary to model the stochastic nature of cell fate decisions, infer developmental trajectories, and understand the emergent properties of cellular populations. At the heart of these models lies the challenge of reconciling the discrete, probabilistic nature of cellular decisions with the continuous, deterministic nature of traditional developmental biology. This subsection explores the conceptual and mathematical models that underpin the analysis of single-cell data, with a focus on their role in interpreting cellular heterogeneity and lineage dynamics.

Stochastic models of cell fate decisions have been instrumental in capturing the probabilistic nature of lineage progression. Markov chain models, for instance, are widely used to represent the transitions between cell states as a series of probabilistic events. These models provide a framework for understanding the variability in cell fate outcomes and the influence of intrinsic and extrinsic factors on cellular decisions [2]. Similarly, probabilistic graphical models have been employed to capture the dependencies between gene expression states and lineage commitment, enabling the inference of regulatory networks that govern cell differentiation [2]. The integration of these models with single-cell data allows for the reconstruction of lineage trees and the identification of key regulatory checkpoints in development.

Trajectory inference algorithms represent another critical class of models used to analyze single-cell data. These methods, such as pseudotime analysis and RNA velocity, aim to reconstruct the temporal progression of cells through a developmental process. Pseudotime analysis, for example, orders cells along a developmental trajectory based on their gene expression profiles, providing insights into the sequence of gene expression changes that accompany cell fate decisions [2]. RNA velocity, on the other hand, leverages the dynamics of RNA splicing to infer the direction of cellular transitions, offering a more mechanistic view of lineage progression [2]. These approaches have been particularly valuable in studying complex developmental processes where the temporal ordering of events is not directly observable.

In addition to stochastic and trajectory-based models, mathematical frameworks for studying cell population dynamics have also played a crucial role in understanding lineage dynamics. Population balance models and continuum approximations are used to describe the behavior of large cell populations, accounting for the heterogeneity and stochasticity inherent in biological systems [2]. These models provide a macroscopic perspective on lineage dynamics, enabling the study of emergent properties such as cell lineage diversification and the maintenance of cellular homeostasis.

The integration of biological knowledge into computational models has further enhanced the interpretability of single-cell data. Gene regulatory networks and signaling pathways are often incorporated into computational frameworks to inform lineage reconstruction and to identify key regulatory elements that drive cell fate decisions [6]. This approach not only improves the accuracy of lineage inference but also provides a mechanistic understanding of the underlying biological processes.

Looking ahead, the development of more sophisticated models that integrate multiple layers of biological information, such as epigenetic states and spatial organization, is expected to provide deeper insights into cellular heterogeneity and lineage dynamics. The continued refinement of these theoretical frameworks will be essential for fully realizing the potential of lineage tracing combined with single-cell omics in biological research.

## 3 Methodologies and Experimental Approaches

### 3.1 Experimental Techniques for Lineage Tracing

The experimental techniques for lineage tracing are foundational to understanding cellular development, differentiation, and fate decisions. These methodologies allow researchers to label, track, and reconstruct the lineage of individual cells across generations, providing insights into the dynamic processes that govern tissue formation, organogenesis, and disease progression. Traditional approaches such as genetic labeling and fluorescent reporters have long been used to mark and follow cells, while recent advancements in CRISPR-based lineage barcoding have revolutionized the field by enabling high-resolution, scalable lineage reconstruction [12]. These techniques differ in their labeling strategies, resolution, and applicability, and their selection depends on the experimental context and research objectives.

Genetic labeling strategies, such as lineage-specific reporter genes and mosaic analysis, have been instrumental in tracking cell fate in model organisms like *Drosophila* and *C. elegans* [12]. These methods often involve the use of transgenic markers that are activated in specific cell types or developmental stages, allowing for the identification of cell origins and fates. While effective in controlled experimental settings, genetic labeling can suffer from limited resolution and the inability to track dynamic lineage changes in complex tissues. Fluorescent labeling, on the other hand, uses reporter systems that emit light in response to specific stimuli, enabling real-time visualization of cell lineages in living organisms [12]. Techniques like lineage tracing in *C. elegans* and *Drosophila* have been particularly influential, as they combine fluorescent markers with time-lapse imaging to map developmental lineages with high precision.

CRISPR-based lineage barcoding has emerged as a powerful alternative to traditional methods, allowing for the creation of unique genetic barcodes that are inherited by daughter cells. This approach enables the reconstruction of lineage trees with high resolution and accuracy, making it ideal for studying complex biological systems such as tumors or stem cell differentiation [12]. By introducing targeted mutations into the genome, CRISPR-based barcoding provides a scalable and robust framework for lineage reconstruction. However, challenges remain, such as the potential for off-target effects and the need for high-quality sequencing to decode the barcodes.

Recent developments in optogenetic and photoactivatable tools have further expanded the possibilities of lineage tracing by allowing for precise spatial and temporal control of cell labeling [12]. These techniques enable the activation or inhibition of specific cells in response to light, offering unprecedented control over lineage mapping in vivo. The integration of these technologies with single-cell omics platforms is expected to provide a more comprehensive understanding of cellular dynamics and lineage relationships.

As lineage tracing techniques continue to evolve, the integration of high-throughput data acquisition, computational modeling, and multi-omics data is becoming increasingly critical. These advancements are paving the way for more accurate and comprehensive lineage analysis, with the potential to transform our understanding of developmental biology, cancer progression, and regenerative medicine. The ongoing refinement of these experimental techniques will be essential in addressing the complex challenges of cellular heterogeneity and dynamic lineage changes.

### 3.2 Single-Cell Omics Technologies and Their Applications

Single-cell omics technologies have revolutionized our understanding of cellular heterogeneity and functional states by enabling the analysis of biological systems at an unprecedented resolution. Among these, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has emerged as a cornerstone technology, allowing the profiling of gene expression at the individual cell level [2]. This approach has been instrumental in identifying rare cell types, transient states, and developmental trajectories, with applications spanning developmental biology, immunology, and cancer research [2]. However, scRNA-seq primarily captures the transcriptomic landscape and is limited in its ability to resolve the epigenetic and regulatory mechanisms that govern cellular function. To address this, single-cell ATAC-seq (scATAC-seq) has been developed to interrogate chromatin accessibility, providing insights into the regulatory landscapes of individual cells [2]. By mapping open chromatin regions, scATAC-seq complements scRNA-seq by revealing the cis-regulatory elements that control gene expression, thus offering a more comprehensive view of cellular states [2]. Despite its power, scATAC-seq faces challenges such as lower sensitivity and the need for larger cell numbers compared to scRNA-seq, which limits its applicability in certain experimental contexts [2]. 

Spatial transcriptomics represents another major advancement in the field, combining gene expression profiling with spatial localization to study cellular dynamics within the tissue context [6]. This technology has enabled the mapping of gene expression patterns in three-dimensional tissue environments, revealing the spatial organization of cell populations and their interactions. Techniques such as Visium and spatially resolved transcriptomics have been particularly useful in studying tumor microenvironments and developmental processes [1]. However, spatial transcriptomics is still limited by spatial resolution and the complexity of data integration, requiring sophisticated computational tools to interpret the spatially structured data [2]. 

The integration of spatial and single-cell transcriptomics has further expanded the scope of single-cell omics, enabling multi-scale analysis of cellular dynamics. This approach allows researchers to study the relationship between cellular function and spatial context, which is critical for understanding tissue architecture and disease mechanisms [1]. For instance, the combination of spatial transcriptomics with lineage tracing has been used to map the developmental trajectories of cells within their native tissue environments [12]. 

Overall, the development of single-cell omics technologies has provided powerful tools for capturing the complexity of biological systems. While each approach has its strengths and limitations, their integration is essential for a holistic understanding of cellular heterogeneity and function. As these technologies continue to evolve, they will play an increasingly important role in advancing our knowledge of cellular processes and their implications in health and disease.

### 3.3 Data Acquisition, Preprocessing, and Integration Pipelines

Data acquisition, preprocessing, and integration pipelines are foundational to the success of lineage tracing combined with single-cell omics studies. These workflows involve the meticulous collection of single-cell data, the rigorous processing to ensure data quality, and the integration of lineage information with omics data to derive meaningful biological insights. The challenges in these steps are multifaceted, ranging from technical limitations in sample preparation to computational complexities in data integration. Addressing these challenges requires a combination of best practices, advanced methodologies, and a deep understanding of the biological context.

The acquisition of single-cell data typically begins with sample preparation, which involves isolating individual cells from tissues or cultures and preparing them for sequencing or imaging. Techniques such as fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) or microfluidic platforms are commonly used to achieve high-throughput cell isolation [2]. Following this, library construction is performed to generate sequencing-ready samples. This step involves capturing RNA, DNA, or other molecular profiles and converting them into libraries suitable for high-throughput sequencing [2]. The quality of the data acquired at this stage is critical, as technical noise and biases can significantly impact downstream analysis.

Once the data is acquired, preprocessing is essential to ensure data reliability and consistency. This involves quality control (QC) steps such as filtering out low-quality cells, normalizing expression values, and correcting for batch effects [2]. Advanced methods like scFP and DropLasso have been proposed to address sparsity and dropout events in single-cell RNA-seq data [2]. These preprocessing steps are crucial for reducing technical variability and improving the accuracy of downstream analyses.

Integration of lineage and omics data presents additional challenges, as the data types often differ in structure, scale, and resolution. Methods such as CAVACHON and scICML have been developed to integrate lineage information with single-cell transcriptomic and epigenomic data, leveraging probabilistic and co-clustering-based approaches [2]. The use of variational autoencoders and deep learning has also shown promise in creating unified representations for diverse omics data, enabling joint analysis and interpretation [6]. However, the integration of such data remains a complex task, requiring careful consideration of the biological context and the limitations of existing methods.

Future directions in this area include the development of more robust and scalable integration strategies, as well as the application of machine learning and deep learning to enhance the accuracy and interpretability of lineage-omics data. As single-cell technologies continue to evolve, the demand for efficient and reliable data acquisition, preprocessing, and integration pipelines will only increase, driving the need for continued innovation and methodological advancement in this field [1; 2].

### 3.4 Computational and Algorithmic Challenges in Lineage and Single-Cell Data Analysis

The analysis of lineage-traced single-cell data presents a unique set of computational and algorithmic challenges that require sophisticated methodologies to address the complexity of biological systems. One of the central issues is trajectory inference, which aims to reconstruct the developmental paths of cells from high-dimensional gene expression data. Traditional approaches, such as pseudotime analysis [2], often rely on assumptions about the underlying biological processes, which can lead to misinterpretations when the data do not conform to these assumptions. More recent methods, such as those based on optimal transport [2], attempt to capture the continuous and nonlinear dynamics of cellular differentiation but require significant computational resources and careful parameter tuning. Additionally, the integration of lineage information with gene expression data adds another layer of complexity, as it necessitates the development of joint models that can reconcile the discrete nature of lineage trees with the continuous nature of gene expression profiles [2]. This integration is further complicated by the inherent noise and sparsity of single-cell data, which can obscure true biological signals and lead to unreliable inferences.

Another critical challenge is lineage reconstruction, which involves inferring the relationships between cells based on their genetic or epigenetic profiles. While tree-based methods [2] and graph-based approaches have been proposed, these methods often struggle with large-scale datasets and may fail to capture the full complexity of lineage dynamics, especially in systems with high cellular heterogeneity. Moreover, the presence of cell plasticity and lineage reprogramming introduces additional uncertainties, as cells can transition between states, making it difficult to establish stable lineage definitions. To address these challenges, machine learning and deep learning techniques have been increasingly employed for lineage prediction and trajectory inference [2]. These approaches offer the potential to model complex, nonlinear relationships and handle high-dimensional data more effectively than traditional methods. However, they also introduce new challenges, such as the need for large, annotated datasets and the risk of overfitting, which can limit their generalizability.

Furthermore, the integration of multi-omics data—such as transcriptomic, epigenomic, and proteomic profiles—requires the development of robust computational frameworks that can handle the heterogeneity and scale of these data types [6]. As discussed in the previous subsection, the integration of lineage information with single-cell omics data is a complex task that demands careful consideration of both technical and biological factors. The challenges outlined here build upon the foundational steps of data acquisition and integration, highlighting the next critical layer in the analysis pipeline. Despite these challenges, the field is rapidly evolving, with emerging trends pointing toward the development of more scalable, interpretable, and integrative algorithms. These advancements are crucial for unlocking the full potential of lineage-traced single-cell data and enabling a deeper understanding of cellular development and disease. As the field continues to grow, addressing these computational and algorithmic challenges will be essential for driving meaningful biological insights and improving the accuracy and reliability of single-cell data analysis.

## 4 Computational and Algorithmic Frameworks

### 4.1 Lineage Reconstruction Algorithms

Computational methods for lineage reconstruction have become essential in deciphering the complex relationships between cells in single-cell data, enabling the inference of developmental trajectories and clonal lineages. These algorithms are particularly critical in systems where cellular dynamics are intricate, such as in embryonic development or tumor evolution. Traditional approaches to lineage reconstruction often relied on phylogenetic trees, which model lineage relationships as branching structures, but modern methods leverage the high-dimensional nature of single-cell data to improve accuracy and scalability. Phylogenetic methods such as maximum likelihood and Bayesian inference have been adapted for single-cell data, allowing for the reconstruction of lineage trees based on mutation or expression profiles [13]. However, these methods often assume a static, tree-like structure, which may not capture the full complexity of lineage dynamics in heterogeneous cell populations. To address this, graph-based approaches have emerged as a flexible alternative, modeling lineage relationships as networks that can accommodate branching, merging, and reprogramming events. Techniques such as the graph neural network-based CellTracker [14] and the trajectory inference method scTree [15] leverage graph structures to capture the dynamic nature of cell lineage, allowing for more accurate reconstruction of complex developmental paths. Bayesian and probabilistic lineage inference methods offer another approach by explicitly accounting for uncertainty in lineage assignments, which is particularly important in noisy or sparse single-cell data. Methods such as PhyloSub [16] and the variational Bayesian framework in scTree [15] use probabilistic models to infer lineage trees while incorporating prior knowledge about mutation frequencies or gene expression patterns. These methods provide a more robust framework for lineage reconstruction by quantifying uncertainty and allowing for the integration of multiple data modalities. Deep learning-based lineage inference has also gained traction, with approaches such as the neural network-based lineage reconstruction in the study by [17] and the generative models used in [18] demonstrating promising results. These methods leverage the power of deep neural networks to learn complex patterns in single-cell data, enabling the prediction of lineage relationships from transcriptomic or epigenomic profiles. However, deep learning approaches often require large amounts of labeled data for training and may lack interpretability, which can be a limitation in biological contexts where understanding the underlying mechanisms is crucial. Another important trend in lineage reconstruction is the integration of spatial and temporal data, which can significantly improve the accuracy of lineage inference. Methods such as the spatial trajectory inference framework in [19] and the deep learning approach in [20] use spatial information to model the positional dynamics of cells, providing a more comprehensive view of lineage relationships. The combination of spatial and temporal data allows for the reconstruction of lineage trees that are consistent with the physical organization of cells within tissues, which is particularly valuable in studies of organogenesis and tumor progression. Despite these advancements, lineage reconstruction remains a challenging task, with ongoing efforts focused on improving the accuracy, scalability, and interpretability of existing methods. Future directions include the development of hybrid models that combine graph-based, probabilistic, and deep learning approaches, as well as the integration of multi-omics data to gain a more holistic understanding of lineage dynamics. The continued refinement of lineage reconstruction algorithms will be crucial for unlocking the full potential of single-cell omics in biological research.

### 4.2 Trajectory Inference and Pseudotime Analysis

Trajectory inference and pseudotime analysis are fundamental to understanding the dynamic processes of cellular development, differentiation, and disease progression. These methods aim to reconstruct the temporal order of cellular states from static single-cell data, enabling the identification of developmental paths, lineage relationships, and key regulatory transitions. Pseudotime, a continuous variable representing the progression of a cell along a trajectory, allows for the ordering of cells in a manner that reflects their biological state, even in the absence of explicit temporal information. This has become a cornerstone in the analysis of complex cellular dynamics, particularly in systems where temporal resolution is limited or absent.

Graph-based trajectory inference methods, such as those using UMAP or t-SNE, have been widely adopted for their ability to preserve local and global structure in high-dimensional single-cell data [2; 2]. These approaches typically construct a graph where nodes represent cells and edges represent similarity, allowing for the identification of branching or linear developmental paths. However, such methods often lack the ability to explicitly model the directionality of developmental processes. To address this, methods like Pseudotime [2], Monocle [2], and SLICER [2] have been developed to assign pseudotime values that reflect the progression of cells along inferred trajectories. These methods leverage gene expression patterns to determine the relative position of each cell within a trajectory, enabling the reconstruction of cell differentiation paths and the identification of key regulatory genes.

Dynamic models, such as those based on ordinary differential equations (ODEs) or stochastic processes, offer a more mechanistic approach to trajectory inference by explicitly modeling the temporal evolution of cellular states [6; 1]. These models can capture complex developmental dynamics and are particularly useful for systems with well-characterized regulatory networks. However, they often require additional assumptions about the underlying biological processes, limiting their applicability in less well-understood systems. In contrast, machine learning approaches, including deep learning and probabilistic models, have gained traction for their ability to infer trajectories from high-dimensional data without strong parametric assumptions [2; 1]. These methods are particularly effective in capturing non-linear relationships and can be trained on large-scale single-cell datasets to infer trajectory structures and pseudotime orderings.

Despite the progress in trajectory inference and pseudotime analysis, several challenges remain. These include the handling of noisy or incomplete data, the accurate identification of branching points in complex developmental processes, and the integration of multi-omics data to enhance biological interpretability. Emerging trends, such as the use of optimal transport and generative models, are showing promise in addressing these challenges by enabling more robust and interpretable trajectory inference [12; 21]. As the field continues to evolve, the integration of trajectory inference with other computational frameworks, such as lineage reconstruction and multi-omics integration, will be essential for gaining a comprehensive understanding of cellular dynamics.

### 4.3 Multi-omics Data Integration for Lineage Analysis

Multi-omics data integration is essential for lineage analysis, as it enables the synthesis of diverse molecular profiles—such as transcriptomic, epigenomic, and proteomic data—with lineage information to reveal the complex regulatory networks governing cell fate decisions. This integration provides a more holistic view of cellular dynamics, allowing researchers to uncover the relationships between molecular states and lineage progression. Various computational frameworks have been developed to address the challenges of integrating these data types, each with distinct strengths, limitations, and trade-offs. A key challenge is the heterogeneity and high dimensionality of multi-omics data, which necessitate sophisticated methods for alignment, normalization, and joint analysis.

One prominent approach is the use of variational autoencoders (VAEs) and deep learning models to jointly analyze lineage and omics data. For instance, OmiVAE [2] employs a VAE to extract low-dimensional representations from multi-omics data, enabling accurate classification of cell types and lineage predictions. Similarly, the OmiEmbed framework [2] uses a multi-task deep learning strategy to integrate diverse omics data, demonstrating superior performance in tasks such as tumour classification and survival prediction. These models leverage the power of deep learning to capture complex, non-linear relationships between omics features and lineage information, yet they often require substantial computational resources and careful tuning of hyperparameters.

Another class of methods focuses on probabilistic models that explicitly incorporate lineage information into the analysis. For example, the variational mixtures of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) approach [2] models gene expression dynamics by combining lineage data with temporal information, enabling the reconstruction of latent cell states and future gene expression profiles. This method provides a principled way to infer lineage progression while accounting for the variability inherent in single-cell data. However, the complexity of these models can make them challenging to train and interpret.

In addition to these, graph-based and network-based approaches have gained traction in integrating lineage and omics data. Techniques such as the graph neural networks (GNNs) in GrID-Net [2] are designed to infer causal relationships between genomic loci and gene expression, offering insights into the regulatory mechanisms underlying lineage decisions. These methods benefit from their ability to model dependencies and interactions but require extensive feature engineering and domain expertise.

The integration of spatial and temporal information has also emerged as a critical direction. Tools like SpaDiT [2] leverage diffusion models to predict spatial gene expression patterns from scRNA-seq data, enhancing the interpretability of lineage relationships in a spatial context. This approach not only improves the accuracy of gene expression prediction but also provides a means to study the spatial organization of cells during lineage progression.

Despite these advancements, challenges remain. The integration of multi-omics data often involves dealing with missing values, batch effects, and varying data scales. Future directions should focus on developing more robust and scalable methods, as well as on improving the interpretability of models to facilitate biological insights. As the field evolves, the integration of lineage information with multi-omics data will continue to play a pivotal role in uncovering the molecular mechanisms driving cellular development and disease.

### 4.4 Machine Learning and Deep Learning Techniques for Cell Classification and Lineage Prediction

Machine learning and deep learning techniques have become indispensable tools for cell classification and lineage prediction in the context of lineage tracing combined with single-cell omics. These methods enable the identification of cell types, the inference of developmental trajectories, and the prediction of lineage relationships by leveraging the complex and high-dimensional data generated by single-cell technologies. Supervised learning approaches, such as random forests, support vector machines, and deep neural networks, have been widely applied to classify cells based on labeled single-cell data. These models benefit from their ability to learn complex decision boundaries and capture non-linear relationships between gene expression profiles and cell identities [1]. However, the reliance on labeled data remains a limitation, as manual annotation is labor-intensive and subjective.

Unsupervised and semi-supervised learning methods have emerged as promising alternatives to overcome the challenges of data scarcity and labeling bias. Techniques such as autoencoders, generative adversarial networks (GANs), and clustering algorithms like t-SNE and UMAP are employed to identify cell clusters and lineage relationships without prior labeling [22; 23]. These methods are particularly effective in exploratory analysis, where the biological context may not be fully known. However, their performance can be sensitive to parameter choices and the quality of the input data, necessitating careful validation and interpretation.

Interpretable machine learning models have gained attention for their ability to provide insights into the features and patterns driving lineage decisions. Deep learning-based lineage inference methods have also shown significant potential. Graph neural networks (GNNs) and transformer models have been used to track cell lineages and reconstruct complex cell division events [25; 1]. Despite these advances, challenges remain in the application of machine learning and deep learning to lineage tracing. The high dimensionality and noise in single-cell data, along with the computational complexity of large-scale models, require further innovation in algorithm design and optimization. Additionally, the integration of lineage information with other omics data, such as transcriptomic, epigenomic, and proteomic profiles, presents new opportunities and challenges for multi-omics analysis. ### 4.5 Computational Challenges and Optimization Strategies

Computational challenges in analyzing lineage-traced single-cell data are multifaceted, encompassing scalability, noise handling, and algorithmic efficiency. As single-cell datasets grow in size and complexity, traditional methods often struggle to process the data efficiently. Scalability remains a central issue, particularly when dealing with large-scale lineage reconstruction and trajectory inference tasks, which require handling high-dimensional data and complex dependencies. For instance, methods based on Bayesian inference and graph-based models can become computationally infeasible as the number of cells increases [26]. To address this, scalable algorithms that leverage distributed computing, dimensionality reduction, and sparse data representations have been developed. Techniques like those proposed in the BIDIFAC framework [27] allow for efficient integration of multi-omics data while maintaining biological interpretability. These approaches often involve optimizing the computational complexity of the underlying models to ensure that they can be applied to large datasets without excessive runtime or memory overhead.

Noise and artifacts in lineage and omics data present another significant challenge. Single-cell data are inherently noisy due to technical variability, dropout events, and measurement errors. This noise can severely impact the accuracy of lineage reconstruction and trajectory inference. Several studies have proposed methods for noise handling, including imputation strategies, batch correction techniques, and robust statistical methods. For example, the BayesHammer tool [26] employs Bayesian clustering for error correction, while methods like those in the scOTGM framework [28] aim to align and integrate data across different modalities. These approaches are crucial for ensuring that the downstream analysis of lineage-traced single-cell data is reliable and robust.

Algorithmic efficiency is also a critical concern, particularly in trajectory and lineage inference. Many existing algorithms suffer from high computational costs, making them impractical for large datasets. To optimize performance, researchers have explored gradient-based optimization, parallel computing, and approximation methods. For example, TrajectoryNet [29] leverages continuous normalizing flows to model dynamic cellular processes, offering improved efficiency compared to traditional optimal transport methods. Additionally, the development of specialized hardware and software frameworks, such as those in the CellAgent framework [30], enables more efficient and automated analysis of single-cell data.

Benchmarking and evaluation of lineage and trajectory inference methods remain essential for ensuring reproducibility and performance comparison. Standardized datasets, metrics, and validation strategies are necessary to assess the accuracy and robustness of different approaches. Recent works, such as the CHOTA metric [31], provide comprehensive evaluation frameworks that account for both local and global aspects of cell tracking, improving the assessment of lineage reconstruction methods. These efforts are vital for guiding the development of more accurate and efficient algorithms in the field. As the field continues to evolve, the integration of machine learning and deep learning techniques will likely play an increasingly important role in addressing these computational challenges.

### 5.2 Normalization and Quality Control for Single-Cell Data

Normalization and quality control are indispensable steps in the preprocessing of single-cell data, ensuring that the resulting datasets are reliable, consistent, and suitable for downstream integration and analysis. These processes address the inherent variability introduced by technical artifacts, biological noise, and experimental biases, which can otherwise confound biological interpretations. Effective normalization not only corrects for differences in sequencing depth and library size but also accounts for the sparsity and non-linear relationships of single-cell transcriptomic data. Quality control, on the other hand, involves rigorous filtering of low-quality cells and genes, which is crucial for maintaining the integrity of downstream analyses.

One of the most widely used normalization strategies is library size normalization, where read counts are scaled to a common total number of reads across cells. However, this method is often inadequate for single-cell data due to the high dropout rates and the presence of highly expressed genes that can disproportionately influence the normalization. Advanced methods such as scFP [2] and DropLasso [2] have been developed to address these limitations by incorporating more sophisticated models that account for the distributional properties of single-cell data. scFP, for instance, uses a hierarchical Bayesian framework to estimate the true expression levels while accounting for technical noise, whereas DropLasso employs a lasso-based regularization approach to identify and correct for outlier cells and genes.

Batch correction is another critical component of data preprocessing, especially when integrating datasets from different experimental batches or platforms. Methods such as Harmony [2] and Scanorama [2] have been designed to align data from different batches by learning a joint latent representation that preserves biological variation while removing technical artifacts. These approaches are particularly effective in multi-omics integration, where the alignment of different data modalities is essential for accurate biological interpretation. Additionally, deep learning-based methods have emerged as powerful tools for batch correction, as they can learn complex, non-linear relationships between batches and adjust for them effectively [2].

Quality control metrics play a pivotal role in identifying and filtering out low-quality cells and genes. Commonly used metrics include the number of detected genes per cell, the percentage of mitochondrial reads, and the dropout rate. These metrics help in identifying cells with abnormal expression profiles, which may result from poor sequencing quality, low RNA content, or other technical issues. For example, a high mitochondrial read percentage may indicate cell death or stress, while a low number of detected genes may suggest incomplete library preparation. Tools such as Seurat [2] and Scanpy [2] provide automated pipelines for applying these quality control thresholds and visualizing the data to guide the filtering process.

Despite the advances in normalization and quality control, challenges remain, particularly in handling the high dimensionality and heterogeneity of single-cell data. Emerging trends suggest that integrating domain-specific knowledge and leveraging machine learning techniques will be key to improving the robustness and scalability of these methods. Future directions may also involve the development of more interpretable models and the incorporation of spatial and temporal information to enhance the biological relevance of the preprocessing steps. Overall, the continuous refinement of normalization and quality control strategies is essential for unlocking the full potential of single-cell omics in understanding complex biological systems.

## 7 Challenges and Limitations

### 7.1 Technical Limitations in Lineage Data Generation

The generation of lineage data in single-cell experiments is a critical yet technically challenging process that underpins the accuracy and reliability of lineage tracing combined with single-cell omics. One of the primary technical limitations lies in the labeling techniques used to trace cellular lineages. While CRISPR-based lineage barcoding has emerged as a powerful tool for generating unique genetic markers, it is not without its drawbacks [2]. Issues such as off-target effects, mosaic labeling, and the potential for barcode mutations during cell division can introduce artifacts that compromise the fidelity of lineage reconstruction [1]. Furthermore, the scalability of such methods remains a challenge, as the complexity of lineage trees increases with the number of cells and generations, making it difficult to maintain accurate lineage tracking in large and complex biological systems [1].

Tracking cells across multiple generations is another significant technical challenge. Traditional tracking methods often rely on fluorescent labeling or other imaging-based approaches, which can suffer from limited resolution, photobleaching, and the inability to distinguish between closely related cells, particularly in high-density or rapidly dividing populations [2]. The dynamic nature of cellular processes, such as cell division, migration, and differentiation, further complicates lineage tracking, as it requires high temporal and spatial resolution to capture accurate lineage relationships [3]. Additionally, the integration of lineage data with single-cell omics data, such as transcriptomic or epigenomic profiles, introduces further technical hurdles. The mismatch in data structure, scale, and resolution between lineage and omics data can lead to inconsistencies and biases in downstream analyses, limiting the interpretability of the integrated data [1].

Preserving lineage integrity during downstream processing is another critical limitation. Single-cell experiments often involve a series of steps, including cell dissociation, library preparation, and sequencing, which can introduce technical noise, cell loss, or bias in the data. These processes can disrupt the spatial and temporal relationships between cells, making it difficult to reconstruct accurate lineage trees [24]. Moreover, the high-dimensional and heterogeneous nature of single-cell data requires sophisticated computational methods for lineage reconstruction, which can be computationally intensive and prone to errors [39]. 

Despite these challenges, recent advances in computational modeling and machine learning offer promising solutions. Techniques such as deep learning-based lineage inference and probabilistic models are being developed to improve the accuracy and robustness of lineage reconstruction [40]. However, these methods still face limitations in terms of scalability, interpretability, and the ability to handle noisy or incomplete data. As the field continues to evolve, addressing these technical limitations will be essential for advancing the integration of lineage tracing with single-cell omics and unlocking new insights into cellular dynamics and disease mechanisms.

### 7.2 Computational Challenges in Lineage Reconstruction

The reconstruction of lineage relationships from high-dimensional single-cell data presents a multitude of computational challenges, stemming from the complexity of the data, the limitations of existing algorithms, and the inherent noise and uncertainty in biological measurements. At the core of these challenges is the difficulty of accurately inferring the evolutionary history of cells based on their molecular profiles, which often exhibit high variability and are influenced by multiple confounding factors. This problem becomes increasingly complex as the number of cells and the dimensionality of the data grow, necessitating scalable and efficient computational strategies that can handle large-scale, heterogeneous datasets [2; 2].

One of the primary computational challenges is the handling of noise and missing data in lineage tracing experiments. Single-cell omics data are inherently noisy due to technical artifacts, such as dropout events, and biological variability, such as differences in cell cycle stages or environmental conditions. Traditional lineage reconstruction algorithms often struggle to distinguish true lineage relationships from spurious associations caused by such noise. For instance, methods that rely on clustering or nearest-neighbor approaches can produce inaccurate lineage trees when the data contain a high proportion of noisy or missing measurements [2; 2]. To address this, recent approaches have integrated probabilistic models and deep learning techniques that explicitly account for uncertainty in lineage assignment, improving the robustness of lineage inference [2].

Another significant challenge is the high dimensionality of single-cell data, which poses computational and algorithmic bottlenecks. Single-cell RNA-seq and epigenomic data are typically represented as high-dimensional feature vectors, making it difficult to apply standard clustering or trajectory inference methods directly. Dimensionality reduction techniques, such as t-SNE, UMAP, and PCA, are often used to project the data into lower-dimensional spaces, but these methods can distort the underlying biological relationships and may not capture the full complexity of lineage dynamics [6; 1]. To overcome this, researchers have developed more sophisticated algorithms that preserve the topological structure of the data, such as graph-based trajectory inference and manifold learning approaches [2; 1].

Moreover, the integration of lineage data with other omics layers, such as transcriptomic or epigenomic data, introduces additional computational challenges. While multi-omics integration can provide richer insights into cellular differentiation and fate decisions, it also requires the development of new algorithms that can handle the heterogeneity and complexity of these data. Existing methods often struggle with aligning lineage information with other omics data due to differences in data structure, scale, and resolution [12; 21]. To address this, researchers are exploring deep learning and probabilistic modeling approaches that can jointly model lineage and omics data, enabling more accurate and interpretable lineage reconstructions [1; 22].

In conclusion, the computational challenges in lineage reconstruction from single-cell data remain a significant hurdle in the field, requiring continued innovation in algorithm design, data integration, and uncertainty quantification. Emerging trends, such as the use of deep learning and probabilistic modeling, offer promising solutions to these challenges, but further research is needed to develop scalable, accurate, and robust methods for lineage reconstruction in complex biological systems.

### 7.3 Biological Complexities in Lineage Dynamics

The inherent biological complexities in lineage dynamics pose significant challenges to the accuracy and reliability of lineage tracing and interpretation. These complexities, including cell plasticity, lineage reprogramming, and dynamic cellular states, complicate the assumptions and methodologies used in lineage reconstruction. Cell plasticity, the ability of cells to transition between different states, introduces ambiguity in defining lineage boundaries and can lead to misinterpretation of lineage relationships [2]. For instance, stem cells and progenitor cells can adopt alternative fates under different environmental or experimental conditions, making it difficult to establish a clear lineage tree. This phenomenon is particularly pronounced in regenerative and cancer biology, where cells can switch between quiescent, proliferative, and differentiated states, blurring the lines of traditional lineage models [2].

Lineage reprogramming, another major complexity, refers to the ability of cells to revert to a more primitive state, often through epigenetic or transcriptional modifications. This process challenges the static assumptions of many lineage tracing models, which typically assume a unidirectional progression of cell fate decisions. Lineage reprogramming can occur naturally during development or be induced experimentally, further complicating the interpretation of lineage data. For example, in cancer, tumor cells can undergo reprogramming to acquire stem-like properties, leading to the emergence of resistant subclones that are difficult to trace [2]. This dynamic behavior necessitates the development of more flexible and adaptive lineage reconstruction methods that can account for such non-linear transitions [2].

Dynamic cellular states, including transitions between different gene expression profiles or metabolic states, add another layer of complexity to lineage analysis. Cells can exhibit heterogeneous responses to external stimuli, leading to varying lineage outcomes even within the same developmental context. This heterogeneity is particularly challenging to capture in high-throughput single-cell omics data, where the resolution of cellular states can be limited by technical noise and sampling bias. For example, in the context of immune cell development, cells may transiently enter intermediate states that are not captured by traditional lineage tracing methods, resulting in incomplete or misleading lineage trees [6]. Advanced computational models that integrate temporal and spatial information, such as those using probabilistic graphical models or deep learning approaches, are being explored to better capture these dynamic states [1].

These biological complexities highlight the need for more sophisticated and integrative approaches to lineage tracing. Current methods often struggle to account for the fluid and context-dependent nature of cell fate decisions, leading to limitations in their predictive power and biological interpretability. Addressing these challenges requires a deeper integration of experimental and computational techniques, as well as a more nuanced understanding of the underlying biological mechanisms. As the field advances, the development of models that can dynamically adapt to changing cellular states and account for lineage plasticity will be crucial for improving the accuracy and relevance of lineage tracing in biological research.

### 7.4 Ethical and Practical Considerations

The integration of lineage tracing with single-cell omics presents a powerful approach to dissecting cellular heterogeneity and developmental dynamics. However, this integration also raises significant ethical and practical challenges, particularly in the context of human-related research. One of the primary concerns is the issue of privacy and data security, given the highly sensitive nature of human-derived lineage and single-cell data. The potential for misuse or unauthorized access to such data necessitates robust data governance frameworks and strict compliance with ethical guidelines, as highlighted in recent studies on the ethical implications of single-cell data [2]. Furthermore, the storage and sharing of large-scale single-cell datasets pose logistical challenges, as these datasets often require significant computational resources and standardized formats to ensure reproducibility and interoperability [2].

Another critical issue is the ethical implications of lineage tracing in clinical settings. While lineage tracing can provide valuable insights into disease progression and treatment response, it also raises questions about informed consent and the potential for genetic discrimination. For instance, the identification of specific cell lineages or mutations through single-cell omics can inadvertently reveal information about an individual's genetic predispositions, which could be exploited in ways that compromise personal autonomy [2]. This necessitates the development of clear ethical guidelines for the collection, storage, and use of lineage and single-cell data, especially in translational research contexts.

Practically, the integration of lineage tracing with single-cell omics faces significant computational and technical barriers. The generation of high-quality lineage data requires precise labeling and tracking techniques, which can be challenging in complex biological systems. Moreover, the integration of lineage data with single-cell omics data often involves handling high-dimensional and noisy datasets, which can complicate downstream analysis and interpretation [2]. Recent advances in machine learning and deep learning have shown promise in addressing these challenges, but they also raise new concerns about model interpretability and the potential for algorithmic bias [2].

In addition, the scalability of lineage tracing and single-cell omics technologies remains a key practical challenge. While these technologies have been successfully applied to model organisms, their application to human tissues and complex biological systems is still in its infancy. This necessitates the development of standardized protocols and robust validation strategies to ensure the reliability and reproducibility of results [2]. Furthermore, the integration of these technologies with clinical workflows requires careful consideration of practical constraints, such as the cost of data acquisition and the need for specialized expertise [2].

Overall, the ethical and practical considerations surrounding lineage tracing and single-cell omics are multifaceted and require a multidisciplinary approach to address. While these technologies hold great promise for advancing our understanding of biological processes, their responsible use demands careful attention to ethical, legal, and practical challenges. As the field continues to evolve, it is essential to develop robust frameworks that balance scientific innovation with the protection of individual rights and the integrity of biological research.

## 8 Future Directions and Emerging Trends

### 8.1 Advances in Spatial Omics and Multi-Omics Integration

Spatial omics technologies have emerged as a transformative force in the study of cellular organization and function, enabling the integration of spatially resolved molecular data with single-cell omics. These technologies allow researchers to map gene expression, chromatin accessibility, and other molecular features within the context of tissue architecture, thereby bridging the gap between bulk tissue analysis and single-cell resolution [20]. This integration is particularly powerful for lineage analysis, as it provides a framework to track cellular development and differentiation in their native spatial environment, offering unprecedented insights into the complex interplay between cellular identity, location, and function [41].

Recent advances in spatial transcriptomics, such as Visium and Slide-Seq, have enabled high-resolution mapping of gene expression across tissues, while techniques like spatially resolved single-cell RNA sequencing (srscRNA-seq) combine the spatial information with single-cell resolution [9]. These methods are being further enhanced through the integration with single-cell omics data, allowing for the reconstruction of lineage trees that account for both molecular and spatial context. For example, frameworks like stEnTrans and SpaDiT leverage spatial information to improve the accuracy of lineage and cell identity inference [41; 20]. These tools utilize deep learning and generative models to integrate spatial and single-cell data, enabling the prediction of gene expression patterns and the identification of spatially correlated cell populations.

The integration of spatial omics with other omics layers, such as epigenomics and proteomics, is also an emerging trend. Techniques like spatial epigenomics and spatial proteomics provide complementary information about the regulatory landscapes and functional states of cells within tissues, further enriching the multi-omics landscape [42]. This multi-omics integration allows for the identification of regulatory modules and the inference of cell-cell interactions, which are critical for understanding tissue development and disease progression.

Despite these advances, several challenges remain. The integration of spatial and single-cell data is computationally intensive, requiring sophisticated algorithms to handle the high dimensionality and heterogeneity of the data. Furthermore, the spatial resolution and coverage of current technologies are still limited, making it challenging to capture the full complexity of tissue organization. Addressing these challenges will require continued innovation in both experimental and computational methods.

Looking ahead, the future of spatial omics and multi-omics integration lies in the development of more scalable and accurate computational frameworks that can seamlessly integrate diverse data modalities. The application of machine learning and deep learning techniques, such as graph neural networks and diffusion models, will play a crucial role in this endeavor, enabling the reconstruction of dynamic cellular processes and the identification of novel biological insights [43; 18]. As these technologies continue to evolve, they will further transform our understanding of cellular dynamics and open new avenues for biological research and clinical applications.

### 8.2 Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning in Lineage Reconstruction

Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) are playing an increasingly pivotal role in enhancing the accuracy, scalability, and interpretability of lineage reconstruction from single-cell data. Traditional lineage reconstruction methods often face challenges in handling the high dimensionality and noise inherent in single-cell omics data. However, the integration of AI and ML techniques has introduced novel approaches that address these limitations and enable more robust and interpretable lineage inference. Deep learning, in particular, has emerged as a powerful tool for modeling complex cellular dynamics, offering new opportunities to unravel lineage relationships and track cell fate decisions with high precision [2].

One of the most significant advancements in this domain is the use of deep learning for lineage reconstruction. For instance, graph neural networks (GNNs) have been employed to model the relationships between cells and infer lineage trees by capturing the spatial and temporal dependencies between cellular states [2]. Similarly, transformer-based architectures have demonstrated success in tracking cell lineages by leveraging self-attention mechanisms to capture long-range dependencies in time-series data [6]. These approaches not only improve the accuracy of lineage inference but also allow for the integration of multi-omics data, enabling a more comprehensive understanding of cellular development and disease [2].

Generative models, such as diffusion transformers and conditional diffusion models, have also been applied to lineage reconstruction, offering the ability to simulate and predict cell trajectories and lineage relationships [2]. These models are particularly useful in scenarios where the available data is sparse or incomplete, as they can generate realistic synthetic data that reflects the underlying biological processes. Furthermore, the use of self-supervised learning and transfer learning has enabled the application of pre-trained models to new datasets or biological contexts, reducing the need for large annotated datasets and improving generalization [2].

In addition to deep learning, probabilistic models and Bayesian inference techniques have been widely used to incorporate uncertainty into lineage reconstruction. These methods provide a principled way to quantify the confidence in lineage assignments and account for experimental noise and sampling biases [39]. For example, the use of variational autoencoders (VAEs) and Gaussian mixture models has allowed for the joint modeling of lineage and omics data, capturing complex relationships between lineage and molecular profiles [39].

The integration of AI and ML in lineage reconstruction is not without challenges. Scalability, computational efficiency, and the need for robust algorithms to handle high-dimensional and noisy data remain critical issues. Furthermore, the interpretability of these models is essential for biological insights, necessitating the development of explainable AI techniques that can provide meaningful interpretations of lineage relationships [39]. As the field continues to evolve, the development of more efficient and interpretable algorithms will be crucial for advancing the application of AI and ML in lineage reconstruction. Future directions will likely involve the integration of domain-specific knowledge into these models, enabling more accurate and biologically relevant lineage inference [2].

### 8.3 Real-Time and Dynamic Lineage Tracking Technologies

Real-time and dynamic lineage tracking technologies are emerging as critical tools for understanding the complex and often rapid processes of cell fate decisions, differentiation, and lineage progression in living systems. These technologies enable continuous monitoring of individual cells and their descendants, providing insights into cellular dynamics that static or snapshot-based approaches cannot capture. The integration of advanced imaging, computational modeling, and real-time data processing has opened new frontiers in lineage tracing, allowing for the study of lineage events as they unfold in vivo or in cultured systems [21].

One of the most promising approaches is the use of deep learning and temporal sequence modeling for real-time cell tracking. These methods leverage convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to analyze video sequences from live-cell imaging, enabling the detection and tracking of individual cells as they divide and change states. For example, the development of algorithms that use the isosceles triangle transform for 2D position detection and the Rayleigh-Sommerfeld model for 3D axial reconstruction has significantly improved the accuracy and speed of real-time tracking [1]. These approaches not only allow for the identification of cell lineages but also provide a framework for predicting future cell states and trajectories.

In vivo lineage tracking has also seen significant advancements with the use of CRISPR-based barcoding and live imaging. CRISPR-based lineage barcoding allows for the introduction of unique genetic tags in cells, which are inherited by daughter cells, enabling the reconstruction of lineage trees over time [22]. This method, when combined with live-cell imaging and real-time data acquisition, allows for the study of lineage dynamics in complex biological systems. Additionally, the integration of spatial information with lineage data is becoming increasingly important, as it provides context about how cell fate decisions are influenced by the surrounding microenvironment [23].

Another key area of development is the application of deep learning in real-time data processing. For instance, the use of deep neural networks for real-time cell sorting and classification has demonstrated the potential to achieve high-throughput and low-latency inference, making it suitable for use in flow cytometry and other high-speed applications [24]. These models are trained to recognize patterns in real-time data, enabling the immediate classification and sorting of cells based on their lineage or functional state.

Despite these advancements, several challenges remain, including the need for more scalable and efficient computational models, the handling of noisy or incomplete data, and the integration of multiple data modalities in real-time. The development of hybrid models that combine deep learning with probabilistic and Bayesian inference may offer solutions to these challenges, allowing for more robust and interpretable lineage tracking [25]. As these technologies continue to evolve, they hold great promise for advancing our understanding of cellular dynamics and enabling new applications in regenerative medicine, immunology, and cancer research.

### 8.4 Expansion to Non-Model Organisms and Complex Biological Systems

The expansion of lineage tracing and single-cell omics to non-model organisms and complex biological systems represents a significant frontier in modern biological research. While much of the foundational work in these fields has been conducted in model organisms such as *Drosophila*, *C. elegans*, and mice, the growing recognition of the importance of ecological, evolutionary, and non-traditional biological systems necessitates the development of methodologies that can accommodate the diversity and complexity of these systems. This shift is driven by both the need to understand biological processes beyond the confines of well-characterized model systems and the increasing availability of high-throughput sequencing technologies that can be applied to a broader range of species [40].

One of the primary challenges in extending lineage tracing to non-model organisms lies in the lack of established genetic tools and genomic resources. Many non-model species lack well-annotated genomes, extensive genetic toolkits, and the same level of functional genomic knowledge that exists for model organisms. This necessitates the development of flexible and adaptable lineage tracing strategies that do not rely on pre-engineered genetic constructs. For instance, CRISPR-based lineage barcoding has been successfully adapted to a variety of organisms, including plants and non-traditional invertebrates, by leveraging endogenous genetic variation or synthetic barcoding strategies [40]. Moreover, recent advances in single-cell transcriptomics have enabled the profiling of gene expression in species with no prior genomic resources, opening the door to lineage reconstruction in these systems [6].

The integration of lineage information with single-cell omics in complex biological systems, such as ecological communities or multi-cellular organisms with intricate developmental programs, presents additional challenges. In these systems, cell-cell interactions, environmental influences, and dynamic spatial organization play critical roles in shaping lineage outcomes. Emerging spatial omics technologies, such as spatial transcriptomics and multiplexed imaging, are being used to capture the spatial context of lineage dynamics, which is essential for understanding how cells interact and differentiate in their native environments [44]. Furthermore, the application of lineage tracing to microbial communities, where cellular diversity and dynamic interactions are key features, has the potential to reveal previously inaccessible insights into ecological and evolutionary processes [39].

Another key area of development is the adaptation of computational and algorithmic approaches to handle the increased complexity and heterogeneity of non-model organism data. Traditional lineage reconstruction algorithms, which were developed for simpler systems, often fail to accurately capture lineage relationships in more complex biological contexts. New methods are being developed to handle the high dimensionality and spatial variability of data from non-model organisms, including the use of deep learning and probabilistic models to infer lineage relationships from single-cell transcriptomes [40]. These methods are also being extended to incorporate environmental and ecological data, enabling a more holistic understanding of lineage dynamics in complex systems [40].

The expansion of lineage tracing and single-cell omics to non-model organisms and complex biological systems not only broadens the scope of biological research but also introduces new challenges in data interpretation, computational modeling, and experimental design. Future directions in this area will require interdisciplinary collaboration, the development of robust and scalable computational frameworks, and the continued refinement of experimental techniques to ensure accurate and reproducible results. As these methodologies mature, they will enable a deeper understanding of cellular dynamics in a wide range of biological contexts, from ecological systems to human health and disease.

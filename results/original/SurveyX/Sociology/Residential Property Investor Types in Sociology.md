# Abstract

This survey paper examines the heterogeneous landscape of residential property investorsincluding small-scale landlords, corporate entities, and speculative buyersand their differential impacts on housing markets through sociological and economic lenses. By synthesizing empirical evidence from urban housing studies, we analyze how investor typologies shape landlord-tenant relations, affordability, and neighborhood dynamics, revealing that corporate ownership correlates with accelerated gentrification and formal evictions, while small-scale landlords demonstrate greater tenant retention but limited maintenance capacity. The study highlights how investor identities, constructed through intersecting dimensions of social class, ethnicity, and gender, mediate housing market outcomes and reproduce spatial inequalities. Policy analysis demonstrates the limitations of uniform regulations across investor categories, emphasizing the need for typology-specific interventions. The paper identifies critical research gaps in longitudinal investor behavior, comparative global studies, and climate adaptation impacts, while advocating for interdisciplinary approaches that integrate institutional economics with critical social theories. Ultimately, this survey provides a comprehensive framework for understanding how investor diversity structures housing access and urban development, offering insights for equitable policy design in increasingly financialized real estate markets.

# 1.2 Sociological and Economic Frameworks in Real Estate Investment

The analysis of residential property investment necessitates a dual theoretical lens, combining sociological perspectives on investor behavior with economic frameworks that elucidate market dynamics and landlord-tenant relations. Sociological theories, particularly those grounded in social class and identity formation, reveal how investor motivations are embedded within broader structural inequalities and cultural capital distributions [1]. Economic theories, conversely, provide formal models of market interactions, price determination, and the allocation of housing resources, offering mechanisms to quantify the impacts of investor strategies on affordability and spatial segregation [1].

Understanding the sociological underpinnings of investor behavior is essential for grasping the complexities of housing markets. Social class theory suggests that landlord identities and investment strategies are distinctly stratified along socioeconomic lines, with higher-income investors tending to pursue speculative acquisitions and manage extensive property portfolios, while lower-income landlords typically depend on rental income for their livelihoods. This dynamic reflects broader patterns of socio-spatial segregation in urban housing markets, where factors such as neighborhood attractiveness and social characteristics influence investment behaviors. Research indicates that areas experiencing gentrification often exhibit specific morphological patterns, further illustrating how urban form interacts with socioeconomic factors to shape investment practices and housing market trends [1, 1]. This stratification manifests in divergent tenant selection criteria, maintenance practices, and eviction patterns, reinforcing existing inequalities in housing access. Ethnicity and gender further intersect with class dynamics, as demonstrated by studies showing racialized disparities in property ownership concentrations and gendered differences in risk tolerance among small-scale investors.

Economic frameworks, including neoclassical models of supply-demand equilibrium and behavioral economics approaches, explain how investor heterogeneity affects market efficiency and stability. The former approach focuses on rational actor paradigms, where investors make decisions aimed at optimizing returns within specific constraints, while the latter approach integrates cognitive biases and herd behavior, which can lead to the formation of speculative bubbles or cycles of disinvestment. These phenomena reflect how social dynamics and urban morphology influence market behavior and investment patterns [1, 1]. Institutional economics adds another layer by examining how regulatory environments and property rights regimes mediate investor-tenant power asymmetries, particularly in contexts with weak tenant protections.

Integrating these frameworks reveals tensions between individual profit maximization and collective housing needs. Sociological analyses of eviction cascades provide valuable insights that enhance economic models of vacancy chains by demonstrating how investor decisions influence neighborhood dynamics through a combination of market mechanisms and social networks. These analyses reveal that housing transactions are affected not only by economic factors but also by the social characteristics of neighborhoods, which can lead to socio-spatial segregation while still preserving some level of social diversity. Furthermore, studies on gentrification indicate that urban form plays a significant role in shaping these dynamics, as evidenced by the recognizable morphological patterns observed in gentrified areas, which are linked to their geographic and infrastructural features [1, 1]. Future research directions should prioritize interdisciplinary synthesis, developing unified models that capture the recursive relationships between investor identities, market structures, and policy interventions.

# 1.3 Research Objectives and Scope

This survey establishes three primary objectives: first, to systematically categorize residential property investor typologies and their behavioral patterns; second, to analyze how these investor identities intersect with housing market dynamics through sociological and economic lenses; and third, to evaluate the policy implications of investor diversity for housing affordability and neighborhood stability. The analysis incorporates both micro-level interactions between landlords and tenants as well as macro-level effects on the housing market, utilizing empirical data from diverse urban housing markets to highlight the influence of socio-spatial segregation and urban morphology on market dynamics and social diversity [1, 1].

Focusing on three dominant investor categoriessmall-scale landlords, corporate entities, and speculative buyersthis investigation acknowledges the fluid boundaries between these classifications. Methodologically, the survey synthesizes findings from quantitative housing market analyses, qualitative studies of landlord decision-making, and spatial examinations of investment concentration effects. The scope of this survey intentionally excludes non-residential real estate investments and informal housing markets, as these areas necessitate unique theoretical frameworks that extend beyond the survey’s primary focus on housing sociology, which emphasizes the dynamics of urban housing markets, social diversity, and the impact of socio-spatial segregation. This distinction is crucial because understanding the complexities of gentrification and urban form requires specialized analyses that are not addressed within the context of residential housing transactions and neighborhood social characteristics [1, 1].

Key limitations include geographical bias toward documented cases in North American and European cities, where investor behavior has been extensively studied compared to Global South contexts. Temporal constraints also emerge, as the survey primarily examines post-2008 financial crisis investment patterns, potentially overlooking historical shifts in landlord-tenant power dynamics. Additionally, the analysis of social equity outcomes relies on available eviction and displacement data, which may underrepresent marginalized tenant experiences due to measurement challenges [1]. Addressing these gaps through comparative international studies and longitudinal analyses of investor strategy evolution will be essential for advancing the understanding of these complex interactions.

# 3.1 Investor Typologies in Residential Property (Small-Scale Landlords, Corporate Entities, Speculative Buyers)

Residential property investors vary in behavior and market impact based on scale, investment horizons, and strategies. Small-scale landlords, owning fewer than ten units, dominate numerically but have limited capital and localized knowledge [1]. They prioritize stable cash flow over aggressive appreciation, fostering long-term tenant retention and community engagement. Their risk tolerance varies, with some using high mortgage debt to expand portfolios and others maintaining conservative equity positions, necessitating tailored policy interventions for their unique challenges.

Corporate entities, such as real estate investment trusts (REITs) and private equity firms, use institutional management and algorithmic models for portfolio optimization across metropolitan regions [1]. Their strategies include macroeconomic indicators and zoning changes, influencing local markets and often leading to rent inflation and tenant displacement. These investors capitalize on distressed asset sales during economic downturns, raising concerns about long-term housing affordability.

Speculative buyers, with short investment horizons, exploit price discrepancies and regulatory gaps, influencing market dynamics and contributing to socio-spatial segregation and gentrification [1, 1]. This group includes house-flippers and absentee investors, introducing market volatility and artificial scarcity during booms. Their prevalence correlates with housing market financialization, where properties serve as financial instruments rather than dwellings.

These typologies result in measurable market outcomes. Small-scale landlords are responsive to tenant needs but have lower capital expenditure capacity, while corporate investors achieve maintenance economies of scale but have rigid rent protocols. Speculative activity increases liquidity but destabilizes neighborhoods through frequent ownership changes. These impacts underscore the need for typology-specific policy interventions for housing affordability, as uniform regulations often have unintended consequences. Future research should explore hybrid investor models integrating characteristics from traditional classifications, focusing on their roles in digitally-mediated property management platforms. Understanding these hybrid models can provide insights into investor preferences shaped by property attributes and neighborhood characteristics, as evidenced by studies on urban form and income distribution in cities like Paris and London [1, 1].

Their localized knowledge and tenant relationships enable adaptive management but limit capital for improvements and expansion, balancing personal investment with community responsibility.

Corporate investors focus on profit maximization, using standardized criteria and centralized systems for economies of scale across portfolios [1]. This efficiency often results in rigid rent algorithms, increasing tenant turnover and dissatisfaction.

Speculative buyers show volatile behavior, driven by short-term market conditions. Their activities create temporal housing market patterns, intensifying cyclical volatility and reflecting socio-spatial dynamics, contributing to gentrification and socioeconomic segregation [1, 1]. Their risk profiles differ: small-scale landlords face liquidity constraints, corporate investors manage systemic risks through diversification, and speculative buyers gamble on market timing, producing varied responses to economic shocks.

Speculative buyers use creative financing, increasing systemic risk. These pathways influence housing market stability and accessibility.

# 4.1 Theoretical Frameworks for Understanding Landlord Identities (Social Class, Ethnicity, Gender Dynamics)

Landlord identities are constructed through social class, ethnicity, and gender, each influencing investment practices and tenant relations. Social class theory views property ownership as reinforcing economic stratification, with access to capital and credit markets creating class-specific landlord identities [1]. Cultural capital affects decision-making, with upper-class landlords using professional management and working-class landlords engaging directly with tenants, affecting housing precarity experiences. These identities manifest in maintenance standards, eviction practices, and neighborhood choices, reinforcing spatial inequalities and shaping societal perceptions.

Ethnicity, intersecting with class, produces racially stratified landlord-tenant dynamics, especially in ethnic enclave markets. Minority landlords face financial constraints and valuation disparities, limiting growth [1]. They may form culturally-specific tenant networks absent in institutional markets. Gentrifying areas often see white investors displacing minority residents, creating tensions between economic rationality and community preservation. Critical race theory highlights property ownership as a racialized privilege, with historical exclusion affecting current investment landscapes, contributing to systemic housing market inequalities.

Gender dynamics add complexity, with differences in investment strategies and risk tolerance between male and female landlords. Feminist economic frameworks show women adopt conservative strategies, prioritizing tenant stability over expansion [1]. Structural constraints and cultural risk perceptions influence these gendered approaches, with male-dominated networks reinforcing disparities in acquisition opportunities. Gender also affects performative aspects, with male landlords emphasizing authority and female landlords navigating nurturing expectations. This understanding calls for targeted interventions to address gender disparities in landlord practices.

Intersectional analyses reveal unique landlord subjectivities, such as middle-class minority women balancing financial and community goals. These identities affect management styles, with varied power dynamics influencing housing security experiences. Understanding these intersections is crucial for developing equitable housing policies.

Institutional sociology explains how identity-based practices are codified through professional norms and regulations, shaping real estate investor frameworks. Licensing, certifications, and associations reinforce identity hierarchies and influence socio-spatial dynamics, perpetuating income segregation while allowing social diversity. Urban housing market research shows these frameworks’ impact on urban form and socioeconomic patterns [1, 1]. Digital platforms may mitigate or exacerbate disparities, depending on design and implementation. Longitudinal mixed-methods research should track evolving landlord identities amid technological and regulatory changes.

# 4.2 Socioeconomic Trends and Their Impact on Landlord Identities

Socioeconomic trends reshape landlord identities through financialization, demographic shifts, and technological disruption. Financialization transforms landlords from local managers to global investors, especially corporate and institutional ones, adopting practices like algorithmic rent-setting and securitized income streams [1]. This shift alters landlord-tenant relationships and raises ethical concerns. Platform economies enable small-scale landlords to achieve scale efficiencies, changing rental market dynamics.

Demographic changes, such as aging populations and non-traditional households, affect investment horizons and tenant criteria. Single-person households and intergenerational wealth transfers create new landlord cohorts with different management approaches [1]. Regional disparities produce distinct identities in shrinking versus growing cities, affecting housing stability.

Technology mediates these forces through professionalization and disintermediation. Management software standardizes practices but may embed biases [1]. Peer-to-peer platforms allow bypassing traditional systems, creating personalized but less regulated relationships. These tensions highlight the need for research on evolving landlord identities in the digital age.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated trends and introduced identity challenges, with landlords balancing mortgage obligations and tenant retention. Institutional landlords maintained rent collection through automation, while small-scale landlords personalized payment plans [1]. Post-pandemic norms altered urban demand, requiring investors to reconsider location and property configurations. These changes necessitate identity negotiation, balancing financial and social expectations.

Climate change pressures add constraints, with landlords in vulnerable regions facing insurance crises and regulatory adaptation. Disparities between climate-resilient and at-risk properties create new stratifications, with sustainability-focused investors integrating environmental considerations. Urban gentrification studies show neighborhood characteristics influence investment behaviors and market dynamics [1, 1]. Minority and working-class landlords in affected areas face compounded challenges. Future research should explore new landlord archetypes and practices in global markets amid these intersecting forces.

# 5.1 Housing Market Dynamics (Price Fluctuations, Gentrification, Socio-Spatial Segregation)

Investor behavior significantly influences housing market dynamics through price formation, gentrification, and socio-spatial segregation. Price fluctuations are notably affected by investor types, with corporate and speculative activities causing more volatility compared to markets dominated by small-scale landlords [1]. Institutional investors use algorithmic valuation models that create feedback loops, accelerating appreciation in targeted neighborhoods and suppressing values in others, producing discontinuous price gradients that challenge traditional urban land economics. These dynamics impact community stability and accessibility, altering urban demographic compositions.

Gentrification varies with investor composition, as corporate investors drive rapid neighborhood changes through coordinated acquisitions and renovations, targeting areas with specific urban characteristics [1]. In contrast, small-scale landlords contribute to gradual neighborhood transformation through passive appreciation. Early speculative purchases can establish price anchors that attract institutional capital, leading to significant shifts in community identity.

Socio-spatial segregation results from investor criteria intersecting with urban inequalities. Corporate reliance on algorithmic risk assessments often institutionalizes exclusionary practices, reinforcing racial and economic divides [1]. Small-scale landlords display diverse spatial behaviors, maintaining properties in declining markets due to personal ties or following capital flows to higherreturn areas, creating investment intensity patchworks that amplify socioeconomic divisions.

Financialization introduces novel price transmission dynamics, where investor-driven markets correlate more with financial indicators than local income levels, decoupling housing costs from community economics [1]. Global real estate investment complicates these dynamics, as cross-border capital flows respond to macroeconomic conditions, not local needs, marginalizing residents in their neighborhoods.

Gentrification’s path dependency is evident in investor composition, with corporate-led transitions standardizing neighborhoods through formulaic renovations, while fragmented ownership retains diversity [1]. However, diversity often proves temporary as institutional investors target partially gentrified areas for consolidation, reducing neighborhood resilience to economic shocks.

Socio-spatial segregation operates through exclusionary displacement and inclusionary clustering. Exclusionary displacement occurs when investor activity prices out residents, while inclusionary clustering forms high-income enclaves [1]. Public infrastructure investments can create feedback loops, attracting investors and accelerating demographic shifts. Investor type influences segregation scale, with corporate activities transforming neighborhoods broadly and small-scale investors creating micro-level variations.

Climate risk is increasingly salient in investor-driven dynamics. Properties in vulnerable areas experience divergent investment patterns, with institutional capital retreating faster than small-scale owners [1]. This response creates environmental spatial segregation, where climate vulnerability correlates with ownership fragmentation. Future research should explore climate adaptation policies’ interactions with investor behavior and housing market stability.

# 5.2 Policy and Regulatory Responses in Housing Markets

Policy interventions targeting investor behavior address market distortions across regulatory domains. Rent control policies vary in efficacy, with corporate entities exploiting loopholes, while small-scale landlords comply more but reinvest less under strict caps [1]. Vacancy taxes and antispeculation measures target short-term investors, but effectiveness depends on design features like vacancy penalty durations and capital gains tax timelines [1]. These policies highlight the limitations of one-size-fits-all approaches in diverse investor landscapes, necessitating nuanced understanding of regulatory impacts on investor behaviors.

Zoning and land use regulations mediate investor impacts through density controls and use restrictions, often producing unintended consequences. Inclusionary zoning mandates affordable units but can trigger corporate avoidance or fee-in-lieu compliance, diluting effectiveness [1]. Small-scale investors benefit from relaxed zoning in ADU policies, increasing density without attracting institutional capital. Regulatory landscapes require coordinated approaches aligning local zoning with broader housing strategies to address shortages and affordability challenges.

Tenant protection frameworks mitigate landlord-tenant power asymmetries, but enforcement varies by investor type. Just-cause eviction ordinances and right-to-counsel programs show higher compliance among corporate landlords, while small-scale landlords often settle pre-court to avoid litigation [1]. Rent stabilization interacts with investor financial structures, with corporate entities facing constraints in absorbing rent limitations. These responses highlight the need for tiered regulatory systems accounting for investor scale and financing models in tenant protection design.

Financial regulation addresses systemic risks from investor activities through macroprudential measures and transparency requirements. Mortgage underwriting standards and loan-to-value ratios target speculative buying, though their impact diminishes as cash purchases proliferate [1]. Corporate ownership disclosure laws counter opaque investment vehicles, but jurisdictional arbitrage enables continued anonymity. Emerging innovations include algorithmic auditing of rent-setting software and mandatory climate risk disclosures, representing next-generation regulatory approaches to market distortions.

Public housing investments and community land trusts counterbalance investor dominance in tight markets. These non-market mechanisms preserve affordability when strategically located to disrupt speculative geographiessuch as acquiring properties along gentrification frontiers [1]. Scalability limitations necessitate complementary policies like public-private partnerships with long-term affordability covenants. Effective policy portfolios combine supply-side interventions with demandside supports, recognizing investor behavior responds to both regulatory constraints and alternative housing options.

International policy experiments reveal context-dependent successes in moderating investor impacts, from Berlin’s corporate ownership caps to Singapore’s additional buyer stamp duties. These cases show effective policies must align with local market structures, where investor types exhibit distinct concentration patterns and financialization levels [1]. Future innovations should focus on dynamic tools adjusting to market conditions, such as escalating vacancy tax rates with price inflation or affordability-triggered density bonuses. Developing adaptive frameworks requires monitoring investor strategy evolution in response to regulations, forming a feedback loop between policy and market behavior analysis.

# 5.3 Case Studies of Social Equity Outcomes

Empirical case studies show divergent investor behaviors produce measurable social equity outcomes, focusing on displacement, affordability erosion, and community fragmentation. In Toronto’s Parkdale, REIT acquisitions correlated with a $42 \%$ rent increase and tripling of eviction applications, affecting low-income immigrant communities [1]. This concentration created housing precarity clusters, where standardized rent increases displaced long-term residents. Community-led research showed algorithmic pricing models exacerbated affordability gaps despite stagnant neighborhood median incomes, highlighting the need for policy interventions addressing vulnerabilities in aggressive investment strategies.

In Berlin, fragmented small-scale ownership preserved diversity during early gentrification. Kreuzberg properties with individual landlords maintained $30 \%$ lower turnover rates compared to corporate-owned counterparts [1]. However, institutional investors targeted the area post-2015, acquiring portfolios from aging landlords, accelerating displacement despite strong tenant protections. This necessitates re-evaluating tenant measures to remain effective amidst shifting investor dynamics.

Atlanta’s BeltLine area illustrates speculative investment dynamics and equity implications. Analysis showed $78 \%$ of properties sold within two years involved speculators, creating artificial scarcity and increasing median prices by $154 \%$ , while Black homeownership rates declined by 23 percentage points [1]. Speculative activity disrupted neighborhood continuity, highlighting the need for frameworks mitigating speculative impacts on vulnerable populations.

Singapore’s public housing interventions counter investor impacts on equity by restricting foreign and corporate investment in HDB flats, maintaining homeownership rates above $90 \%$ despite demand pressures [1]. This shows targeted regulationpermitting small-scale owner-occupiers while excluding speculative and institutional capitalpreserves affordability and stability in high-growth contexts, though transferability is limited to strong-state governance with substantial public housing.

Minneapolis’ 2040 Plan addresses investor-driven inequities through zoning reforms, showing a $12 \%$ increase in small-scale multifamily development by local landlords, contrasting with $3 \%$ growth in corporate-owned units [1]. This shift correlated with gradual rent increases, suggesting typology diversification moderates affordability pressures. Land use policies can intentionally shape investor composition to achieve equity objectives, emphasizing proactive governance in inclusive urban development.

Miami’s Little Haiti reveals climate gentrification intersections with investor-driven displacement. Analysis showed institutional investors acquired elevated parcels at triple the rate of low-lying areas, pricing out Haitian residents despite historical undervaluation [1]. This exemplifies how climate adaptation pressures and investor typologies create new equity challenges, where financialized responses to risk accelerate displacement of vulnerable communities. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for policies addressing climate resilience and social equity.

These cases indicate investor typologies, rather than investment volume, crucially determine equity outcomes. The interplay between socio-spatial segregation, social diversity, and neighborhood morphology influences price distribution and urban development dynamics [1, 1]. Policy responses must move beyond generic restrictions to nuanced interventions differentiating investor categories by social impacts. Future research should prioritize comparative methodologies controlling for regional conditions while isolating specific investor behavior effects on equity indicators, contributing to understanding housing investments and social equity.

# 6.1 Tenant Rights and Legal Protections

Tenant rights and protections are crucial in mediating landlord-tenant power dynamics, with efficacy varying across investor types and jurisdictions. Statutory protections typically cover security of tenure, habitability standards, and affordability mechanisms like rent stabilization [1]. These elements shape tenant experiences, especially in urban areas with high housing demand. Corporate landlords often comply with regulations but use complex legal strategies to bypass protections, while small landlords may lack resources for maintenance, exacerbating tenant challenges [1].

Eviction moratoriums and just-cause eviction laws highlight the varied impacts of legal protections. During the COVID-19 pandemic, institutional landlords managed rent arrears with economies of scale, while small landlords, facing financial strain, resorted to informal evictions [1]. This reveals how uniform protections yield asymmetric outcomes, with corporate investors absorbing regulatory costs and small landlords exiting the market, reducing affordable housing supply. Effectiveness of eviction protections depends on tenant awareness and legal access, favoring educated, higher-income renters and widening the gap between legal entitlements and practical realization.

Habitability standards enforcement varies by landlord type, with corporate landlords leveraging standardized systems for compliance but using waivers to limit obligations [1]. Small landlords struggle with repair costs, leading to selective compliance.

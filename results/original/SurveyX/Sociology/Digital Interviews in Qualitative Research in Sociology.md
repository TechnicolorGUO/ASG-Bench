# Abstract

This survey paper systematically examines the evolution, methodological implications, and applications of digital interviews in qualitative research, with a focus on sociological and interdisciplinary contexts. Tracing the transition from traditional face-to-face methods to digital platforms, we analyze how synchronous (e.g., video calls) and asynchronous (e.g., chat-based) techniques enhance accessibility, ecological validity, and cost-effectiveness while introducing new challenges in data security, participant equity, and methodological rigor. The paper evaluates theoretical foundations rooted in interpretative phenomenological analysis and digital ethnography, alongside practical considerations for maintaining reliability and validity in virtual data collection. Through case studiesparticularly from software engineering researchwe demonstrate digital interviews’ effectiveness in studying geographically dispersed populations and digitally mediated behaviors. Critical challenges include technological barriers, representativeness biases, and ethical dilemmas in digital consent procedures. Emerging directions explore AIassisted analysis and hybrid methodologies, positioning digital interviews as both innovative research tools and objects of study in understanding technology’s role in social inquiry. The survey concludes by outlining future research priorities, including standardized quality criteria for digital formats and the epistemological implications of computational qualitative analysis.

# 1 Introduction

The advent of digital technologies has fundamentally transformed various research methodologies, particularly in qualitative research. Among these transformations, the evolution of interview methods stands out as a significant development, reshaping how researchers collect and analyze data. Traditional face-to-face interviews, while rich in context and depth, often faced logistical and geographical constraints that limited their applicability in diverse settings. The emergence of digital platforms has not only alleviated these constraints but has also introduced new dimensions to qualitative inquiry. This review paper aims to explore the evolution, significance, objectives, and structure of digital interviews, providing a comprehensive understanding of their impact on contemporary research practices.

# 1.1 Evolution of Interview Methods

The evolution of interview methods from traditional face-to-face interactions to digital platforms reflects broader technological and methodological shifts in qualitative research. Early qualitative research predominantly relied on in-person interviews, which provided rich contextual data but were constrained by geographical and logistical limitations [1]. The advent of digital technologies, particularly video conferencing tools and asynchronous communication platforms, has enabled researchers to transcend these barriers, facilitating remote data collection while maintaining the depth of qualitative inquiry [1]. This transition has not only expanded the reach of qualitative research but has also introduced new challenges that require careful consideration of methodological rigor.

# 2.1 Theoretical Foundations of Digital Interviews

Digital interviews ground themselves in qualitative research traditions, particularly narrative construction, participant agency, and interpretative analysis, drawn from phenomenological and constructivist paradigms. These approaches prioritize co-creation of meaning with researchers and participants, a principle sustained in their digital evolution [1]. Digital platforms encourage rich narrative interaction and maintain the interpretive flexibility crucial for understanding human experiences through technology. As digital interviews proliferate, examining their theoretical foundations ensures alignment with qualitative research’s evolving nature.

Digital methodologies are underpinned by three central theoretical approaches: interpretative phenomenological analysis (IPA), narrative analysis, and discourse analysis. IPA’s emphasis on lived experience thrives in digital environments, encouraging openness in familiar virtual spaces [1]. Narrative analysis benefits from digital tools facilitating detailed recording and repetitive study of storytelling, while discourse analysis adapts to analyze technologically mediated communications. These frameworks collectively uphold qualitative researchs depth and contextuality, even as interactions are digitized. Researchers examine not only interview content but also digital platforms’ roles in shaping interactions and resultant data characteristics.

The theoretical framework extends to digital ethnography, where digital interviews complement broad observational studies of online social dynamics, aligning with qualitative principles like thick description and reflexivity via advanced technology. Utilizing large-scale generative language models (LLMs) allows simulation of open-ended interview responses, mirroring traditional qualitative analyses. Algorithmic fidelity evaluates how closely LLM outputs reflect actual human sentiments. By modeling interviews with virtual participants resembling human demographics, researchers identify key thematic parallels and discern differences in interview structures and tones, highlighting LLMs’ potential in qualitative research. Ensuring rigorous evaluations and diverse representation in future studies is crucial [1, 1]. Theoretical considerations also examine platform influences on power dynamics within researcher-participant interactions and how digital artifacts, like chat logs or video records, expand traditional qualitative data definitions. Future theoretical explorations may address reconciling digital methods’ scalability with qualitative researchs idiographic emphasis, possibly via hybrid analytical frameworks.

# 2.2 Technological Enablers of Digital Interviews

Digital interviews’ proliferation in qualitative research owes much to advancements in communication technologies and digital recording infrastructure. Video conferencing platforms are pivotal, facilitating synchronous interactions that closely mimic face-to-face interviews without geographical constraints [1]. These platforms offer essential features, like high-quality audio/video transfer, visual stimulus presentation, and recording functions, enhancing qualitative research’s methodological integrity by faithfully capturing participant nuances.

Asynchronous communication technologies, such as email, chat interfaces, and specialized qualitative software, expand methodological scope by addressing coordination challenges and allowing thoughtful participant responses. Digital devices and cloud storage revolutionize data management, ensuring secure preservation and resource sharing across research teams [1]. Automated transcription integration streamlines workflows, despite accuracy variances necessitating researcher verification, prompting scrutiny of data reliability and validity.

Emerging technologies include mobile-optimized platforms expanding accessibility across socioeconomic spectrums and qualitative analysis software supporting digital interview data coding and thematic analysis. These innovations dismantle traditional research participation barriers but also raise considerations about technological literacy and equitable digital tool access. This is especially pertinent as qualitative approaches, essential for understanding human behavior, gain momentum in fields like software engineering that traditionally prioritize quantitative methods. Future technological developments may incorporate AI for enhanced real-time interaction analysis during digital interviews, balancing automation with qualitative research’s interpretive essence.

# 3.1 Digital Interviews and Their Variants

Digital interviews utilize digital platforms for qualitative data collection, categorized into synchronous and asynchronous methods, each offering distinct advantages and considerations for sociological research [1]. Synchronous interviews, conducted in real-time via video conferencing or instant messaging, preserve the immediacy and interaction dynamics of traditional interviews while overcoming geographical barriers. These methods enable spontaneous dialogue and nonverbal cue interpretation, crucial for studies requiring in-depth participant engagement. The immediacy of these interactions often results in richer data, as participants respond in real-time, allowing for nuanced exploration of their thoughts and feelings.

Asynchronous methods, using email, forums, or voice messages, allow participants to respond at their convenience, offering temporal flexibility that accommodates diverse schedules and encourages reflective responses [1]. This approach is particularly effective for studying populations with limited availability or across time zones, and for sensitive topics where participants benefit from extended response formulation periods. The ability to craft thoughtful responses can yield deeper insights into complex issues, making this method invaluable for certain research contexts.

Hybrid models, combining synchronous and asynchronous elements, such as initial video interviews followed by text-based exchanges, exemplify methodological innovation in digital qualitative research. Platform-specific variants, including mobile-optimized interfaces and specialized software integrating interview recording with analytical tools, illustrate the shift towards methodological diversification in digital sociology. This trend aligns with recognizing the importance of human experiences in fields like software engineering, where diverse qualitative methods are increasingly employed. By integrating techniques like interpretative phenomenological analysis, narrative analysis, and discourse analysis, researchers maintain qualitative principlesdepth, contextuality, and interpretive rigorwhile ensuring research reflects diverse experiences. The emergence of large language models (LLMs) in qualitative research highlights the need for rigorous evaluation of algorithmic fidelity, as researchers navigate complexities of generalizing AI-generated data to human populations. These advancements underscore the ongoing evolution of qualitative methodologies, emphasizing reflexivity and adherence to quality assurance frameworks [1, 1].

# 3.3 Comparative Analysis with Traditional Methods

Digital and traditional face-to-face interviews offer distinct methodological approaches in qualitative research, each with unique advantages and limitations influencing their suitability for different contexts. Comparative analysis reveals differences in interaction dynamics, data quality, and logistical considerations shaping their applications in sociological inquiry [1]. Traditional interviews capture nuanced nonverbal cues and foster immediate rapport, while digital methods provide accessibility and flexibility in recruitment and data collection. Understanding these differences is crucial for designing studies aligned with research goals and participant demographics.

Three dimensions differentiate these approaches: ecological validity, participant-researcher dynamics, and methodological scalability. Traditional interviews occur in controlled settings, potentially limiting ecological validity, whereas digital interviews often take place in participants’ natural environments, particularly for studying technology-mediated behaviors [1]. Participant-researcher dynamics transform in digital contexts, where technological mediation can reduce social desirability biases and introduce new power asymmetries. Methodological scalability favors digital approaches, enabling cost-effective studies across boundaries while potentially compromising depth through reduced sensory channels. These dimensions illustrate trade-offs in selecting qualitative methods, emphasizing context’s importance in guiding choices.

Digital interviews offer enhanced accessibility for dispersed or mobility-limited populations, reduced logistical burdens, and the ability to study digital-native behaviors in their context. These advantages are valuable in fields like software engineering, where digital methods facilitate studies of distributed teams and online collaboration patterns [1]. Conversely, traditional interviews maintain advantages in building rapport, interpreting nonverbal cues, and controlling environmental variables influencing data quality. Method choice involves trade-offs, with hybrid approaches emerging as potential solutions. As researchers adapt to the changing landscape, integrating digital and traditional methodologies may offer comprehensive understanding of complex social phenomena.

Technological limitations are critical differentiators, as digital interviews rely on stable internet and technological competence, potentially introducing sampling biases. Traditional methods avoid these barriers but face challenges in recruiting diverse populations across distances [1]. Future development may focus on optimizing hybrid models combining digital and traditional elements, leveraging strengths while mitigating limitations. This evolution reflects trends in qualitative methodology towards adaptive, context-sensitive designs acknowledging technological mediation and human interaction’s interplay. The ongoing dialogue between methodologies will likely shape qualitative research’s future, encouraging innovative approaches prioritizing rigor and responsiveness to participant needs.

# 4.1 Methodological Rigor in Digital Interview Settings

Ensuring methodological rigor in digital interviews involves strategic participant engagement and robust data quality assurance, given the technological mediation in these methods. Transitioning from traditional to digital formats introduces challenges such as disrupted rapport-building and variable data collection conditions [1]. Researchers must adapt to preserve narrative richness despite the lack of physical presence. Structured protocols are essential to adapt qualitative quality criteriacredibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmabilityto virtual contexts, ensuring the depth and authenticity of participant responses.

Effective participant engagement in digital settings requires compensating for the absence of physical presence and mitigating technological distractions. In qualitative software engineering, platformspecific strategies like pre-interview technical sessions and icebreakers help establish rapport [1]. These techniques counteract screen fatigue and interface unfamiliarity, enhancing interaction quality. Maintaining conversational flow is crucial; synchronous interviews benefit from visual stimuli and active listening, while asynchronous methods rely on prompts that elicit detailed responses.

Data quality assurance in digital interviews involves multi-layered validation addressing technological and methodological aspects. Technical controls include equipment checks and backup systems for connectivity issues. Methodologically, reflexivity is crucial, requiring documentation of how platform features influence data [1]. Adaptive research designs balance standardization with flexibility across digital environments. Contingency protocols for technical failures, ethical guidelines for digital data handling, and rigorous transcription verification processes ensure validity and reliability [1, 1]. # 4.2 Ensuring Reliability and Validity in Virtual Data Collection

Reliability and validity in virtual data collection demand methodological adaptations to address digital interview challenges while preserving qualitative research integrity. Digital mediation introduces reliability (consistency) and validity (accuracy) threats, necessitating specialized verification techniques [1]. In fields like software engineering, qualitative methods must match quantitative robustness while accommodating digital constraints [1]. Researchers must innovate methodologies to ensure credible and applicable findings.

Four strategies enhance reliability: protocol standardization, technological redundancy, intercoder verification, and longitudinal consistency checks. Protocols tailored to digital platforms account for interaction dynamics across formats [1]. Technological redundancy, through simultaneous recording methods, mitigates data loss. Intercoder verification ensures interpretation consistency, and longitudinal checks enable data reexamination to identify contradictions or evolving perspectives.

Validity in digital interviews employs triangulation of multimodal data. [1] emphasizes analyzing verbal, textual, and paraverbal cues for comprehensive interpretations. Member checking, facilitated digitally, enhances interpretation accuracy. Digital interviews’ ecological validity benefits from studying behaviors in virtual environments, though platform affordances’ influence on responses must be considered [1].

Innovations address digital-specific validity threats, like algorithmic bias in transcription or interfaceinduced patterns. Platform-aware coding frameworks and metadata-assisted interpretation differentiate technological artifacts from substantive content [1]. Future developments may integrate computational linguistics to analyze large digital corpora while maintaining qualitative validity, bridging qualitative depth and quantitative scale in virtual methodologies. These advancements promise enhanced rigor and applicability in an increasingly digital research landscape.

# 6.1 Ethical and Practical Considerations

Digital interviews present distinct ethical dilemmas and practical challenges that necessitate careful consideration to ensure participant protection and research integrity. As outlined by [1], obtaining informed consent in virtual environments and maintaining robust data security protocols are primary ethical concerns. Unlike traditional interviews, digital settings require innovative consent processes to verify participant understanding, especially for vulnerable populations or cross-cultural samples.

This shift necessitates clear communication strategies to effectively convey participation implications in a digital context.

Data security is a critical challenge, as digital interviews generate electronic records susceptible to unauthorized access. The distributed nature of virtual data collection expands potential attack surfaces and complicates compliance with data protection regulations [1]. Solutions include end-toend encryption, secure cloud storage, and systematic procedures for data anonymization, balancing security needs with preserving contextual data essential for qualitative analysis. These measures ensure data richness without compromising participant confidentiality.

Practical challenges involve technological accessibility and equity considerations, potentially excluding certain populations from research participation. The digital divide manifests through disparities in internet connectivity, device quality, and platform literacy, biasing samples toward technologically privileged groups [1]. Researchers must implement inclusive protocols, offer multiple platform options, and provide technical support to mitigate these barriers while maintaining methodological consistency. Collaborations with community organizations can enhance outreach efforts to underrepresented groups, enriching the diversity of perspectives captured through digital interviews.

Emerging ethical considerations include managing digital traces beyond formal interview recordings, such as metadata containing sensitive information. The blurring of public-private boundaries in digital spaces complicates consent processes, especially in semi-public online communities. Future frameworks may need to address artificial intelligence tools in digital interviews, including automated analysis of behavior patterns and ethical use of predictive algorithms during interviews. These developments require continuous methodological reflection to ensure digital interviews adhere to ethical standards and harness their research potential. Recent studies highlight the algorithmic fidelity of outputs generated by large language models (LLMs) in qualitative research, questioning the validity of insights from artificial "silicon participants" and their generalizability to human populations.

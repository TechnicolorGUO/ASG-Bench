# 2 Background and Definitions

# 2.1 Foundations of Multilayer Networks

Multilayer networks represent a significant advancement in network science, extending beyond traditional single-layer models by incorporating multiple connectivity dimensions. These structures capture heterogeneous relationships across various layers, enabling a nuanced representation of complex systems, particularly in social networks where diverse affiliations shape community structures [1]. Interdependencies between layers introduce novel topological properties, revealing latent cognitive structures and behavioral patterns not evident in single-layer analyses [2]. Advanced tools like tensors and supra-adjacency matrices are employed to model these networks, accommodating dynamic interactions and addressing static model limitations [3]. The temporal dimension further differentiates multilayer networks, with continuous-time Markov processes modeling evolving relationships and community dynamics [4]. Challenges in scalability and parameter estimation, particularly in heterogeneous and evolving layers, must be addressed to advance the theoretical and practical applications in sociological research.

# 2.2 Key Definitions in Community Detection

Community detection in multilayer networks involves identifying cohesive groups of nodes with stronger intra-group connectivity across multiple layers [5]. This task is more complex than singlelayer approaches due to overlapping communities, reflecting the multidimensional nature of social affiliations [1]. Multilayer networks extend conventional graph representations by incorporating cross-layer dependencies, crucial for analyzing interconnected community structures in sociological contexts [3]. Maximum Likelihood methods address the challenges of parameter estimation in dynamic settings, essential for scalable community detection algorithms [4]. Multilayer stochastic block models refine community detection by capturing layer-specific patterns while preserving inter-layer correlations [6]. These models are applied in diverse areas, from social media discourse analysis [7] to quantifying segregation in opinion networks [8]. Future advancements should focus on developing scalable algorithms for large-scale multilayer systems.

# 2.3 Social Network Dynamics and Sociological Relevance

The dynamic nature of social networks is central to sociological research, capturing evolving interdependencies among individuals and groups [9]. Multilayer networks provide a robust framework for modeling these dynamics by integrating temporal and contextual dimensions, enabling the analysis of complex phenomena like opinion diffusion and collective behavior [10]. Social influence shapes network evolution, with individual behaviors influenced by both local and global structures. The coevolution of resource use and social feedback in common-pool systems exemplifies how multilayer networks model the tension between individual exploitation and collective sustainability [11]. Temporal networks refine this understanding by quantifying interaction stability and information flow through entropy-based measures [12]. Mesoscopic models combining mean-field and networkbased approaches demonstrate multilayer frameworks’ utility in simulating epidemic dynamics under varying social distancing policies [13]. Future research should refine these models to account for heterogeneous behaviors and cross-layer feedback, enhancing their applicability to diverse sociological contexts.

# 3.2 Cross-Layered Edge Clustering Coefficient (CLECC)

The Cross-Layered Edge Clustering Coefficient (CLECC) quantifies neighborhood similarity across multiple layers in multilayer networks, revealing communities obscured in single-layer analyses [5]. Unlike traditional coefficients, CLECC evaluates node neighborhood overlap across layers, capturing interdependencies reflecting multidimensional social affiliations [5]. This approach is significant in social networks where individuals maintain overlapping affiliations. CLECC computes neighbor set similarity for node pairs by comparing connectivity patterns across layers, effectively identifying overlapping community structures [5]. Applications include identifying latent cognitive structures and cohesive groups in dynamic social systems [2]. CLECC mitigates single-layer methods’ limitations by modeling cross-layer dependencies, enhancing community detection resolution in heterogeneous networks [5]. Future extensions could integrate CLECC with dynamic network models to improve scalability and adaptability to temporal changes.

# 3.3 Multilayer Stochastic Block Models

Multilayer stochastic block models (SBMs) incorporate layer-specific connectivity and inter-layer dependencies, offering a framework for community detection in complex networks. These models partition nodes into latent communities, accounting for heterogeneous interaction types across layers, suitable for social networks with diverse affiliations [2]. The Nonnegative Tucker Decomposition (NNTuck) method decomposes tensor representations of cognitive social structures into latent dimensions, revealing hidden patterns in multilayer networks [2]. Multilayer SBMs’ strengths include flexibility in modeling mixing patterns and capturing layer correlations through shared community assignments. However, scalability for large networks remains challenging due to computationally intensive inference methods [4]. Future directions involve integrating multilayer SBMs with reinforcement learning techniques to handle evolving structures and improving inference algorithms for large-scale systems [6].

# 3.4 Algorithmic Recommendation Mechanisms (ARM) and Influence Models

Algorithmic Recommendation Mechanisms (ARM) and influence models actively shape network structures to mitigate segregation and enhance inter-community connectivity [8].

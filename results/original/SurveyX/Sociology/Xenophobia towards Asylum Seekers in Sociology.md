# Abstract

This survey paper systematically examines the multifaceted dynamics of antiimmigrant attitudes, public perception of asylum seekers, and refugee integration within contemporary migration crises. Drawing on interdisciplinary perspectives from sociology, political science, and computational social science, it synthesizes structural, cultural, and policy dimensions to analyze how media narratives, economic anxieties, and cognitive biases shape hostility toward displaced populations. Key findings reveal the dual role of digital platforms in amplifying polarization while facilitating integration through transnational networks, as well as the contextual nature of xenophobia, which fluctuates with public health crises and labor market conditions. The paper highlights divergent outcomes in recent refugee crisesVenezuelan, Rohingya, and Ukrainiandemonstrating how geopolitical factors and policy frameworks influence reception and integration trajectories. Methodologically, it bridges qualitative insights with computational tools like sentiment analysis and predictive modeling to decode public discourse and forecast displacement patterns. The survey concludes with policy implications for mitigating prejudice through dynamic integration frameworks, hybrid forecasting systems, and participatory technology design, while calling for future research to address cross-cultural variations and longitudinal impacts of crisis-driven migration.

# 1 Introduction

The contemporary landscape of migration presents a complex tapestry of challenges and opportunities, particularly in the context of forced displacement. As global conflicts, persecution, and environmental crises escalate, the number of refugees and displaced persons has surged to unprecedented levels, demanding urgent attention from scholars, policymakers, and society at large. The dynamics of anti-immigrant attitudes and the integration of refugees are critical areas of inquiry, as they significantly influence the experiences of displaced individuals and the responses of host communities. This review aims to illuminate the multifaceted nature of these issues, drawing on interdisciplinary perspectives to provide a comprehensive understanding of the factors at play. Through a systematic examination of existing literature, the survey seeks to highlight gaps in knowledge and propose directions for future research that can inform both theory and practice in migration studies.

# 2 Background and Definitions

# 2.1 Core Conceptual Definitions

This survey is grounded in the precise definitions of key terms pivotal to understanding antiimmigrant attitudes and refugee crises. The refugee crisis involves large-scale forced displacement due to conflicts, persecution, or environmental disasters, demanding urgent humanitarian and policy interventions [10]. Public discourse often frames this phenomenon through narratives of threat and victimhood, as seen in the 2015 EU crisis, where debates polarized between "welcome culture" and border security. These narratives shape public perceptions and policy responses, influencing refugee treatment and societal attitudes toward immigration, with long-term implications for integration strategies and social cohesion.

Anti-immigrant attitudes refer to negative perceptions and hostility toward migrants, rooted in economic anxieties, cultural insecurities, or perceived threats to social cohesion [7]. These attitudes are often analyzed using the Stereotype Content Model (SCM), which categorizes biases along warmth and competence dimensions, revealing how displaced populations are dehumanized as either incompetent dependents or threatening outsiders [9]. SCM provides insights into the perpetuation of these stereotypes, aiding in the identification of prejudice roots and strategies to counteract them.

Prejudice and discrimination against refugees manifest as systemic or interpersonal biases limiting access to resources and opportunities. Prejudice stems from cognitive heuristics and in-group/outgroup dynamics, while discrimination involves institutional barriers in housing, employment, and legal status [8]. Crises exacerbate these phenomena, as seen with Venezuelan migrants in labor markets [11]. The interplay between prejudice and discrimination can create a cycle further marginalizing refugees, complicating their integration into host societies. Addressing these biases is crucial for fostering inclusive environments promoting social equity and cohesion.

Asylum policies are governmental frameworks balancing border control with international obligations under treaties like the 1951 Refugee Convention. These policies are influenced by public sentiment, significantly shaped by media coverage and political rhetoric. The European refugee crisis discourse and contrasting social media sentiments illustrate public opinion’s impact on policy outcomes [8, 6, 2]. Understanding the relationship between media narratives and policy implementation is vital, as is the potential for advocacy to promote humane asylum practices.

Social integration involves the multidimensional process by which refugees adapt to host societies, encompassing economic participation, cultural acclimatization, and supportive social networks [7]. Techniques like zero-shot information extraction applied to Finnish refugee interviews reveal integration pathways through granular data on hobbies and community ties [11]. Social integration’s complexity necessitates a multifaceted approach considering individual experiences, community dynamics, and institutional support. By examining these factors, researchers can identify best practices for enhancing integration and fostering resilience among refugees. These definitions provide the analytical framework for exploring the interplay between structural constraints, cultural narratives, and policy responses in migration studies.

# 2.2 Understanding the Refugee Crisis

The refugee crisis is a complex global challenge characterized by forced displacement due to conflicts, persecution, and environmental disruptions. The post-World War II era’s 1951 Refugee Convention established legal protections for displaced populations, yet contemporary crises have surpassed these frameworks in scale and complexity, with over 59.5 million refugees worldwidethe highest since World War II [12]. The Syrian conflict exemplifies the inadequacy of existing responses to protracted displacement, necessitating a reevaluation of international frameworks to handle modern displacement nuances.

Displacement’s structural drivers include armed conflicts, political instability, and climate-induced migration, disproportionately affecting low-resource regions. The Syrian and Rohingya crises highlight geopolitical tensions and ethnic persecution’s role in mass displacement, while the Venezuelan exodus underscores economic collapse’s impact [12]. These crises reveal systemic gaps in protection regimes, particularly for linguistically marginalized groups, such as the Baltic-Ukrainian translation gap impeding asylum access [13]. Addressing these issues is vital for developing comprehensive solutions accommodating displaced populations’ diverse needs.

Technological advancements offer tools for tracking and analyzing displacement patterns. Internet measurement data maps refugee movements and assesses host infrastructure impacts [14]. However, digital divides limit these tools’ utility in low-connectivity regions. Computational methods also identify stereotypes in public discourse, revealing how dehumanizing narratives perpetuate exclusionary policies [9]. Integrating technology enhances data accuracy and provides insights into societal perceptions shaping policy responses.

The global impact of refugee crises extends beyond humanitarian concerns, influencing host-country labor markets, social cohesion, and political landscapes. Economic anxieties often fuel antiimmigrant attitudes, as seen in Venezuelan migrants’ scapegoating in Brazil [12]. Future research must address displacement drivers, technological inequities, and policy design intersections to develop inclusive solutions. Emerging trends like climate migration and digital resettlement tools will redefine 21st-century refugee crises. Understanding these trends is essential for policymakers and scholars navigating migration complexities in an interconnected world.

# 3.1 Theoretical Frameworks in Migration Studies

Migration studies utilize diverse frameworks to analyze structural, cultural, and psychological factors shaping displacement and integration. Push-pull models, rooted in neoclassical economics, view migration as a rational response to opportunities, where economic disparities, political instability, or environmental stressors "push" individuals from origin countries, while perceived benefits "pull" them toward destinations [7]. Contemporary frameworks like transnationalism emphasize migrants’ sustained ties across borders, highlighting migration as a complex process influenced by social networks and cultural ties [1].

Evolutionary psychology, through the Behavioral Immune System (BIS) theory, suggests xenophobic attitudes arise as responses to perceived disease threats, particularly during crises like the COVID-19 pandemic [4]. Integrated Threat Theory expands this by identifying realistic threats (e.g., economic competition) and symbolic threats (e.g., cultural erosion) as drivers of anti-immigrant sentiment [4]. These frameworks intersect with computational approaches, such as the Stereotype Content Model (SCM), which maps biases along warmth-competence axes to reveal stereotypes of refugees as either incompetent dependents or threatening outsiders [9].

Agent-based modeling (ABM) offers a dynamic lens to examine acculturation, simulating how tolerance and reciprocity between migrants and locals influence social network formation and integration outcomes [5]. Digital frameworks like the Sentiment-enhanced Multidimensional Analysis Framework (SEMDAF) provide granular analysis of public discourse by integrating temporal, spatial, and sentiment dimensions, revealing how influential social media actors shape narratives about refugees. Machine translation and zero-shot learning techniques enhance cross-lingual political claim categorization, though challenges persist in multilingual embedding alignment [6].

Future research should integrate macro-level structural analyses with micro-level behavioral insights, particularly in non-Western contexts where transnational networks and digital divides intersect uniquely. Bridging these frameworks can develop comprehensive models accounting for migrants’ diverse experiences and integration influences, essential for informing effective policies and practices supporting migrant inclusion and belonging.

# 3.2 Structural and Cultural Lenses

Structural and cultural factors shape migration and integration processes, each offering distinct yet interrelated explanatory frameworks. Structurally, economic and political conditions influence migration feasibility, while cultural dimensions impact identity formation and social cohesion in host societies. The mini-cost flows model optimizes migration routes based on economic and logistical constraints, revealing how policy design can mitigate displacement inefficiencies [16]. The MigrAgent model integrates structural intake policies with cultural adaptation dynamics, simulating how reciprocity and tolerance thresholds between migrants and locals affect long-term integration outcomes [5].

Economic structures, particularly labor market segmentation, dictate refugee resettlement patterns. Host countries with flexible labor markets facilitate faster economic integration, whereas rigid markets exacerbate exclusion. Political structures, including asylum policies and border regimes, mediate access to rights and resources, creating stratified integration pathways. These barriers intersect with cultural narratives framing migrants as either economic assets or societal burdens, reinforcing systemic inequalities. The multimodal nature of refugee experiences underscores how cultural dimensions mediate structural constraints, enabling migrants to navigate hostile environments through informal networks and identity preservation strategies [1].

Cultural lenses emphasize identity negotiation and normative conflicts in integration. Acculturation theories categorize assimilation, separation, integration, and marginalization strategies, each influencing interaction dynamics between migrant and local populations. Agent-based simulations demonstrate that societal tolerance levels lead to varying acculturation outcomes, such as coexistence among different subgroups, particularly under differing migrant intake conditions. Rapid intake may exacerbate polarization, while slower intake could facilitate transitional acculturation experiences [8, 5, 9, 2].

Host societies with inclusive cultural norms and robust civic engagement mechanisms foster integration, while exclusionary national identities amplify xenophobic attitudes. Digital platforms amplify these dynamics by enabling diasporic cultural preservation but also facilitating echo chambers polarizing host-migrant relations. Future research should bridge structural and cultural analyses, particularly in understudied contexts where informal economies and transnational networks dominate migration governance. Emerging computational methods, such as hybrid predictive models, offer promising tools to quantify the interplay between structural policies and cultural adaptation trajectories, aiding policymakers in developing targeted interventions for cohesive societies.

# 3.3 Technology and Migration

Technology in migration encompasses opportunities and challenges, reshaping how refugees navigate displacement, integration, and public discourse. Digital platforms serve as critical tools for communication, enabling migrants to maintain transnational ties, access information, and mobilize support networks. Social media and messaging apps facilitate real-time coordination during journeys, as evidenced in the 2015 European refugee crisis, but also perpetuate digital divides with marginalized groups facing barriers due to limited connectivity, language proficiency, or algorithmic biases [2, 6].

Computational methods are powerful instruments for analyzing migration patterns and public sentiment. Sentiment-enhanced frameworks, like SEMDAF, leverage temporal and spatial data to decode polarized narratives about refugees across social media, revealing how influential actors amplify threat perceptions or humanitarian appeals [10]. Machine learning techniques enable cross-lingual analysis of political claims, though challenges persist in aligning multilingual embeddings for lowresource languages, as seen in Baltic-Ukrainian machine translation gaps [13]. Zero-shot information extraction methods, applied to Finnish refugee interviews, demonstrate how granular data on social connections and hobbies can inform integration policies [11].

Technology introduces risks, including surveillance and misinformation. Predictive models forecast displacement trends but raise ethical concerns about data privacy and algorithmic determinism [12]. Internet measurement tools, while mapping refugee flows through digital footprints, may exclude populations with limited online access, exacerbating inequities in humanitarian response [14]. Digital dehumanization compounds these challenges, with computational analyses identifying pervasive stereotypes framing refugees as threats or burdens [9].

Innovative solutions, like language-learning apps and online vocational training, offer pathways to mitigate integration barriers. Their efficacy depends on addressing structural inequities, including broadband access and digital literacy. Future research must prioritize participatory design, ensuring technologies align with migrants’ needs while countering exclusionary narratives. Emerging trends, such as blockchain for identity verification and AI-driven legal aid, hold promise but require rigorous evaluation to balance scalability with ethical safeguards. The intersection of technology and migration demands interdisciplinary collaboration to harness its potential while mitigating its risks, ensuring technological advancements contribute positively to the migration experience.

# 4.1 Media and Public Perception

Media narratives significantly influence public attitudes toward asylum seekers by framing them as either threats or victims. The DebateNet2.0 corpus illustrates how political discourse polarizes around "welcome culture" versus border security, affecting policy and public perception [2]. Media often emphasizes crisis rhetoric, sidelining humanitarian perspectives, as seen in discussions about the Rohingya community [15]. Computational analyses reveal media biases, with a focus on security and economic concerns over human rights [6]. Internet measurement data shows how media coverage correlates with public sentiment shifts, as observed during the 2015 refugee crisis [14]. This framing simplifies migration dynamics, omitting contextual factors like transnational networks, thereby distorting public understanding and affecting asylum seekers’ integration.

Media not only sets agendas but also constructs social realities, where dehumanizing language normalizes exclusionary attitudes. Sentiment analysis shows a feedback loop between media framing and public anxiety, especially when economic or cultural changes are politicized [7]. Future research should use multimodal datasets to explore visual and textual framing interplay, while interventions should promote counter-narratives that emphasize refugee agency. Cross-lingual claim categorization tools can audit media biases across contexts, fostering more informed and empathetic discourse.

# 4.2 Economic and Cultural Drivers of Negative Attitudes

Economic insecurity and cultural differences drive anti-immigrant sentiments, often intersecting to heighten hostility toward asylum seekers. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted this, with increased xenophobia linked to economic anxieties and health fears [4]. Labor market competition exacerbates tensions, as refugees are scapegoated for economic challenges. Media narratives amplify these concerns, as seen in the German Tageszeitung corpus, which frames immigration through security and economic lenses [2]. Cultural barriers, such as language and social norms, further compound economic insecurities, fostering identity conflicts and exclusion [15]. Machine translation gaps, like those in Baltic-Ukrainian systems, hinder integration by limiting service access [13]. Addressing these barriers requires considering both economic and cultural dimensions to foster inclusive attitudes.

Public sentiment analysis shows how attitudes fluctuate with economic or security crises [10]. This volatility reflects how structural inequalities and cultural polarization reinforce exclusionary policies. Future research should disentangle these interdependencies, using cross-national comparisons of labor market integration and cultural adaptation programs. Sentiment-enhanced frameworks offer avenues to quantify economic and cultural drivers’ interactions, informing targeted interventions that address negative attitudes’ root causes.

Digital technology also supports constructive engagement, as refugees use social media for language acquisition and communication [17]. # 5 Xenophobia and Anti-Immigrant Attitudes

Xenophobia and anti-immigrant attitudes are deeply rooted in psychological mechanisms that manifest as discrimination, social exclusion, and violence. Xenophobia involves irrational fear or hatred of those perceived as foreign, while anti-immigrant attitudes specifically target immigrants, driven by cultural, economic, and political factors. These attitudes impact social cohesion and inform policy, necessitating an understanding of their origins. The Behavioral Immune System (BIS) theory provides a framework for examining xenophobia, particularly in response to perceived disease threats.

# 5.1 The Behavioral Immune System and Disease Threat

The BIS theory, grounded in evolutionary psychology, suggests that xenophobic attitudes are adaptive responses to perceived disease threats, especially during health crises. This mechanism predisposes individuals to avoid out-group members, associating unfamiliarity with pathogen risks. During the COVID-19 pandemic, heightened disease threat perceptions correlated with increased anti-immigrant sentiment, scapegoating migrants as infection vectors [4]. The BIS thus elucidates the psychological underpinnings of xenophobia, exacerbated during crises.

BIS operates via cognitive heuristics and affective responses. Cognitive heuristics simplify social environments, leading to generalized negative perceptions of out-groups. Stereotyping, as a low-cost categorization mechanism, reinforces exclusionary attitudes without direct contact [18]. Opinion dynamics models reveal trust asymmetries within social networks, amplifying distrust toward migrant subgroups [19]. Digital discourse exacerbates these biases, with algorithmic amplification of negative narratives, such as those targeting the Rohingya, intensifying perceived threats [15].

Empirical evidence indicates BIS-driven xenophobia fluctuates with disease prevalence and media framing. Computational analyses show public sentiment shifts during health crises, with contamination anxiety markers directed at migrants [4]. Mitigating these effects requires interventions decoupling disease threats from out-group stigmatization, emphasizing shared vulnerability and cooperation. Future research should explore BIS activation variations across cultures and its interaction with structural inequalities, especially in digital environments where threat perceptions are algorithmically amplified.

# 5.2 Stereotypes and Cognitive Heuristics

Stereotypes and cognitive heuristics simplify social realities, perpetuating negative immigrant perceptions through oversimplified categorizations. Social identity theory posits that self-esteem from in-group affiliations leads to out-group denigration [19]. This fosters intergroup conflict, as host communities categorize refugees along threat or incompetence dimensions, reinforcing exclusionary attitudes.

The evolutionary origins of stereotyping, as low-cost proxies for assessing out-group behavior, enable rapid judgments under uncertainty [18]. In migration contexts, these shortcuts manifest as assumptions about refugee criminality and economic dependency, persisting despite contrary evidence. The Stereotype Content Model maps refugee perceptions along warmth-competence axes, predicting discrimination patterns [9]. Refugees are stereotyped as high-warmth, low-competence dependents, eliciting paternalistic treatment.

Countering stereotypes requires addressing cognitive and systemic roots. Effective anti-stereotypes are challenging to generate due to their ill-defined nature in public discourse [9]. Interventions should combine counter-stereotypical narratives with structural reforms, like anonymized hiring or community integration programs. Future research should use computational tools to identify stereotype reinforcement patterns and develop debiasing strategies considering cross-cultural variations.

# 5.3 In-Group/Out-Group Dynamics and Systemic Biases

In-group/out-group dynamics significantly shape anti-immigrant attitudes, reinforcing boundaries between host communities and refugees, exacerbating systemic biases. Perceived differences in norms and resource access amplify social conflict and discrimination [19]. This categorization influences individual attitudes and societal narratives around immigration policies.

Stereotyping mechanisms entrench these divisions, as cognitive load or economic crises intensify heuristic reliance [18]. Under scarcity, host communities attribute negative traits to refugees, reinforcing systemic biases in policy and discourse. Structural inequalities limit refugees’ access to resources, reinforcing perceptions of them as burdens. Computational analyses reveal institutionalized biases in asylum policies, reflecting assumptions about refugee criminality [6].

Systemic biases manifest in digital spaces, where algorithmic curation amplifies polarizing narratives framing refugees as incompatible with host societies. The absence of counter-narratives perpetuates divisions, marginalizing pro-refugee voices [15]. Future research should explore interventions disrupting these dynamics, such as contact-based programs or policy reforms reducing structural barriers. Addressing tensions requires cognitive debiasing strategies and systemic changes for equitable resource access, fostering social cohesion and inclusivity.

# 6.1 Challenges in Housing and Resettlement

Housing is a fundamental barrier to refugee integration, with systemic constraints worsening displacement trauma and hindering stability. Urban housing scarcity, restrictive zoning, and discrimination create precarious resettlement landscapes [7]. U.S. resettlement agencies face constraints like proximity to services, often unattainable due to market shortages and inefficiencies [20]. Spatial mismatches isolate refugees from services and jobs, perpetuating poverty and exclusion. Housing inadequacies affect social cohesion and integration, necessitating comprehensive policy responses.

Semi-structured refugee housing data complicates policy responses, as qualitative insights from surveys reveal overlooked social connections and hobbies [11]. Informal networks, often digital, help navigate housing markets through diaspora knowledge-sharing [17]. However, digital divides exclude older or low-literacy refugees. Inconsistent tracking mechanisms obscure real-time housing needs and mobility [14], hindering effective policy and perpetuating vulnerabilities.

Resettlement programs face tensions between short-term humanitarian and long-term integration goals. Temporary housing lacks pathways to permanence, trapping refugees in limbo. Host community resistance, such as NIMBYism, limits options. Future interventions should integrate computational housing stock tracking with ethnographic insights into refugees’ social capital. This approach can inform spatially equitable policies using predictive models like the Gary Verhulst Model and comprehensive discourse analyses [2, 10, 12]. AI-assisted housing systems could alleviate administrative bottlenecks while preserving refugee agency.

# 6.2 Economic and Labor Market Integration

Refugee labor market integration is complex, influenced by structural barriers, policy constraints, and host attitudes. The Venezuelan influx into Brazil illustrates this, with Roraima’s formal sector seeing a $2 \%$ wage rise without job loss, challenging competition assumptions [3]. Outcomes depend on regional structures and policies affecting employment access. Migration models often miss localized variations, limiting predictive utility [16]. Understanding these factors is crucial for effective labor policies.

Barriers include credential gaps, language requirements, and discrimination, pushing refugees into informal work. The MigrAgent simulation shows conservative hosts and slow policies deepen labor segmentation by lowering reciprocity [5]. These insights align with Finnish refugee interviews, where LLMs reveal social capital from hobbies aiding informal job access [11]. These barriers exclude refugees from meaningful opportunities.

Policy must address supply and demand constraints. Predictive models like the Logistic RegressionGray Verhulst hybrid offer data-driven strategies by forecasting flows and capacities [12].

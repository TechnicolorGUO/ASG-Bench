# Abstract

This survey paper provides a comprehensive analysis of analytical methods for antioxidant measurement, encompassing spectrophotometric assays (DPPH, ABTS, FRAP), electrochemical sensors, HPLC-based quantification, and the ORAC test. These techniques are critically evaluated for their principles, protocols, and limitations in assessing antioxidant activity, free radical scavenging capacity, and antioxidant content across pharmaceuticals, food, and biological samples. Spectrophotometric methods offer simplicity and cost-effectiveness but lack physiological relevance, while electrochemical techniques enable real-time monitoring yet face selectivity challenges. HPLC delivers high specificity for compound profiling but involves operational complexity, and the ORAC assay excels in kinetic physiological relevance but requires stringent standardization. Emerging advancements, such as spectral single-molecule localization microscopy (sSMLM) and hybrid platforms, are discussed to address current limitations. The paper underscores the importance of method selection based on application-specific needs and highlights ongoing standardization efforts to enhance reproducibility. Future directions emphasize multi-modal integration, nanomaterials, and computational approaches to advance antioxidant measurement accuracy and applicability in research and industry.

# 2 Background and Definitions

# 2.1 Key Terms and Definitions

Antioxidants are vital compounds that protect biomolecules from oxidative damage by neutralizing reactive oxygen species (ROS) and free radicals, thereby preventing cellular injury and supporting cellular health. Beyond cellular protection, antioxidants are crucial in physiological processes such as immune modulation and cellular signaling regulation [1, 2]. Free radicals, including hydroxyl radicals (·OH) and superoxide anions $\left( \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { - } \right)$ , are highly reactive due to their unstable molecular structure and unpaired electrons, initiating chain reactions that cause cellular damage and oxidative stress. Understanding the dynamics of these radicals is essential, particularly through advanced techniques like single-molecule microscopy, which offer insights into their behavior and effects on cellular systems [1, 2].

Antioxidant activity is defined by a substance’s ability to scavenge free radicals or chelate prooxidant metal ions, assessed through assays like DPPH (2, 2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl) and ABTS $( 2 , 2 ^ { \prime }$ -azinobis-3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid), which are essential for quantifying antioxidant efficacy in various matrices. Oxidative damage results from ROS overproduction, damaging lipids, proteins, and DNA, linked to aging and chronic diseases. Distinguishing between antioxidant content (concentration) and capacity (efficacy) is crucial, as capacity depends on reaction kinetics and redox potentials, affecting their biological function. Electrochemical methods measure antioxidant capacity via redox potentials $( E ^ { \circ } )$ and electron transfer rates, while chromatographic techniques like HPLC separate and identify antioxidants by retention times and spectral signatures. The ORAC assay evaluates radical scavenging kinetics through fluorescence decay, reflecting antioxidant efficacy under physiological conditions. These definitions highlight the methodological diversity in antioxidant analysis, necessitating context-specific standardization.

Understanding these terms is crucial for appreciating the broader implications of antioxidant research. The interaction between antioxidants and oxidative stress has significant health and disease consequences, emphasizing the need for rigorous measurement methodologies. As research progresses, sophisticated techniques will continue to refine our understanding of antioxidant mechanisms and applications in preventing oxidative damage. This foundational knowledge paves the way for exploring the relevance of antioxidant measurement across various fields, discussed in the following subsection.

# 2.2 Relevance of Antioxidant Measurement

Antioxidant capacity measurement is significant across disciplines due to antioxidants’ role in mitigating oxidative stress and related pathologies. In pharmaceuticals, antioxidant assays evaluate drug stability, shelf-life, and therapeutic efficacy, especially for compounds prone to oxidative degradation, ensuring chemical integrity and clinical effectiveness [1, 2]. Assays like DPPH and ABTS are standard for screening compounds for radical scavenging potential, aiding drug discovery and formulation optimization. Electrochemical methods, with real-time capabilities, are increasingly used to assess antioxidant interactions in biological matrices, providing insights into redox mechanisms at physiological potentials, underscoring robust measurement methodologies’ importance in drug quality and efficacy.

In the food industry, antioxidant measurement is crucial for quality control, shelf-life prediction, and nutritional labeling. HPLC-based quantification precisely determines phenolic acids, flavonoids, and bioactive compounds, ensuring regulatory compliance and meeting consumer demand for healthpromoting products. The ORAC assay, despite its complexity, accurately reflects antioxidant efficacy under physiological conditions, guiding dietary recommendations and functional food development [1, 2]. The rise of electrochemical sensors for on-site analysis of antioxidant-rich products, like wines and oils, reflects the industry’s shift toward efficient testing methods, essential for responding to consumer trends and ensuring product quality.

Biological and clinical applications emphasize the diagnostic and prognostic value of antioxidant capacity measurements. Oxidative stress biomarkers, quantified via spectrophotometric or chromatographic methods, correlate with cardiovascular diseases, neurodegenerative disorders, and cancer. Differentiating endogenous and exogenous antioxidant contributions is crucial for understanding disease mechanisms and evaluating therapeutic interventions, allowing pinpointing pathways influencing oxidative stress and cellular responses [1, 2]. Emerging trends include multi-method approaches, like combining HPLC with mass spectrometry, enhancing antioxidant profiling specificity and coverage. Challenges remain in assay standardization across matrices, necessitating harmonization for reproducibility and comparability. Future directions may leverage machine learning for predictive modeling of antioxidant behavior, alongside advancements in nanomaterial-based sensors for high-throughput screening, enhancing understanding and applications of antioxidants in various fields, paving the way for innovative solutions to combat oxidative stress-related diseases.

# 3.1 DPPH, ABTS, and FRAP Assays

Spectrophotometric assays, including DPPH, ABTS, and FRAP, are widely used for assessing antioxidant activity due to their simplicity, reproducibility, and cost-effectiveness. The DPPH assay evaluates hydrogen atom or electron donation by monitoring the decolorization of the DPPH radical at $5 1 7 \mathrm { n m }$ , particularly suitable for lipophilic antioxidants but limited by interference from pigments and hydrophilic compounds. The ABTS assay generates a stable radical cation $( \mathrm { { A B T S } ^ { + } }$ ) to quantify scavenging activity at $7 3 4 \mathrm { n m }$ , compatible with both hydrophilic and lipophilic antioxidants, though influenced by $\mathrm { p H }$ and solvent conditions. The FRAP assay measures the reduction of $\mathrm { F e ^ { 3 + } }$ -TPTZ to $\mathrm { F e ^ { 2 + } }$ -TPTZ at $5 9 3 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , providing insights into electron-transfer mechanisms in redox reactions, though it underestimates antioxidants relying on radical adduct formation or hydrogen atom transfer.

Comparative studies highlight DPPH’s specificity for radical scavenging kinetics, ABTS’s pH adaptability, and FRAP’s efficacy in quantifying reductants like ascorbate. Stability tests underscore the importance of standardizing conditions to mitigate degradation artifacts. Limitations include the non-biological radicals in DPPH and ABTS and FRAP’s static endpoint measurement. Emerging adaptations integrate these assays with HPLC or electrochemical detection to enhance specificity and address interferences, expanding their utility in complex matrices.

In food science, FRAP quantifies reducing capacity in beverages and oils, correlating with phenolic content, while ABTS excels in complex matrices like spices and cereals. Electrochemical validation reveals discrepancies due to matrix effects, emphasizing the need for method-specific calibration for accurate measurements.

# 4.1 Principles of Electrochemical Detection

Electrochemical detection hinges on redox reactions where electron transfer between analytes and electrodes is measured through current, potential, or impedance signals, offering direct quantification of antioxidant capacity. The Nernst equation $\begin{array} { r } { E = E ^ { \circ } - { \frac { R T } { n F } } \ln \left( { \frac { a _ { r e d } } { a _ { o x } } } \right) } \end{array}$ provides a quantitative framework for analyzing the impact of redox-active species concentrations on electrode potential, influencing the study of the interactions between analytes and fluorescent emitters at hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) surfaces [1, 2]. Recent approaches in single-molecule imaging enhance spatiotemporal analysis at high resolutions, addressing traditional limitations of electrochemical techniques by offering precise monitoring of concentration gradients [1]. This integration offers superior spatial and temporal resolution for understanding heterogeneous electron transfer processes.

# 4.2 Types of Electrochemical Sensors

Electrochemical sensors for antioxidant detection distinguish themselves by their transduction mechanisms and electrode materials, each offering distinct benefits based on the application. Potentiometric sensors effectively detect ionic antioxidants like ascorbate and glutathione through potential measurement, aided by ion-selective membranes for heightened selectivity, even though they face challenges like slow response time and ionic interference [1, 2]. Amperometric sensors measure current at a fixed potential to monitor redox-active compounds such as polyphenols, with sensitivity improved by nanostructured electrodes enhancing surface area and electron transfer.

Voltammetric sensors, including cyclic voltammetry and differential pulse voltammetry (DPV), offer detailed redox profiling. CV helps decipher antioxidant mechanisms through peak potentials and currents, while DPV’s enhanced analytical resolution is beneficial for complex biological matrices due to reduced capacitive currents [1]. Impedimetric sensors provide a label-free detection method by measuring changes in charge transfer resistance upon binding antioxidants, suitable for extensive screening.

Emerging designs involving molecularly imprinted polymers (MIPs) and enzyme-modified electrodes advance molecular recognition. MIPs mimic natural interactions, selectively binding antioxidants, while enzyme-linked sensors amplify phenolic compound signals. Fouling from matrix components challenges these innovations; however, surface passivation and regenerative protocols enhance their longevity and functionality, heralding future advancements in wearable devices for ongoing antioxidant monitoring.

# 4.3 Sensitivity and Selectivity Challenges

Electrochemical techniques encounter sensitivity and selectivity limitations due to complex matrices and overlapping redox species. Techniques like cyclic voltammetry and amperometry face challenges in spatial resolution, obscuring localized reaction dynamics in biological matrices where similar electroactive compounds coexist. Sensitivity issues persist in matrices like plasma, necessitating detection capabilities down to micromolar or nanomolar ranges. Despite nanostructured electrodes enhancing sensitivity through increased surface area, they remain prone to biomolecule fouling, affecting signal stability [1].

Selectivity challenges arise in differentiating structurally similar antioxidants, such as flavonoids, due to overlapping redox potentials. Hybrid methods, integrating electrochemical sensors with spectroscopic techniques, address such issues, offering spatially resolved imaging of redox processes [1]. Enzyme-modified electrodes further improve selectivity though stability concerns remain. Future directions might involve machine learning for signal analysis and multi-sensor array development to enhance both sensitivity and selectivity.

# 4.4 Advancements in Electrochemical Monitoring

Recent advances have significantly bolstered the spatial and temporal resolution of electrochemical monitoring, allowing detailed studies of antioxidant redox dynamics. Integrating spectral singlemolecule localization microscopy (sSMLM) with electrochemical systems enables high-resolution imaging at the single-molecule level, improving understanding of redox processes in non-aqueous matrices [1]. Modifying electrodes with nanomaterials like graphene and MOFs amplifies sensitivity, while wearable electrochemical devices advance personal nutrition strategies through continuous antioxidant tracking [1, 2].

Machine learning aids in overcoming voltage overlap by recognizing patterns in voltammetric signals, enhancing analysis capabilities on reaction kinetics and concentrations. Future advancements may integrate microfluidics for high-throughput screening and explore quantum dot-based sensors for multiplexed profiling, extending electrochemical monitoring’s scope in diverse applications.

# 4.5 Applications in Pharmaceuticals and Food Analysis

In pharmaceuticals and food industries, electrochemical techniques are crucial for real-time and sensitive analysis. They evaluate drug stability, degradation kinetics, and formulation efficacy, crucial for drug development and quality control [1, 2]. For instance, CV and DPV analyze redox properties of APIs and excipients, whereas amperometric sensors detect antioxidants like ascorbic acid in formulations.

In food analysis, electrochemical sensors assess the quality and nutritional content, with voltammetric techniques measuring phenolic compounds in beverages and edible oils. Impedimetric sensors offer portable, minimal-preparation-required analysis for honey and dairy products [1]. Emerging applications, such as integrating electrochemical arrays with machine learning, enhance antioxidant profiling in functional foods and supplements, addressing long-standing challenges in standardizing materials and protocols across industries. Future endeavors may focus on wearable sensors for continuous monitoring, supporting personalized nutrition and oxidative stress management.

# 5.1 Principles of HPLC in Antioxidant Analysis

HPLC is essential for antioxidant analysis due to its resolution, reproducibility, and adaptability to diverse matrices. Separation in HPLC relies on differential partitioning between a stationary phase, typically C18 or C8 bonded silica, and a mobile phase of aqueous and organic solvents, driven by hydrophobic, polar, and ionic interactions. These interactions enable precise resolution of complex mixtures, as demonstrated in polyyne compound analysis using electronic absorption spectroscopy and advanced techniques [1, 2]. Reverse-phase HPLC (RP-HPLC) predominantly retains hydrophobic antioxidants like flavonoids and tocopherols, while hydrophilic compounds like ascorbic acid elute earlier.

Retention time $( t _ { R } )$ is influenced by the analyte’s partition coefficient $( K )$ , expressed as:

$$
t _ { R } = t _ { 0 } \left( 1 + K { \frac { V _ { s } } { V _ { m } } } \right)
$$

where $t _ { 0 }$ is the void time, $K$ is a proportionality constant, $V _ { s }$ is the surface voltage, and $V _ { m }$ is the maximum voltage threshold [1, 2]. Gradient elution enhances resolution for complex mixtures by sequentially eluting compounds of varying polarity. Advancements include core-shell particles and monolithic columns for efficiency and reduced analysis time, with structural modifications enhancing stability during separation [2]. Detection methods like diode-array detection (DAD) and electrochemical detection (ECD) offer simultaneous quantification and superior sensitivity for redox-active compounds. Challenges include column fouling and method optimization for co-eluting peaks, particularly in plant extracts and biological fluids, with future directions exploring multidimensional HPLC and machine learning-assisted method development.

# 5.2 Detection Methods for Antioxidants

HPLC employs various detection methods, including electrochemical and spectroscopic techniques, each offering distinct advantages tailored to specific applications in complex mixtures. HPLC separates and identifies compounds like polyyne chains through electronic absorption spectra and complementary techniques like FT-IR and Raman spectroscopy, essential for antioxidant profiling [1, 2]. UV-Vis detection, particularly DAD, is widely used for monitoring multiple wavelengths, identifying phenolic acids, flavonoids, and carotenoids based on absorption spectra. Electrochemical detection (ECD) provides superior sensitivity for redox-active antioxidants, with coulometric array detectors enhancing resolution through stepwise oxidation. Fluorescence detection (FLD) excels in quantifying fluorescent antioxidants or those with fluorescent tags, achieving sub-nanogram detection limits. LC-MS offers exceptional specificity and structural elucidation, identifying unknown antioxidants through fragmentation patterns [1, 2]. Tandem MS (MS/MS) enhances selectivity by monitoring specific ion transitions, crucial for complex biological matrices. Emerging techniques like charged aerosol detection (CAD) and chemiluminescence detection (CLD) offer universal response and sensitivity for radical-scavenging antioxidants [1, 2]. The choice of detection method depends on analyte properties, matrix complexity, and required sensitivity, with hybrid systems increasingly adopted for complementary strengths.

# 5.3 Quantification Strategies

Quantifying antioxidants with HPLC requires robust calibration strategies, internal standardization, and advanced data processing for accuracy and reproducibility, especially in complex matrices. Innovative methodologies, such as advanced spectroscopic techniques, enhance HPLC’s capabilities in determining antioxidant levels [1, 2]. External calibration involves standard curves using pure compounds, effective for simple matrices but requiring matrix-matched calibration or standard addition for complex samples. Internal standardization mitigates variability by spiking samples with structurally similar compounds, normalizing responses for injection volume inconsistencies and detector drift.

For multicomponent analysis, relative response factors (RRFs) estimate concentrations based on surrogate standards, useful for plant extracts with unidentified polyphenols. LC-MS offers absolute quantification using stable isotope-labeled internal standards (SIL-IS), compensating for ionization efficiency variations [1, 2]. Machine learning algorithms deconvolute overlapping peaks and predict retention times, enhancing throughput in untargeted metabolomics. Challenges include maintaining column performance and ensuring antioxidant stability, addressed through temperature control and antioxidant-preserving mobile phases [2]. Future directions may focus on real-time quantification via inline sensors and automated calibration systems for high-throughput applications.

# 5.5 Advantages and Limitations

HPLC offers high resolution and reproducibility for antioxidant analysis, capable of separating mixtures with varying polarities and molecular weights. Its versatility includes compatibility with diverse detection methods, such as DAD for UV-absorbing compounds and ECD for redox-active species, enabling tailored approaches [1, 2]. RP-HPLC effectively separates hydrophobic antioxidants, while HILIC broadens capabilities to polar compounds like ascorbic acid. LC-MS integration enhances specificity, allowing structural elucidation and absolute quantification.

Limitations include column fouling from complex matrices, requiring frequent regeneration or replacement, and reliance on expensive instrumentation and consumables, limiting accessibility for high-throughput or field applications. Labile antioxidants may degrade during analysis, necessitating stabilization strategies [2]. While HPLC offers greater specificity than spectrophotometric assays, its complexity and cost limit routine screening applications [1, 2]. Emerging solutions include miniaturized systems and automated algorithms, though standardization remains a challenge. Future advancements may focus on hybrid systems combining HPLC with electrochemical arrays or machine learning-driven peak deconvolution for enhanced throughput and robustness.

# 6.1 ORAC Assay

The ORAC assay measures antioxidant capacity through the inhibition of peroxyl radical-induced oxidation, observed using fluorescence decay kinetics. Employing the radical source $^ { 2 , 2 ^ { \circ } }$ -azobis(2- amidinopropane) dihydrochloride (AAPH), which consistently generates ROO· radicals, these radicals attack a fluorescent probe such as fluorescein, decreasing its fluorescence over time. Antioxidants in the sample delay this fluorescence decay, permitting precise analyte concentration monitoring through nanoscale imaging techniques. The assay calculates the antioxidant’s net protection via the area under the fluorescence decay curve (AUC) by:

$$
O R A C v a l u e = k \left( \frac { A U C _ { s a m p l e } - A U C _ { b l a n k } } { A U C _ { T r o l o x } - A U C _ { b l a n k } } \right) ,
$$

where $A U C _ { s a m p l e }$ , $A U C _ { b l a n k }$ , and $A U C _ { T r o l o x }$ represent the area under the curve for the sample, blank, and Trolox, respectively, with $k$ as the standardization constant. The assay simulates physiological radical chain reactions, providing a comprehensive antioxidant assessment beyond singletimepoint assays like DPPH or ABTS. Challenges include sensitivity to experimental conditions and scalability issues, which recent adaptations address through high-throughput formats and alternative probes. Comparative studies affirm ORAC’s superior correlation with cellular antioxidant activity, albeit with higher costs hindering routine use. Future approaches may enhance methodology through hybridization with chromatography and machine learning integration.

# 6.2 Comparison with Other Advanced Techniques

ORAC’s kinetic approach distinguishes it from other antioxidant measurement techniques, granting it enhanced physiological relevance. Unlike other techniques focused on static measurements, ORAC provides real-time antioxidant activity data. In contrast, fluorescence-based assays like the Cellular Antioxidant Activity (CAA) assay offer more biologically contextualized insight by assessing intracellular activity, despite labor-intensive protocols and limited scalability. Mass spectrometry-based techniques, particularly LC-MS/MS, afford exceptional specificity afoot detailed antioxidant profiling but lack ORAC’s kinetic insight, being more suited for mechanistic studies. Electrochemical techniques such as cyclic voltammetry enable real-time redox monitoring but falter in simulating biological relevance compared to ORAC. Emerging hybrid technologies seek to bridge gaps by integrating ORAC with separation or sensor systems, enhancing profiling accuracy and specificity.

# 6.3 Pros and Cons

ORAC, fluorescence-based, MS, and electrochemical antioxidant measurement techniques each possess distinct advantages and limitations. ORAC excels in capturing radical-induced oxidation kinetics with high sensitivity, crucial for physiological relevance, although hampered by stringent controls and lower throughput than rapid assays. Its composite output necessitates complementary analytical techniques for component-specific analysis. Fluorescence-based CAA assays enhance biological relevance, assessing intracellular antioxidants but struggle with scalability. MS methods enable precise antioxidant identification, essential for structural analyses, yet face limitations in static concentration assessments and accessibility for routine analysis. Electrochemical techniques offer portable and rapid redox process monitoring, although limited by non-biological mediators and potential matrix interference. Multimodal platforms integrating ORAC with chromatographic and machine learning elements aim to leverage the collective strengths of these techniques, focusing future advancements on miniaturization, automation, and standardization for increased reproducibility.

# 7.1 Method Comparison

Choosing the right antioxidant measurement method depends on application requirements such as sample type, sensitivity, throughput, and physiological relevance. Spectrophotometric assays like DPPH, ABTS, and FRAP are favored for their simplicity, cost-effectiveness, and high-throughput capabilities, allowing rapid assessment of antioxidant activity. DPPH is ideal for lipophilic antioxidants, ABTS is compatible with both hydrophilic and lipophilic compounds, and FRAP quantifies reducing capacity, making it suitable for samples rich in electron-donating antioxidants. However, these assays lack physiological relevance due to their use of non-biological radicals and static measurements.

Electrochemical techniques, such as cyclic voltammetry and amperometry, offer real-time monitoring of redox processes and high sensitivity to trace antioxidants, making them suitable for on-site analysis in food and pharmaceutical quality control. Advances like spectral single-molecule localization microscopy (sSMLM) enhance spatial resolution for tracking antioxidant interactions at the single-molecule level. Despite these strengths, electrochemical methods face selectivity challenges in complex matrices due to overlapping redox potentials.

HPLC-based quantification provides unmatched specificity and resolution for individual antioxidants, essential for profiling complex mixtures. Reverse-phase HPLC is effective for hydrophobic compounds, while hydrophilic interaction liquid chromatography (HILIC) is suitable for polar antioxidants. The technique’s compatibility with detection methods like diode-array detection and mass spectrometry broadens its applicability. However, HPLC’s complexity, cost, and lengthy method development limit its routine use.

The ORAC assay is notable for its physiological relevance, simulating peroxyl radical-induced oxidation kinetics and assessing antioxidant activity’s magnitude and duration. This makes it suitable for complex biological samples and functional foods. However, ORAC’s complexity and cost often confine it to research settings. Emerging hybrid approaches, like coupling ORAC with HPLC or integrating electrochemical sensors with fluorescence detection, aim to provide comprehensive insights into antioxidant behavior.

# 7.2 Selection Criteria

Selecting an antioxidant measurement method involves considering sample matrix, target analytes, sensitivity, throughput, and application context. The DPPH assay is preferred for rapid screening of lipophilic antioxidants in plant extracts or food products due to its simplicity and affordability, though colored compounds can interfere with results. The ABTS assay, with its broader applicability, suits complex biological fluids. FRAP is ideal for quantifying reducing capacity in samples rich in electron-donating antioxidants but lacks kinetic and physiological insights.

Electrochemical methods, optimal for real-time monitoring in pharmaceuticals and food products, excel in sensitivity and adaptability to miniaturized formats, though their selectivity is challenged by overlapping redox potentials. Recent advancements like sSMLM improve spatial resolution but limit routine use due to operational complexity.

HPLC-based quantification is crucial for profiling individual antioxidants in complex mixtures, offering high specificity and compatibility with various detection methods. This technique is suited for research requiring structural elucidation or absolute quantification, though its high cost and lengthy development restrict high-throughput use.

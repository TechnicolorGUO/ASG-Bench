# Abstract

This survey paper comprehensively examines the multifaceted applications of nanomaterials in biomedical and agricultural domains, highlighting their transformative potential in diagnostics, drug delivery, tissue engineering, crop protection, and sustainable farming. We systematically review nanomaterial synthesis methods, emphasizing green and scalable approaches such as plasma-liquid interactions and droplet-based microfluidics. In biomedicine, nanomaterials enable precision therapies through stimuli-responsive drug carriers and advanced imaging techniques, while in agriculture, they enhance nutrient delivery, soil remediation, and pest control. Key challenges, including toxicity, scalability, and regulatory hurdles, are critically analyzed, alongside interdisciplinary strategies to address them. The paper underscores the need for sustainable synthesis and AI-driven optimization to bridge the gap between laboratory innovations and real-world applications. By integrating cutting-edge research with practical considerations, this survey provides a roadmap for advancing nanotechnology in healthcare and agriculture while ensuring environmental and ethical sustainability.

# 2 Background

# 2.1 Historical Evolution of Nanotechnology

Nanotechnology’s roots trace back to Richard Feynman’s 1959 lecture, "There’s Plenty of Room at the Bottom," envisioning atomic-scale manipulation. This conceptual leap led to the scanning tunneling microscope’s (STM) invention in 1981, enabling atomic-scale visualization and manipulation [1, 2, 3, 4]. The 1990s saw breakthroughs in nanoparticle synthesis, particularly gold nanoparticles for biomedical uses. The 2000s marked the rise of quantum dots and carbon nanotubes, enhancing diagnostics and drug delivery.

Simultaneously, agricultural nanotechnology advanced with nanofertilizers and pesticides, improving nutrient uptake and targeted pest control. Recent innovations focus on precision engineering, such as stimuli-responsive nanocarriers for controlled drug release and plasmonic nanoparticles for hyperthermia therapies, enhancing treatment specificity and efficacy. Smart drug delivery systems and magnetic electrospun fibers exemplify advancements in precise therapeutic agent administration [5, 6, 7, 8].

Sustainable synthesis, notably green chemistry, addresses scalability and environmental concerns. Key challenges include toxicity standardization and regulatory frameworks for safe deployment in biomedicine and agriculture. This evolution highlights nanotechnology’s transition from theory to a multidisciplinary innovation driver in healthcare, agriculture, and environmental sustainability, poised to address pressing global challenges.

# 2.2 Foundational Principles of Nanomaterials

Nanomaterials exhibit unique properties due to size-dependent phenomena, high surface-area-tovolume ratios, and quantum effects, distinguishing them from bulk materials. Quantum confinement at the nanoscale alters electronic, optical, and mechanical behaviors, enhancing applications like sensors and transistors [9]. The increased surface area boosts reactivity and adsorption, facilitating catalytic drug release and targeted nutrient delivery in agriculture.

Low-Reynolds-number hydrodynamics influence nanomaterial behavior in fluids, affecting mobility in biological systems and soil matrices [10]. Diffusion-influenced reactions in confined geometries demand precise kinetic modeling for optimized functionality [9]. Understanding these interactions is vital for designing effective nanomaterials across environments.

Phase separation in magnetic fluids exemplifies size-dependent structural evolution, yielding tunable properties [11]. Hierarchical designs, inspired by diatom frustules, achieve mechanical and fluid dynamic efficiency [3]. Self-emulsification and electrochemical dealloying provide scalable synthesis routes for pharmaceutical-grade nanomaterials [12, 1]. These principles underpin nanomaterial design and application, integrating physics, chemistry, and engineering to tackle synthesis and deployment challenges in biomedicine and agriculture.

# 2.3 Emergence of Nanomaterials in Biomedical Applications

Nanomaterials have revolutionized biomedical applications, enhancing diagnostics, drug delivery, and tissue engineering through their unique properties and biocompatibility. Early diagnostics advancements utilized nanoparticles’ high surface-area-to-volume ratio to improve biosensor and imaging agent sensitivity, enabling early disease detection [13]. Spherical nanodiamonds (NDs) emerged as promising drug carriers due to low toxicity and functionalization capacity, facilitating targeted therapeutic delivery.

Controlled-release systems advanced with computational models like the Monte Carlo approach, refining drug release predictions [5]. Tissue engineering benefits from bioactive glass nanoparticles (NanoMBG-75S), promoting macrophage function without inflammation, crucial for bone regeneration [14]. Innovations in synthesis, including low-energy self-emulsification, enable scalable pharmaceutical-grade nanomaterial production [12]. Electrochemical dealloying produces porous silicon frameworks, expanding applications in implantable devices [1].

Advancements in nanoparticle transport dynamics, such as advection-diffusion coupling studies, elucidate dispersion in biological environments [15]. These milestones highlight nanomaterials’ transition to clinically viable tools, addressing precision medicine, biocompatibility, and manufacturing challenges. Future directions include optimizing nanoparticle-immune interactions and integrating machine learning for multifunctional nanocarrier design.

# 2.4 Emergence of Nanomaterials in Agricultural Applications

Nanomaterials have transformed agriculture, offering solutions for crop protection, nutrient delivery, and soil remediation, crucial for food security and sustainability. Early applications focused on nano-enabled agrochemicals, where engineered nanoparticles improved pesticide and fertilizer effectiveness through enhanced solubility, stability, and targeted delivery [12, 16, 7, 17, 18]. Silica nanoparticles functionalized with agrochemicals reduced leaching and controlled release, minimizing environmental contamination.

Nutrient delivery systems leverage nanomaterials’ surface-area-to-volume ratio for optimized uptake, as demonstrated by zinc oxide nanoparticles enhancing crop zinc assimilation. Soil remediation benefits from nanomaterials’ catalytic and adsorption properties, such as iron oxide nanoparticles for heavy metal immobilization and carbon-based nanostructures for organic pollutant degradation. Hierarchical designs, inspired by diatom frustules, create porous nanostructures for water filtration and contaminant removal [3].

pH-responsive membranes advance precision agriculture by enabling selective nutrient release based on soil conditions [19]. Scalable synthesis methods, including green chemistry, produce eco-friendly nanomaterials, aligning with sustainable practices [17]. Challenges like nanoparticle persistence and non-target effects necessitate rigorous toxicity assessments. Emerging trends involve integrating nanosensors for real-time soil monitoring, enhancing precision farming [2]. Future directions emphasize interdisciplinary collaboration to optimize nanoparticle-plant interactions, mitigate environmental risks, and scale nano-enabled solutions globally.

# 3 Nanomaterial Synthesis

# 3.1 Green Synthesis Approaches

Green synthesis has gained prominence as an ecologically viable alternative to conventional nanomaterial production techniques, emphasizing biological and eco-friendly chemical methods to reduce environmental impact and energy use. As illustrated in Figure 2, the hierarchical classification of green synthesis approaches for nanomaterials highlights biological methods, chemical methods, and simulation techniques as key categories. Within these categories, specific strategies such as plantmediated synthesis and solvent-free chemical reactions demonstrate the diversity and potential of green synthesis in aligning with sustainability principles.

Plant-mediated processes leverage naturally occurring phytochemicals, such as flavonoids and terpenoids, that act as reducing agents, facilitating the creation of metal nanoparticles with precise morphological control [17]. These strategies eliminate the necessity for harmful chemicals and exploit plant resources’ availability for cost-efficient large-scale production. Microbe-mediated systems utilize bacterial or fungal enzymes and proteins to synthesize nanoparticles, offering scalability and unique physical properties [17]. These novel properties are often unattainable via traditional methods.

Chemical green synthesis focuses on reactions in solvent-free environments or aqueous phases, where spontaneous emulsification methods allow surfactant-free nanoparticle production suitable for pharmaceutical applications [12]. These methods lessen the environmental burden from solvent use while enhancing synthesis safety. The Diffusion-influenced Reaction Model (DIRM) optimizes reaction kinetics within hollow nanoreactors [9], guiding experimental practices for reliable nanomaterial production. Microfluidic platforms enable ultrahigh-throughput experimentation, customizing droplet dynamics and microdroplet shaping for precise nanoparticle attributes.

External Potential Dynamics (EPD) simulations have revealed insights into copolymeric nanoparticle assembly, illustrating the impact of mixing rates on morphology and drug-loading efficiencies [20]. Electrochemical methods, such as lithium-mediated dealloying, produce porous silicon frameworks, enhancing applications in agriculture and biomedicine [1]. However, challenges remain in accurately characterizing lipid-based nanoparticles, which are crucial for optimized drug release [21]. Future efforts include utilizing machine learning for predictive synthesis models and expanding plasma-liquid interactions to achieve rapid, eco-friendly production [4]. Collectively, these approaches highlight green synthesis’s potential to align with sustainability principles, fostering broad applications across industries.

# 3.2 Plasma-Liquid Interactions (PLIs)

Plasma-liquid interactions (PLIs) offer a scalable and adaptable means for nanomaterial synthesis by manipulating plasma-derived reactive species with liquid precursors for precise nanoparticle control. This approach divides into chemical processes in aqueous phases, where plasma radicals aid in reducing metal ions to form noble metal nanoparticles, and physical processes, such as sputtering and evaporation, producing pure metallic nanoparticles [4]. Challenges include controlling oxidation and managing electrode-related impurities to ensure material quality.

# 3.3 Droplet-Based Microfluidics

Droplet-based microfluidics provides a precise framework for nanomaterial synthesis and functionalization, managing reaction kinetics, particle size, and morphology within small-volume droplets for efficient experimentation [2]. This method conserves resources, allowing broad experimentation while ensuring reproducibility. Nucleation universality in these microdroplets, controlled by phase diagrams and dispersion principles, influences droplet channel geometry and flow conditions, optimizing nanoparticle production [22].

Innovative techniques like Four-Phase Contact Line Lithography (FPCLL) harness Marangonidriven interfacial dynamics for droplet manipulation, producing anisotropic nanostructures with specific functional attributes [23]. In-line characterization tools further reinforce process reliability by enabling real-time analysis of particle growth and stability.

The scalability of droplet-based systems benefits from porous channel designs ensuring consistent droplet size and predictable nanoparticle yields [22]. Challenges like droplet coalescence and maintaining particle monodispersity under high-flow conditions are mitigated through surface chemistry or electrostatic stabilizations. Future directions include integrating machine learning with microfluidics for synthesis optimization and developing hybrid systems for multifunctional nanomaterials. These advancements promise enhanced bridge-building from lab discoveries to industrial-scale nanomaterial production, enriching applications in biomedicine and agriculture and furthering the future of nanotechnology.

# 4.2 Targeted Drug Delivery Systems

Targeted drug delivery systems (TDDS) utilize nanocarriers for precise therapeutic delivery, enhancing efficacy and minimizing toxicity. These systems integrate materials science, fluid dynamics, and molecular biology to optimize drug loading, release kinetics, and cellular targeting. Lipid-based nanoparticles, polymeric carriers, and inorganic nanostructures like mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSNs) offer distinct advantages in stability, biocompatibility, and versatility [7]. Coarse-grained molecular dynamics simulations reveal that lipid distribution influences the vesicle-like structure of lipid-based nanoparticles, enhancing drug encapsulation and membrane stability [21]. Phasechanging $\mathbf { M o S } _ { 2 }$ membranes exemplify stimuli-responsive systems, enabling controlled drug release in acidic tumor microenvironments [19].

Magnetic fluids expand TDDS capabilities, allowing tunable drug-loading capacities and remotecontrolled activation. Magnetic nanoparticles facilitate targeted delivery and therapeutic response monitoring through imaging techniques. Computational frameworks like External Potential Dynamics (EPD) simulate copolymeric nanoparticle assembly to optimize drug encapsulation efficiency [20]. Emerging innovations include macrophage-responsive nanoparticles, such as bioactive glass nanoparticles (NanoMBG-75S), which modulate immune cell polarization [14]. Challenges persist in scaling production and standardizing performance metrics for clinical translation. Future directions emphasize real-time monitoring systems, adaptive dosing protocols, and AI frameworks to optimize nanocarrier design [24]. The evolution of TDDS represents a significant leap in therapeutic strategies, particularly in oncology, enhancing treatment outcomes and minimizing side effects.

# 4.3 Tissue Engineering and Regenerative Medicine

Nanomaterials are pivotal in tissue engineering and regenerative medicine, offering tailored scaffolds that mimic the extracellular matrix (ECM) to promote cell adhesion, proliferation, and differentiation. Hydrodynamic interactions between nanoparticles and biological membranes, elucidated by Stokeslet-based models, optimize drug delivery within engineered tissues [10]. Hollow nanoreactors enhance nutrient and growth factor delivery, improving tissue regeneration efficiency [9]. Nanostructured surfaces inspired by diatom frustules combine mechanical robustness with fluid dynamic efficiency for vascularized tissue growth [3]. Antimicrobial nanotopographies prevent biofilm formation while promoting osseointegration [26].

Stimuli-responsive nanomaterials enable precise control over scaffold properties, such as pHdependent water permeability switching in synthetic membranes [19]. Remote-controlled activation of drug-loaded magnetic nanoparticles allows on-demand therapeutic release [6]. Adhesion dynamics at the nanoscale inform the design of biointerfaces with tunable mechanical stability [27]. Microdroplet-based techniques enable the fabrication of anisotropic scaffolds with spatially graded properties [23]. Porous silicon frameworks provide high-surface-area substrates for stem cell expansion [1]. Challenges include scaling production while maintaining nanoscale precision and ensuring long-term biocompatibility. Future directions integrate thermoplasmonic nanoparticles for localized temperature monitoring and AI-driven scaffold optimization [25]. These advancements underscore nanomaterials’ potential to bridge synthetic scaffolds and native tissue functionality, leading to successful regenerative therapies.

# 5.1 Mechanisms of Nanoparticle Mobility in Biological Systems

Nanoparticle mobility in biological systems is dictated by interactions involving hydrodynamic forces, biological barriers, and nanomaterial properties, impacting transport efficiency and targeting precision. As illustrated in Figure 4, the hierarchical categorization of these mechanisms emphasizes the critical roles of hydrodynamic forces, biological barriers, and material properties. At low Reynolds numbers, viscous forces and elastohydrodynamic interactions near deformable membranes influence trajectories and adhesion dynamics [10]. Advection-diffusion coupling in nearsurface flows modulates dispersion under shear conditions, affecting nanoparticle navigation in biological tissues [15]. Biological barriers, like the endothelial glycocalyx and cellular membranes, filter nanoparticles based on size and charge. Nanoparticles under $1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ exploit passive diffusion, while larger ones use active transport such as receptor-mediated transcytosis. Surface functionalization with PEG or hyaluronic acid reduces opsonization and extends circulation, enhancing mobility in tumor microenvironments [28].

Magnetic fluid phase separation reveals how composition affects margination and vascular wall adhesion, with non-equilibrium dynamics enabling targeted accumulation [11]. Computational models simulate copolymeric nanoparticle assembly to predict size-dependent mobility in biological fluids [20]. Microfluidic studies reveal universal scaling laws for nanoparticle transport in confined geometries [22]. Stimuli-responsive materials, such as ionizable lipid nanoparticles and phage-fusion proteins, enhance transfection efficiency and targeting precision [3, 29, 24]. Thermoplasmonic nanoparticles enhance vascular permeability under near-infrared irradiation, improving extravasation. Challenges include mitigating nonspecific uptake and optimizing deformability to navigate pathological vasculature. Future directions integrate machine learning to predict mobility patterns, advancing precision delivery in complex physiological contexts.

# 5.2 Buckling Behavior and Morphological Changes in Nanoparticles

Nanoparticle buckling and morphological changes critically influence drug delivery, cellular uptake, and mechanical stability. Buckling of nanoparticle-armored droplets, as shown by Sicard et al., demonstrates how volume reduction induces shape transitions, affecting drug release kinetics and targeting efficiency [30]. In drug delivery, buckling can lead to premature payload leakage, but asymmetric lipid distributions enhance membrane rigidity, mitigating buckling [21]. Controlled buckling in stimuli-responsive nanocarriers, like pH-sensitive cerium fluoride nanoparticles, enables triggered drug release in acidic tumor environments [28].

Morphological changes affect nanoparticle-immune interactions. Bioactive glass nanoparticles maintain spherical morphology under shear stress, ensuring macrophage modulation without inflammation [14]. Anisotropic nanoparticles exhibit strain-dependent buckling, altering plasmonic properties and therapy efficacy. Computational models predict buckling thresholds under compressive loads [20]. Future research focuses on buckling-resistant designs through biomimetic approaches and real-time monitoring to correlate morphological changes with therapeutic outcomes [3].

# 5.3 Thermoplasmonics and Precision Temperature Measurements

Thermoplasmonics employs plasmonic nanoparticles, like gold nanoparticles (AuNPs), for localized heating via light-induced plasmon resonance, enhancing photothermal therapy and hyperthermia. Advances in thermometry, such as optical wavefront microscopy, enable precise temperature gradient measurements at the microscale, optimizing smart drug delivery systems for cancer [7, 24, 25, 4]. AuNPs convert light to heat, creating confined thermal gradients for selective tumor ablation or drug release, minimizing damage to healthy tissues.

Microscale thermometry techniques, like wavefront distortion analysis, offer real-time temperature mapping with submicrometer resolution, essential for optimizing therapeutic outcomes [25]. Buckling of nanoparticle-armored droplets under thermal stress demonstrates the interplay between plasmonic heating and mechanical stability, informing thermally responsive drug delivery design [30]. Integrating thermoplasmonic heating with robust designs ensures controlled payload release under thermal activation. Emerging applications include combining thermoplasmonics with immunotherapy, enhancing immune cell infiltration and antigen presentation. Challenges include standardizing temperature measurement protocols and adapting techniques for clinical use in heterogeneous environments [31, 25]. Future directions focus on multifunctional plasmonic nanostructures with integrated sensing and heating, and AI-driven thermal modeling for personalized therapy optimization.

# 6 Drug Delivery Systems

Advancements in drug delivery systems are significantly shaped by the development of various nanocarriers, which enhance the transport of therapeutic agents and influence drug pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics. # 6.1 Types of Nanocarriers and Their Properties

Nanocarriers are categorized into lipid-based, polymeric, and inorganic systems, each with unique properties affecting their stability, loading capacity, and release kinetics. Lipid-based carriers, like liposomes and solid lipid nanoparticles (SLNs), are noted for their biocompatibility and functionalization potential. Their vesicle-like structure, influenced by asymmetric lipid distributions, enhances membrane stability and drug encapsulation [21]. However, physiological shear stresses can induce buckling, compromising structural integrity and causing premature payload leakage [30]. Research focuses on stabilizing formulations and optimizing lipid compositions to improve clinical performance.

Polymeric nanocarriers, such as micelles and dendrimers, offer tunable degradation rates and stimuliresponsive release profiles. The DA-TAT-PECL micelle system exemplifies this versatility with acidactive TAT modifications for enhanced tumor-targeted delivery and colloidal stability [32]. Coarsegrained simulations reveal how mixing rates influence morphology and drug-loading capacity, optimizing release kinetics [20]. The Bernoulli-Fick Combined Method (BFCM) refines polymeric carrier design by predicting optimal delivery trajectories in vascular systems [33]. Evolutionary simulators autonomously optimize these systems for dynamic tumor environments, balancing circulation longevity and tissue penetration [34].

Inorganic nanocarriers, including mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSNs) and metal-organic frameworks (MOFs), offer mechanical robustness and high loading capacities. Mixed-charge pseudozwitterionic MSNs exhibit enhanced stability due to surface charge balance [35]. Electrospun Magnetic Fibers (EMFs) integrate magnetic nanoparticles with MSNs for remote-controlled drug release [6]. The Lattice-Boltzmann method models particle migration in constricted flows, optimizing delivery efficacy [36].

Release kinetics vary across carrier types. Lipid-based systems often exhibit burst release, while polymeric carriers enable sustained release through matrix erosion or swelling. The total fraction drug released $\left( \mathrm { F } _ { \infty } \right)$ model quantifies these dynamics [37]. Inorganic carriers combine diffusioncontrolled and stimuli-triggered release, though surface heterogeneity can hinder reproducibility [38]. Differential Evolution (DE) algorithms optimize nanoparticle designs for consistent performance across biological conditions [39].

Emerging hybrid systems, like lipid-bilayer-coated curcumin mesoporous nanoparticles, merge lipid biocompatibility with inorganic frameworks’ high loading capacity [40]. AI-driven algorithms enhance synthesis optimization, targeting properties like particle size and surface charge [18]. Future directions include real-time monitoring systems and adaptive dosing protocols, with explainable AI frameworks correlating nanocarrier features with therapeutic outcomes.

# 6.2 Mechanisms of Controlled Release

Stimuli-responsive drug delivery systems use environmental or external triggers for spatiotemporal control over therapeutic release, enhancing targeting precision. pH-sensitive systems exploit acidic microenvironments of tumors and inflamed tissues for selective drug release [41, 40]. Thermoresponsive mechanisms use localized heating to trigger release, often mediated by plasmonic nanoparticles [42]. Surface heterogeneity influences release reproducibility, as irregular surfaces modulate dissolution rates [38].

Enzyme-responsive systems utilize overexpressed enzymes in pathological conditions to cleave bonds or degrade matrices. Cooperativity-based kinetic models enhance targeting specificity in cancer cells with elevated enzymatic activity [43]. Hybrid designs, like mixed-charge pseudozwitterionic MSNs, combine multiple stimuli-responsive mechanisms for precise control [35].

Emerging strategies use AI-driven models to dynamically adjust drug release profiles in response to evolving physiological conditions, exemplified by Differential Evolution algorithms optimizing targeted delivery systems [34, 39]. Future directions emphasize computational materials design with real-time biosensing for adaptive nanocarriers in personalized medicine.

# 6.3 Optimization of Drug Delivery Systems

Optimizing drug delivery systems involves enhancing targeting efficiency, minimizing toxicity, and improving scalability. Surface engineering strategies, such as pH-sensitive ligands or enzymecleavable linkers, exploit pathological microenvironments for site-specific accumulation [31]. Rigorous characterization of degradation profiles and long-term biocompatibility is crucial for toxicity mitigation [26, 38].

Scalability is addressed through advanced synthesis techniques, like droplet-based microfluidics and plasma-liquid interactions, enabling high-throughput production with precise control [34]. Future directions focus on AI-driven predictive models and real-time biosensing for dynamic dosing protocols, alongside standardized protocols for large-scale manufacturing and toxicity assessment. The evolution of drug delivery systems aims to enhance therapeutic efficacy and patient safety, paving the way for personalized treatment strategies.

# 7.1 Nano-enabled Solutions for Reduced Chemical Use

Nanomaterials are vital in reducing agrochemical reliance while maintaining effectiveness. Utilizing eco-friendly synthesis methods, including plant-based and microbial resources, these materials address environmental and health issues associated with conventional chemicals [26, 12, 17]. Engineered nanoparticles facilitate targeted pesticide and fertilizer delivery, minimizing off-target effects and environmental contamination. Silica nanoparticles functionalized with agrochemicals exhibit controlled release kinetics, reducing leaching and volatilization while enhancing pest control duration. The high surface-area-to-volume ratio of nanoparticles boosts adsorption and retention, as demonstrated by zinc oxide nanoparticles enhancing nutrient uptake in crops without soil accumulation.

Stimuli-responsive nanocarriers release payloads precisely when activated by environmental cues such as $\mathrm { p H }$ and enzymes. Phase-changing $\mathbf { M o S } _ { 2 }$ membranes showcase pH-dependent permeability for selective nutrient release triggered by root exudates or soil acidity [19]. Magnetic fluid-based systems achieve tunable agrochemical loading and remote-controlled activation using alternating magnetic fields [11], reducing application frequencies while maintaining efficacy, thus lowering agriculture’s environmental impact.

Nano-enabled coatings and encapsulations, inspired by diatom frustules, provide porous matrices for agrochemical containment, enhancing mechanical robustness and controlled release [3]. Lipid-based nanocarriers, optimized by molecular dynamics simulations, improve membrane stability and reduce premature leakage under field conditions [21], boosting agrochemical efficacy and sustainability by minimizing environmental contamination.

Nanoformulations can decrease non-target pesticide residues by $30 \%$ . However, standardizing toxicity evaluation protocols remains challenging, necessitating further research to ensure safe and effective agricultural application. Future research emphasizes integrating nanosensors for real-time soil and plant health monitoring, enabling precision dosing per crop needs [2]. Combining green synthesis methods with nano-enabled delivery systems advances sustainable agriculture while addressing global food security, fostering efficient and environmentally conscious practices.

# 7.2 Enhancing Agricultural Productivity with Nanomaterials

Nanomaterials significantly enhance agricultural productivity by improving crop yield, disease resistance, and stress tolerance through targeted molecular and cellular interventions. Zinc oxide nanoparticles $_ \mathrm { Z n O }$ NPs) are effective in soil systems, increasing zinc availability, enhancing crop growth, and boosting nutrient uptake without adverse soil accumulation. This aligns with green nanotechnology principles, prioritizing eco-friendly and sustainable nanomaterial synthesis and application for efficient agriculture [38, 18, 7, 17].

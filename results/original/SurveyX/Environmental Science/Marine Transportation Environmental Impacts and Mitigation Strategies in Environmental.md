# 2 Background

# 2.1 Current State of Marine Biodiversity and Climate Change

Marine biodiversity is increasingly threatened by climate change and shipping activities, which exacerbate ocean warming, acidification, and deoxygenation, disrupting ecosystems and threatening species diversity [1]. These changes particularly affect coastal communities relying on marine resources. As stressors on marine species intensify, entire ecosystems face jeopardy, leading to cascading effects that challenge sustainability. Shipping contributes significantly to these pressures through greenhouse gas emissions, underwater noise pollution, and invasive species from ballast water discharge, which disrupt ecosystems and economies. Although ballast water regulations aim to mitigate these risks, they can increase shipping costs, impacting Small Island Developing States (SIDS) and Least Developed Countries (LDCs). Emissions from shipping, especially in regions like the North and Baltic Seas, include pollutants such as CO2 and NOx, posing health risks and contributing to social costs [2, 3, 4]. These stressors lead to declines in keystone species and habitat collapse, with regional studies showing compounded effects on ocean acidification in high-traffic corridors. The complex feedback loops between climate change and shipping activities necessitate integrated strategies that align maritime practices with conservation goals to prevent further biodiversity loss.

# 2.2 Shipping Emissions: Scale and Projections

Shipping emissions, accounting for approximately $2 . 9 \%$ of global $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions, are projected to rise by $5 0 \mathrm { - } 2 5 0 \%$ by 2050 if current trends continue, driven by global trade and fleet expansion [2]. Regional disparities in emissions result from differences in fuel standards, operational practices, and regulatory frameworks. For example, the North and Baltic Seas exhibit varying pollutant levels due to different environmental compliance standards. High-traffic routes like the Malacca Strait show elevated emission intensities, while the Arctic faces increased black carbon deposition due to new shipping routes. The IMO’s strategy to reduce emissions by $50 \%$ by 2050 requires more comprehensive measures, as current efforts like slow steaming and alternative fuels are insufficient to meet targets [2, 3]. Discrepancies in emission inventories, highlighted by satellite data, underscore the need for combining top-down and traditional accounting methods. Advances in satellite monitoring and AI can enhance emission assessments, promoting precision in energy consumption and trading [5, 6].

# 2.3 Key Challenges in Marine Pollution

Marine pollution from shipping involves regulatory, technological, and economic challenges. Highresolution emission inventories are often lacking, hindering precise coastal analyses and policy development [2]. Regulatory fragmentation leads to uneven compliance landscapes, with inconsistent ballast water management creating opportunities for regulatory arbitrage, affecting SIDS and LDCs [3, 4]. Technological barriers persist, particularly for deep-sea shipping, where energy density limitations hinder the adoption of sustainable alternatives. Economic disincentives, such as thin profit margins and the "first-mover disadvantage," discourage green investments, while the absence of standardized carbon pricing perpetuates reliance on conventional fuels [6]. Emerging decentralized supply chains increase compliance complexity, requiring coordinated advancements in policy harmonization and green technologies to align economic incentives with ecological goals.

# 2.4 Economic and Ecological Impacts

Marine pollution from shipping has intertwined economic and ecological impacts across multiple scales. Pollutants disrupt ecosystems, alter food webs, and degrade habitats, with ballast water management regulations aiming to mitigate invasive species but also raising shipping costs, affecting SIDS and LDCs [2, 3]. Emissions contribute to air pollution, impacting human health and marine life. Oil spills can reduce fish biomass significantly, with cascading effects on fisheries and tourism. Economically, pollution imposes costs through infrastructure corrosion and healthcare burdens, with indirect costs from reduced property values and tourism revenue [2]. Mitigation investments demonstrate economic benefits; for example, ballast water treatment prevents future damages, and sulfur emission controls reduce healthcare costs. Spatial mismatches between pollution sources and impact zones necessitate international cost-sharing mechanisms. Future research should integrate dynamic modeling to assess cumulative impacts on marine socio-ecological systems, leveraging satellite imagery and AI for real-time monitoring [5].

These emissions vary widely based on vessel type, engine configuration, and fuel quality, underscoring the need for targeted reduction approaches.

Emission concentrations are typically higher along major shipping lanes, highlighting operational practices’ impact.

# 3.2 Ecological Consequences of Shipping Emissions

Shipping emissions result in diverse ecological impacts, affecting marine biodiversity and atmospheric conditions. Emissions of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ from maritime activities contribute to ocean acidification that disrupts calcifying organisms, impacting fundamental marine food webs [2]. $\mathrm { N O } _ { \mathrm { x } }$ and $\mathrm { { S O } _ { x } }$ emissions drive atmospheric deposition, causing eutrophication and histamine bloomy condition in coastal waters, altering ecosystem dynamics and reducing photosynthetically active radiation (PAR), which affects seagrass and macroalgae productivity [2, 3].

Black carbon is especially problematic in Arctic regions, reducing albedo and accelerating ice melt, which in turn increases vessel traffic and exacerbates BC deposition [2]. The cumulative effects of these emissions are compounded by climate change stressors, emphasizing the necessity of integrated assessments that consider multiple environmental interactions [1, 5].

Mitigating these ecological impacts requires strategies that address both emissions and their secondary effects. Advanced technologies like AI-driven assessments offer promising avenues for capturing complex ecosystem interactions over various scales, though domain adaptation to niche marine contexts remains a challenge [1].

# 3.3 Regional and Global Quantification Studies

Quantifying shipping emissions at regional and global scales reveals variability driven by traffic density, regulatory frameworks, and fuel standards. Global estimates attribute around $2 . 9 \%$ of anthropogenic $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions to shipping, with disparities in local emission patterns influenced by regulations and operational practices [2]. High-resolution inventories show that the majority of $\mathrm { N O } _ { \mathrm { x } }$ emissions occur near coastlines, significantly impacting local air and marine quality [2].

Inconsistencies between traditional bottom-up and satellite-based inventory measurements suggest underestimations, particularly for compounds like $\mathrm { { S O } _ { x } }$ , highlighting a need for refined emission quantification methods [1]. Hybrid techniques integrating AIS data with machine learning have improved spatial and temporal resolution, advancing accuracy in emission estimates [6].

Regional studies indicate the ecological effects of emissions, such as black carbon in the Arctic contributing to albedo reduction and intensified ice melt, whereas in the Mediterranean, high nitrogen levels lead to eutrophication [2, 5]. Tailored strategies reflecting regional differences, as seen by variations in ECA effectiveness, are crucial for effective mitigation. Enhanced quantification efforts must address gaps in lifecycle impacts of alternative fuels and auxiliary engine emissions, fostering international collaboration through standardized protocols and data sharing [1, 3].

# 5.1 Alternative Propulsion Systems

The maritime sector is shifting towards sustainable propulsion technologies, offering significant emission reduction potential. Hydrogen fuel cells, especially proton exchange membrane (PEM) types, promise zero operational emissions with green hydrogen, achieving $1 { - } 2 \mathrm { k W } / \mathrm { k g }$ power densities and $50 \%$ efficiency. Despite their advantages, hydrogen storage remains challenging, requiring 4-5 times the volume of diesel. Research into solid-state hydrogen storage aims to address these constraints [2].

Electric propulsion is gaining popularity in short-sea shipping, where lithium-ion batteries offer 8-12 hour ranges, supported by predictable routes and frequent charging. Integrating battery systems with renewable energy and blockchain-enabled platforms enhances sustainability and economic viability by reducing fuel costs. Advances in solid-state and lithium-sulfur batteries could further improve performance [6].

Ammonia-fueled engines leverage existing architectures, offering $70 \%$ $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ reduction, though $\mathrm { N O } _ { \mathrm { x } }$ emissions require advanced treatment. The established ammonia infrastructure supports this transition, but toxicity risks necessitate stringent safety protocols and training. Research into ammonia combustion and ammonia-to-hydrogen conversion is ongoing [2, 3].

Comparative analyses highlight distinct applications: hydrogen fuel cells for high-efficiency, lowvibration uses; battery systems for short-range routes; and ammonia for energy-dense deep-sea shipping. Hybrid systems integrating multiple technologies offer emission reductions of $40 \%$ . Blockchain-enabled algorithms optimize energy use, reducing wasted energy by $7 3 . 6 \%$ and grid purchases by $1 2 . 3 \%$ . Machine learning models using satellite imagery enhance sustainable practices monitoring [5, 6].

Technical barriers include hydrogen’s cryogenic storage and ammonia’s specialized containment. Regulatory frameworks, like the IMO’s interim guidelines for hydrogen and ammonia, are evolving to address these challenges. Future research must focus on scaling green hydrogen and ammonia production, improving fuel cell durability, and standardizing safety protocols to achieve decarbonization goals.

These disruptions can lead to the decline of native species and alter food webs, necessitating effective management measures and technological innovations. # 6.1 Ecological Risks and Regulatory Frameworks

Ballast water discharge introduces invasive species and pathogens, posing significant ecological risks to marine ecosystems. It is a primary vector for biological invasions, impacting coastal ecosystems and fisheries by altering food webs and competing with native species [4]. Satellite monitoring has linked high-risk ballast discharge zones to invasive species outbreaks, especially in semi-enclosed seas [5]. The International Maritime Organization’s Ballast Water Management Convention (BWMC) sets global standards for ballast water exchange (D-1) and treatment (D-2), yet disparities in implementation exist, particularly for Small Island Developing States (SIDS) and Least Developed Countries (LDCs) due to economic constraints [4]. The United States’ stricter standards under the Vessel Incidental Discharge Act (VIDA) highlight compliance challenges for international shipping [3].

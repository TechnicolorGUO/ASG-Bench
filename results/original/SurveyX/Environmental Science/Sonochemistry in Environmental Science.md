# 2 Background

# 2.1 Historical Development of Sonochemistry

Sonochemistry’s origins date back to the late 19th century with Thornycroft and Barnaby’s discovery of acoustic cavitation in 1895, observing the erosion of ship propellers due to bubble collapse. This phenomenon was theoretically framed by Lord Rayleigh in 1917, who described cavitation dynamics and spherical bubble collapse, laying the foundation for subsequent exploration of ultrasound’s chemical effects. Systematic investigation into these effects began in the mid-20th century, notably with Alfred Loomis in 1950, who demonstrated that ultrasound could accelerate chemical reactions, establishing sonochemistry as a distinct discipline. This led to innovations in chemical synthesis, integrating nonconventional methods like microwave-assisted and hydrothermal processes to enhance efficiency and reduce energy consumption [2, 3, 1].

The 1980s saw the advent of high-intensity ultrasound equipment, enabling precise cavitation control. Kenneth Suslick’s 1986 studies elucidated sonoluminescence and sonochemical reactions, highlighting extreme conditions $\left( > 5 0 0 0 \mathrm { K } \right.$ , ${ > } 1 0 0 0$ atm) during bubble collapse that facilitate radical formation. This period marked sonochemistry’s expansion beyond academia, notably in environmental remediation, as demonstrated by Mason and Lorimer in 1988, who showed its efficacy in degrading organic pollutants. This aligns with efforts to develop sustainable pollution control methods, enhancing green chemistry practices [2, 4, 3, 1].

In the 21st century, sonochemistry integrated with advanced oxidation processes (AOPs) for wastewater treatment. Innovations like hybrid sonophotocatalytic systems and pulsed ultrasound reactors improved degradation efficiency and minimized energy consumption. Recent advancements in chemical synthesis and process optimization emphasize nanostructured catalysts that enhance cavitation effects, improving reaction efficiency and product quality. The integration of machine learning models optimizes process parameters for precise control and better performance in applications such as wastewater treatment and sustainable energy production, illustrating sonochemistry’s evolution into a scalable technology for environmental remediation [2, 4, 3, 1].

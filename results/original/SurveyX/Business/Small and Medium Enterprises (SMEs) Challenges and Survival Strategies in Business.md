# Abstract

This survey paper systematically examines entrepreneurship in Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs), focusing on innovation, crisis management, and resiliencebuilding mechanisms. Through a structured analysis of challengesincluding financial constraints, market competition, and regulatory hurdlesthe study explores predictive strategies for economic shocks, leveraging tools like the Web Analytics Index (WAI) and behavior-based dependency networks to enhance SME adaptability. Key findings highlight the role of digital transformation in survival strategies, such as cost optimization and networked redundancy, while emphasizing the interplay between business resilience (firm-level adaptability) and economic resilience (systemic stability). The paper critiques traditional economic models for overlooking SME heterogeneity and proposes advanced frameworks, including the Stripe-corrected Gravity Model (scGM), to reconstruct firm-level interactions and optimize recovery pathways. Policy recommendations advocate for targeted interventions that align with sector-specific vulnerabilities and socio-economic contexts, underscoring the need for hybrid governance models to strengthen SME resilience. Future research directions call for integrating price dynamics into susceptibility analyses and advancing real-time monitoring tools to address evolving global challenges.

# 2 Background and Definitions

# 2.1 Definition and Scope of SMEs

Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) are defined by criteria such as employee count, annual revenue, or asset value, which vary by region and economic context. The European Union defines SMEs as firms with fewer than 250 employees and either an annual turnover below 50 million or a balance sheet total under 43 million. In contrast, the U.S. Small Business Administration uses industry-specific criteria, often setting a threshold of up to 500 employees for manufacturing and a revenue cap of 7.5millionfornon manuf acturingsectors.T hesedef initionsframediscussionsonbusinessgrowthandresilience, particularlyinurban 2, 3].

SMEs contribute approximately $50 \%$ of global employment and $40 \%$ of GDP in emerging economies, spanning sectors like manufacturing, retail, and tech startups, each with distinct growth and vulnerability profiles. This diversity highlights the interplay between urban economic diversity and firm network dynamics, affecting how economic shocks impact industries [1, 4, 3]. Microenterprises face more resource constraints, while medium-sized enterprises show greater capacity for innovation. This variance necessitates tailored policy approaches to support SME growth.

SMEs possess structural characteristics like localized supply chains and owner-manager governance, enhancing adaptability and risk exposure compared to larger firms. These traits enable swift responses to market changes and play a vital role in urban economic resilience, particularly against shocks like natural disasters or crises [2, 3, 1, 4, 5]. Their reliance on local ecosystems amplifies sensitivity to regional shocks, underscoring the need for nuanced policy frameworks. Sector-specific dynamics show knowledge-intensive SMEs are more resilient through digital transformation than traditional manufacturing SMEs constrained by physical assets.

# 2.2 Core Concepts of Entrepreneurship in SMEs

Entrepreneurship in SMEs involves innovation, risk-taking, and value creation, driving adaptability and growth. SMEs exhibit higher innovation agility due to flat structures and market proximity, facilitating swift experimentation with business models and processes, albeit hindered by limited RD resources and institutional support [1, 5, 3].

Risk-taking in SMEs includes strategic (e.g., market entry) and operational (e.g., adopting new technologies) dimensions. Entrepreneurs often bear personal financial risks, using personal assets for funding. This concentration of risk heightens decision-making importance, with successful entrepreneurs employing hybrid risk-mitigation strategies [1, 2, 3]. These strategies allow SMEs to navigate uncertainties while maintaining competitiveness.

Value creation extends beyond profit to include social and ecosystem contributions. SMEs target niche markets and contribute to regional development through job creation and skill enhancement, fostering diverse urban economies and strengthening resilience against shocks [1, 6, 3]. The "liability of smallness" paradox allows SMEs to leverage localized knowledge for unique value propositions.

Digital platforms redefine core concepts, enabling scalable infrastructure and democratizing risksharing. However, digital transition challenges, like algorithm dependency and data security, necessitate updated theoretical frameworks for SME entrepreneurship in the digital age [5, 3]. Future research should align institutional policies with SMEs’ non-linear innovation pathways for sustained growth and resilience.

# 2.3 Business Resilience and Economic Resilience

Business resilience and economic resilience are interconnected dimensions of SME sustainability, at firm and systemic levels. Business resilience involves an SME’s capacity to anticipate, adapt, and recover from disruptions through agile decision-making, operational flexibility, and diversified revenue streams [7]. Digitally mature SMEs enhance resilience by leveraging real-time data analytics for supply chain adjustments [4].

Economic resilience involves the stability of networked production systems and regional economies. Input-output dependencies among SMEs and larger firms determine systemic susceptibility to shocks [7]. Sectoral interdependencies create vulnerabilities during disruptions, necessitating coordinated policy interventions. The Web Analytics Index (WAI) aids in monitoring systemic effects and strategic planning for resilience-building [4].

The interplay between resilience types reveals trade-offs: business resilience focuses on firm survival through cost optimization, while economic resilience requires redundant capacities and networks [7]. Hybrid governance models, like public-private partnerships, align these objectives. Future studies should integrate algorithmic tools with macroeconomic models to predict SME clustering effects during crises, bridging micro-adaptations and system-wide stability.

# 2.4 Key Terminologies

This subsection standardizes key concepts in SME entrepreneurship research. Crisis management involves processes by which SMEs identify, assess, and mitigate disruptions through planning and adaptations. It includes structural measures (e.g., liquidity buffers) and strategic responses (e.g., market pivoting), contingent on digital maturity and network embeddedness [4].

Survival strategies are operational approaches SMEs use to maintain viability during adversity, focusing on sustained continuity rather than shock absorption [4]. They include cost rationalization, revenue diversification, and asset-light transformations.

Business adaptability measures an SME’s capacity to modify operations in response to changes, bridging micro-level behaviors and macro-level resilience [7]. It determines firm survival and economic ecosystem health.

Additional terms include urban business diversity, economic resilience, metropolitan statistical areas, generalized preferential attachment, firm networks, industrial sector proximity, and path-dependency [1, 3]. Urban business diversity addresses global challenges like climate change and economic resilience. Large datasets reveal a universal economic structure across U.S. metropolitan areas, with city size correlating with economic activity diversity. Generative network models for urban firm networks highlight factors like economic size and industry proximity in network growth and resilience.

Innovative methods for reconstructing inter-firm networks emphasize input-output relationships for economic resilience during shocks. The Web-Based Affectedness Indicator (WAI) offers real-time monitoring of firms’ responses to disruptions, aiding adaptive policy-making and resilience enhancement [1, 5, 3, 4].

# 2.5 SMEs in the Global Economic Landscape

SMEs are foundational to global economic systems, significantly contributing to employment, GDP growth, and regional diversification. Their spatial distribution and sectoral composition vary across urban and rural contexts, with urban SMEs forming dense networks that enhance regional resilience [1]. These networks enable knowledge spillovers, resource pooling, and risk-sharing, particularly in metropolitan areas with non-linear business category diversity scaling with city size [3].

SMEs account for about $90 \%$ of businesses and $70 \%$ of employment in low- and middle-income countries, with pronounced contributions in manufacturing and services. SME distributions across urban hierarchies, measured by metrics like the Web Analytics Index (WAI), show larger cities fostering specialized clusters, while smaller towns sustain generalized ecosystems [3]. Urban SMEs benefit from infrastructure but face higher competition, whereas rural SMEs exploit niche markets but face resource scarcity [1].

Globalization introduces opportunities and vulnerabilities for SMEs. Digital platforms enable crossborder operations, but SMEs are affected by trade policy and supply chain disruptions due to limited power. SME-dominated sectors exhibit higher volatility during shocks, yet localized employment structures stabilize regional labor markets [3]. Future studies should explore algorithmic governance tools to optimize SME distributions, balancing efficiency with risk mitigation, particularly in developing urban systems where SME growth influences economic resilience [1].

# 3.1 Dependencies on Urban Amenities and Economic Shocks

SMEs heavily depend on urban amenities and infrastructure, which enhance their operations but also increase their vulnerability to economic shocks. Urban concentration creates dependencies on transportation, digital infrastructure, and specialized services, becoming crucial shock propagation channels during disruptions [6]. Behavioral dependency networks indicate that high-connectivity urban nodes, like logistics hubs, are critical for SME continuity, with disruptions disproportionately affecting micro-enterprises [6]. Urban planning and policies must foster environments that bolster SME resilience.

The structure of urban firm networks affects SME resilience, as shown by [1]. While urban SMEs benefit from shared resources and knowledge spillovers, their lean models lack buffers against infrastructure failures or demand shocks. Retail and food-service SMEs, reliant on amenities like foot traffic, are more vulnerable compared to knowledge-intensive SMEs that can shift to remote operations [1]. This highlights the dual role of urban amenities as both facilitators and risks, necessitating policy recognition of these dynamics.

City-scale socioeconomic factors further influence these dependencies. Urban diversity metrics, as per [3], predict SME shock absorption capacities, with diverse metropolitan areas enabling SMEs to pivot across customer segments. However, in megacities, competition and congestion can offset resilience benefits, especially for SMEs with limited digital integration [3]. Emerging methodologies like behavior-based dependency mapping help quantify these relationships, guiding infrastructure investments to enhance SME resilience. Research should explore how digital platforms in hybrid urban-rural SME networks can mitigate spatial dependencies while maintaining agglomeration benefits.

# 3.2 Limitations of Traditional Economic Models

Traditional economic models fall short in capturing the complexities of SMEs during economic shocks. Conventional macroeconomic frameworks, focusing on supply-demand equilibria, overlook the heterogeneous nature of SMEs and their vulnerabilities [7]. These models often mispredict SME recovery rates due to oversimplified assumptions about adaptability and inter-firm dependencies [7]. A more nuanced approach is needed to accurately reflect SME realities in volatile economies.

Spatial and behavioral dependencies are inadequately addressed in traditional models, which rely on physical proximity metrics, ignoring actual human mobility and firm interactions [6]. Urban SMEs’ reliance on behavioral networks creates vulnerabilities that standard models fail to capture, leading to inaccurate shock propagation predictions [6]. Addressing these gaps is crucial for effective economic policies supporting SME resilience.

Traditional models also struggle to analyze inter-firm networks at the SME level, often assuming homogeneous relationships and disregarding the heterogeneity in transaction volumes and partnerships [5]. This results in flawed estimations of network properties crucial for understanding disruption cascades [5]. Models integrating behavioral data, network topologies, and sector-specific adaptations are needed. Future advancements should combine agent-based modeling with digital footprint analysis to simulate SME responses to macroeconomic shocks, bridging micro-level behaviors and macroeconomic resilience [7].

# 3.3 Sectoral and Product-Specific Constraints

SMEs face sectoral and product-specific constraints shaping their resilience and growth. Manufacturing SMEs are vulnerable to supply chain disruptions due to specialized inputs and just-in-time systems, while service-sector SMEs deal with human capital and regulatory costs, especially in knowledge-intensive areas [7]. Understanding these sector-specific challenges is vital for developing tailored support mechanisms.

As illustrated in Figure 3, the key sectoral and product-specific constraints facing SMEs are categorized into challenges related to sectoral issues, product life cycles, and technological adoption patterns within various industries. Product lifecycle stages also affect constraints. Commoditized goods SMEs face price competition and reduced adaptive capacity, whereas niche product SMEs encounter demand volatility. Agro-processing SMEs are affected by climate variability, while urban retail SMEs face rent inflation and shifting consumer patterns [6]. Regulatory burdens vary across sectors, with food and healthcare SMEs facing stringent compliance, whereas digital platform SMEs deal with evolving data governance [4]. Sectoral clustering effects can mitigate or amplify constraints, depending on local supporting industries [5].

Technological adoption patterns further differentiate constraints. Fintech SMEs integrate advanced analytics, while traditional craft-based SMEs lack digital literacy. Policy interventions need sectorspecific approaches, leveraging network reconstruction methodologies to identify leverage points within SME value chains [5]. Targeted strategies can enhance SME resilience, ensuring competitiveness and adaptability in a dynamic economic landscape.

# 4.1 Predictive Strategies for Economic Shocks

Predictive strategies for economic shocks in SMEs involve proactive measures leveraging datadriven methodologies and scenario planning to enhance preparedness and mitigate disruption impacts. The Web Analytics Index (WAI) represents a significant advancement in real-time monitoring, providing granular, firm-level insights into economic shocks through digital footprint analysis, surpassing traditional survey methods in both timeliness and comprehensiveness [4]. This innovative approach enables SMEs to detect early warning signalssuch as sudden drops in online engagement or supply chain delaysand adjust operations preemptively, enhancing situational awareness and empowering timely interventions to reduce shock impacts.

As illustrated in Figure 4, the main categories and predictive strategies employed by SMEs to address economic shocks include real-time monitoring methods, behavior-based networks for urban resilience, and sector-specific susceptibility metrics. Behavior-based dependency networks offer another predictive framework by quantifying interdependencies between SMEs and urban amenities, mapping how disruptions to critical points-of-interest (POIs) cascade through business ecosystems [6]. These networks identify high-risk nodes, such as transportation hubs, whose failure disproportionately affects SME clusters, allowing targeted contingency planning. Understanding these interconnections enables SMEs to prioritize risk management efforts and develop targeted strategies to bolster the resilience of critical nodes. Urban growth models enhance predictive accuracy by simulating shock propagation through spatially embedded firm networks, incorporating variables like sectoral concentration and digital maturity [1]. This modeling approach facilitates a nuanced understanding of sectoral responses to shocks, enabling SMEs to tailor strategies to their specific contexts.

Sector-specific susceptibility metrics, derived from input-output analysis, provide another predictive tool by measuring how individual industries absorb and transmit shocks [7]. Manufacturing SMEs, for instance, exhibit higher susceptibility to upstream supply disruptions than service-sector SMEs, necessitating differentiated risk mitigation strategies. This differentiation underscores the importance of context-specific approaches in crisis management, as a one-size-fits-all strategy may not adequately address diverse challenges across sectors. Advanced implementations combine these approaches through hybrid models integrating real-time WAI data with structural network analysis, enabling dynamic scenario testing for shocks ranging from localized infrastructure failures to global pandemics [4]. Such hybrid models represent a significant advancement in predictive strategies, allowing SMEs to prepare for potential shocks and adapt strategies in response to evolving circumstances.

Emerging challenges include algorithmic bias in predictive tools, where digitally immature SMEs may be underrepresented, and the need for interoperable data standards to facilitate cross-sector early warning systems. Future developments should focus on federated learning architectures preserving SME data privacy while improving collective shock prediction, alongside policy frameworks incentivizing SME participation in predictive data ecosystems [6]. Addressing these challenges is critical for ensuring predictive strategies are equitable and effective, ultimately enhancing SME sector resilience.

# 4.2 Frameworks for Understanding Firm Resilience

Theoretical frameworks for analyzing SME resilience during crises have evolved from static, firmcentric models to dynamic, network-based approaches accounting for behavioral dependencies and real-time adaptability. Behavior-based dependency networks represent a paradigm shift, demonstrating how SME resilience is shaped not merely by physical proximity but by visitation patterns and functional connections between points-of-interest (POIs) [6]. These networks reveal that SMEs relying on specific POI pairs, such as retail stores and transportation hubs, exhibit distinct recovery trajectories post-disruption, with long-distance dependencies introducing non-linear vulnerabilities that traditional distance-based models fail to capture [6]. This insight emphasizes integrating behavioral data into resilience frameworks, offering a nuanced understanding of SME interactions within ecosystems.

Network diffusion theory further refines these insights by modeling recovery propagation through interconnected business ecosystems, where SME resilience depends on customer and supplier visitation behaviors rather than static geographic clusters [2]. This framework explains why SMEs embedded in high-centrality nodes of behavioral networks often recover faster, benefiting from early reactivation of critical demand and supply flows. By focusing on interaction flows rather than firm locations, this approach provides valuable insights into recovery mechanisms, highlighting the importance of fostering strong connections within business networks. The Stripe-corrected Gravity Model (scGM) operationalizes this perspective by probabilistically reconstructing firm-level interactions based on sectoral relationships and transaction intensities, enabling precise simulations of shock propagation and resilience thresholds in SME networks [5]. This model represents a significant advancement in understanding SME resilience dynamics, facilitating more effective crisis management strategies.

Real-time monitoring frameworks, such as the Web Analytics Index (WAI), complement these structural models by quantifying SME resilience through digital footprints. WAI leverages natural language processing to detect shifts in online engagement, sentiment, and operational indicators, providing a dynamic measure of firm-level affectedness during crises [4]. When integrated with networkbased approaches, WAI enables early identification of resilience asymmetries, where digitally mature SMEs adapt faster than peers despite similar structural positions in dependency networks [4].

This integration underscores the importance of leveraging digital tools to enhance resilience, allowing identification of vulnerabilities and strengths within the SME sector.

# 4.3 Enhanced Network Reconstruction for Crisis Management

Enhanced network reconstruction methodologies provide critical tools for understanding and improving SME resilience by systematically mapping inter-firm dependencies and optimizing postcrisis recovery pathways. The Stripe-corrected Gravity Model (scGM) represents a significant methodological advancement, accurately reconstructing firm-level interactions by incorporating sectoral transaction intensities and geographic proximity, outperforming traditional gravity models in capturing the structural resilience of SME networks [5]. This approach enables precise identification of critical network nodes, such as hub suppliers or distribution intermediaries, whose failure would disproportionately disrupt SME operations, allowing targeted reinforcement of these connections during crisis recovery phases [5]. This targeted approach enhances individual firm resilience and contributes to the overall stability of the SME ecosystem.

Behavior-based dependency networks further refine reconstruction efforts by incorporating dynamic visitation patterns and customer flows into network models. Research by [2] demonstrates that postdisaster recovery follows distinct diffusion dynamics within these networks, with certain SME categories, such as logistics providers and wholesale distributors, acting as recovery multipliers that accelerate system-wide rehabilitation. These findings challenge conventional geographic clustering assumptions, revealing that functional interdependenciesmeasured through actual transaction behaviors rather than static location databetter predict SME recovery trajectories [2]. This shift in perspective is crucial for developing more effective crisis management strategies that account for the complexities of inter-firm relationships.

The integration of these approaches provides valuable, actionable insights for crisis management by enhancing our understanding of the dynamics influencing post-disaster business recovery, improving real-time monitoring of economic disruptions, and elucidating the complex interdependencies within urban economic networks, all of which are critical for developing effective recovery policies and fostering economic resilience in the face of various shocks [6, 2, 3, 1, 4].

Emerging challenges include data granularity requirements for accurate network reconstruction and the need for interoperable standards to combine firm-level transaction data with behavioral metrics. Future research directions should explore hybrid reconstruction frameworks that merge scGM’s structural precision with behavior-based networks’ dynamic adaptability, potentially leveraging machine learning to predict optimal post-crisis network configurations for SME ecosystems [5]. Such advancements would bridge the gap between theoretical network resilience and practical crisis management interventions, particularly for SME-dominated sectors where traditional top-down recovery strategies often prove inadequate.

# Abstract

Organoid-based host models have emerged as transformative tools in infection biology, offering physiologically relevant platforms to study microbial pathogenesis, host-pathogen interactions, and disease mechanisms. This survey systematically examines the applications of organoid technology across infection research, highlighting its advantages over traditional 2D cultures and animal models in recapitulating human tissue architecture and cellular heterogeneity. Key advancements include the successful cultivation of previously uncultivable pathogens like norovirus, elucidation of species-specific tropisms, and quantitative analysis of infection dynamics through deep learning-based image processing. The integration of machine learning approaches with organoid-derived data has significantly improved host-pathogen interaction predictions, while studies of phenotypic switching in microbial populations have revealed mechanisms of persistent infection. Despite current limitations in vascularization and immune component integration, organoids demonstrate immense potential for personalized medicine and therapeutic development. Future directions emphasize the standardization of protocols, enhanced computational integration, and the development of more comprehensive models incorporating immune and vascular components. Collectively, organoid technology represents a paradigm shift in infectious disease research, bridging critical gaps between in vitro models and human pathophysiology.

# 2 Background

# 2.1 Applications in Biomedical Research

Organoids have revolutionized biomedical research by offering advanced tools for drug discovery and personalized medicine. These stem cell-derived three-dimensional structures replicate human tissue architecture and functionality, providing more physiologically relevant studies compared to traditional two-dimensional cultures. Their structural complexity and ability to mimic human tissue microenvironments are crucial for understanding biological processes and disease mechanisms. In drug discovery, organoids serve as high-fidelity platforms for toxicity screening and efficacy testing, reducing reliance on animal models and improving translational potential. Automated contour and cell intensity measurements in brain organoids have enhanced drug response evaluations, offering more reliable therapeutic predictions [1].

In oncology, patient-derived tumor organoids enable personalized therapeutic testing, preserving genetic and phenotypic heterogeneity for accurate treatment response prediction. This is particularly relevant in cancer treatment, where tumor biology variability can significantly influence therapeutic efficacy. Integrating large margin machine learning models with weighted support vector machines enhances predictive capabilities in personalized treatment strategies, facilitating the identification of optimal drug combinations tailored to individual patients [2].

Organoid technology also advances disease modeling for rare genetic disorders, overcoming challenges of scarce patient samples and animal model limitations. Patient-derived iPSCs allow researchers to investigate disease mechanisms and evaluate therapeutic interventions in systems closely mimicking human biology. This approach enhances understanding of cerebral diseases and improves drug testing methodologies. Automated analysis methods in brain organoid technology facilitate detailed cellular structure and interaction studies. Insights into molecular diffusion dynamics in 3D tissue constructs are crucial for understanding stem cell behavior, differentiation, and tissue development, aiding in disease modeling and new treatment discovery, especially in host-pathogen interactions [2, 3, 2, 1]. The combination of organoids with advanced imaging and machine learning techniques is creating new paradigms for high-throughput phenotypic screening and biomarker discovery, showcasing their versatility in addressing diverse biomedical challenges while maintaining human physiological relevance.

# 2.2 Advancements in 3D Tissue Engineering

Advancements in 3D tissue engineering have significantly enhanced the physiological relevance and analytical capabilities of organoid models. Integrating computational methods with traditional imaging techniques, such as deep learning networks for quantitative analysis of organoid contours and cell intensity, overcomes conventional image processing limitations, enabling precise morphological and phenotypic feature measurements in brain organoids [1]. This technology advances understanding of organoid behavior, particularly in disease modeling and therapeutic testing.

Diffusion dynamics in 3D tissue architectures have become a focus area, demonstrating how molecular transport properties influence cellular behavior and disease progression in engineered tissues [3]. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for designing organoid systems that mimic in vivo microenvironments. Computational modeling optimizes scaffold designs and culture conditions, addressing challenges in maintaining organoid viability and functionality over time, essential for reliable basic research and clinical applications.

Machine learning approaches further advance 3D tissue engineering by enhancing host-pathogen interaction predictions. Large margin classifiers with weighted support vector machines and DeNovo negative sampling strategies improve prediction accuracy [2]. These computational innovations complement experimental organoid systems, providing frameworks for analyzing complex host-microbe interaction networks and identifying molecular targets. The synergy between engineered 3D tissues and predictive algorithms represents a paradigm shift in modeling infectious diseases and screening therapeutic interventions, essential for developing targeted strategies to combat pathogens and improve patient outcomes in infectious disease management.

# 2.3 Limitations of Traditional Models

Traditional 2D cell cultures and animal models face fundamental limitations in replicating human physiology and disease pathogenesis, which organoid technology addresses. Two-dimensional cultures fail to capture human tissue’s complex three-dimensional architecture and cellular heterogeneity, leading to altered signaling, proliferation dynamics, and drug responses. The lack of appropriate tissue context results in oversimplified cell-extracellular matrix interactions, inadequately replicating the in vivo environment. This limitation is significant in infectious disease studies, where tissue spatial organization is crucial for understanding pathogen tropism and immune responses. Advancements in 3D tissue engineering and organoid development highlight the importance of molecular diffusion dynamics and concentration gradients in cellular differentiation and tissue development, emphasizing the need for more physiologically relevant models for accurate disease modeling and therapeutic discovery [3, 2].

Animal models provide whole-organism context but suffer from interspecies differences in pathogen susceptibility, immune responses, and tissue microenvironments, limiting translational relevance. Therapeutics effective in animal models often fail in human trials, highlighting these discrepancies. Organoid technology effectively models species-specific tropisms, achieving insights unattainable through conventional animal models, particularly in norovirus and Helicobacter pylori research. This advancement underscores organoids’ potential in studying host-pathogen interactions and developing targeted therapeutic strategies [2, 2, 1].

Organoids preserve human genetic background while incorporating critical 3D tissue architecture and cellular diversity. Computational analyses comparing gene expression profiles demonstrate that organoids resemble human tissues more closely than 2D cultures or animal models across organ systems. Deep learning-based image analysis reveals that organoids maintain physiological cell polarity and differentiation gradients absent in traditional systems [1]. These advantages are valuable for studying host-pathogen interactions, where machine learning approaches integrated with organoid data enable more accurate infection outcome predictions compared to conventional models [2].

Traditional drug development models face limitations in translating findings to human applications, resulting in high attrition rates. Challenges in predicting host-pathogen protein interactions and analyzing brain organoids are critical for understanding disease mechanisms and testing therapies. The absence of standardized quantitative analysis methods and molecular diffusion dynamics in 3D tissue constructs contribute to inefficiencies in drug discovery [2, 3, 2, 1]. Organoids enable highthroughput screening in human-derived tissues while maintaining physiological relevance. Studies show that organoids better predict clinical outcomes, especially for compounds whose efficacy depends on tissue-specific metabolism or cellular crosstalk, addressing a critical need in infectious disease research where species-specific differences complicate therapeutic development.

# 3.2 Phenotypic Switching in Microbial Pathogenesis

Organoids have provided critical insights into phenotypic switching mechanisms of microbial pathogens, revealing how this adaptive strategy influences infection outcomes and therapeutic resistance. Phenotypic switching, involving stochastic transitions between distinct cellular states, enables microbial populations to survive fluctuating host environments and evade immune responses. Intestinal organoid studies have shown how Salmonella enterica uses phenotypic heterogeneity to alternate between invasive and replicative states, optimizing colonization strategies along gastrointestinal tract gradients. Single-cell analysis within organoids has quantified bimodal expression patterns of virulence regulators, emphasizing the necessity of organoid models for exploring dynamic pathogen behaviors.

Human gastric organoids have elucidated Helicobacter pylori’s switching between chronic and acute infection phenotypes in response to epithelial cell differentiation states. Organoids preserve spatial organization for observing localized phenotypic microdomains, where bacterial subpopulations exhibit varied morphologies. Computational modeling of these behaviors, informed by organoid data, improves predictions of antibiotic treatment failure by accounting for transiently resistant subpopulations. This research enhances understanding of H. pylori infections and underscores organoids’ potential to inform strategies against antibiotic resistance.

Brain organoids have revealed how Candida albicans switches between yeast and hyphal forms to breach the blood-brain barrier, with 3D tissue architecture essential for observing invasion patterns mimicking clinical infections. Quantitative image analysis of organoid contours demonstrates correlations between hyphal transition rates and barrier disruption severity, providing parameters for antifungal efficacy testing. Organoids’ capacity to maintain microenvironmental cues driving phenotypic plasticity enables high-resolution observation of infection consequences, informing targeted therapeutic interventions.

Emerging machine learning approaches applied to organoid infection data advance understanding of switching dynamics, with weighted SVM classifiers identifying key host factors modulating microbial phenotypic transitions. This computational-organoid synergy offers strategies to disrupt pathogenic adaptation by targeting signaling networks governing phenotypic switching. Such approaches address antibiotic resistance challenges by circumventing evolutionary advantages conferred by phenotypic heterogeneity. The integration of advanced analytics with organoid technology enhances comprehension of microbial pathogenesis and paves the way for innovative therapeutic strategies, potentially improving patient outcomes in infectious disease contexts.

# 6.1 Current Limitations in Organoid-Based Models

Despite their transformative potential, organoid models face technical and biological limitations that hinder their broader application in infection biology. A significant challenge is the lack of standardized quantitative analysis methods, especially for complex systems like brain organoids, leading to prolonged processing times and inconsistencies [1]. This becomes critical in high-throughput screening, where rapid, reproducible quantification is essential. Diffusion limitations in 3D organoid structures also pose biological constraints, impacting nutrient delivery and cellular viability [3]. These barriers create heterogeneous microenvironments, complicating data interpretation and potentially skewing infection dynamics and drug response profiles.

Simplified representations of biological systems in organoid models may not fully capture the complexity of host-pathogen interactions [2]. This limitation is evident in studies of microbial phenotypic switching, where organoids may not replicate the environmental cues driving pathogen adaptation in vivo. The absence of integrated immune components further restricts the study of comprehensive host responses, particularly for pathogens targeting immune cells. Technical challenges in reproducibility and scalability, such as batch variability in size and cellular composition, introduce experimental noise [3, 1]. This variability complicates infection studies, where subtle differences can alter pathogen tropism and replication efficiency.

Computational integration of organoid data faces limitations in developing predictive models that accurately translate in vitro findings to clinical outcomes. While machine learning approaches have improved interaction predictions [2], current organoid systems may not fully encompass human tissue complexity. Future advancements must focus on improving organoid vascularization, immune integration, and computational frameworks to bridge the gap between simplified in vitro systems and human pathophysiology.

# Abstract

The Hubble tension, a persistent discrepancy between early- and late-universe measurements of the Hubble constant $( H _ { 0 } )$ , has emerged as a critical challenge to the standard $\Lambda { \bf C D M }$ cosmological model. Early-universe probes, such as cosmic microwave background (CMB) observations from Planck and baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO), yield $H _ { 0 } \approx 6 7 - 6 8 \mathrm { k m / s / M p c }$ , while late-universe methods like Cepheid-calibrated Type Ia supernovae and gravitational lensing time delays report values near $\mathrm { 7 3 - 7 4 ~ k m / s / M p c }$ , with tensions now exceeding $5 \sigma$ significance. This survey paper systematically examines the observational foundations, theoretical implications, and proposed resolutions of this tension. We analyze the methodologies underpinning both early- and late-universe measurements, highlighting potential systematic uncertainties in CMB sound horizon calibrations, cosmic distance ladder systematics, and the interplay of cosmological parameters. The paper evaluates extensions to $\Lambda { \bf C D M }$ , including early dark energy models, modified recombination physics, neutrino interactions, and dark sector dynamics, while assessing their ability to reconcile the discrepancy without introducing new tensions. We discuss emerging observational techniquessuch as gravitational wave standard sirens and next-generation CMB experimentsthat promise to refine $H _ { 0 }$ measurements. The synthesis of current evidence suggests that the Hubble tension may necessitate new physics beyond $\Lambda { \bf C D M }$ , with profound implications for our understanding of cosmic acceleration, dark energy, and fundamental gravitational physics. Future research directions emphasize the need for interdisciplinary collaboration, improved systematic error control, and innovative theoretical frameworks to resolve this defining challenge in modern cosmology.

# 1.2 Historical Context and Emergence of the Tension

The Hubble tension emerged as a significant cosmological challenge following the Planck 2013 data release, which reported a value of $H _ { 0 } = 6 7 . 3 \pm 1 . 2 \mathrm { { k m } / \mathrm { { s } / \mathrm { { M p c } } } }$ , systematically lower than local measurements [? ]. This discrepancy was further solidified by subsequent Planck releases and independent early-universe probes, such as baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO), which consistently yielded values around $6 7 { - } 6 8 \mathrm { k m / s / M p c }$ [? ]. Concurrently, late-universe measurements, particularly those from the SH0ES collaboration using Cepheid-calibrated Type Ia supernovae, reported higher values, reaching $\mathrm { 7 3 . 0 4 \pm 1 . 0 4 \ k m / s / M p c }$ [? ]. The tension between these methodologies grew from a $2 - 3 \sigma$ inconsistency to its current $> 5 \sigma$ significance, highlighting a persistent and statistically robust discrepancy [? ].

Key milestones in the recognition of this tension include the 2016 analysis by Bernal et al., which quantified the discrepancy as a $3 . 4 \sigma$ tension between Planck and SH0ES, framing it as a potential crisis for ΛCDM [? ]. Subsequent studies revealed that the tension was not isolated to a single dataset or method; independent late-universe probes, such as gravitational lensing time delays and tip of the red giant branch (TRGB) measurements, corroborated the higher $H _ { 0 }$ values [? ]. The persistence of the tension across diverse observational techniques ruled out localized systematic errors as the sole explanation, prompting broader consideration of new physics [? ].

The historical development of the Hubble tension also intersects with other cosmological anomalies, such as the $S _ { 8 }$ discrepancy, which further complicates the resolution landscape [? ]. Early attempts to reconcile the tension focused on recalibrating the cosmic distance ladder or refining CMB analysis, but these efforts failed to bridge the gap. Theoretical proposals, including early dark energy models and modifications to recombination physics, gained traction as potential resolutions [? ]. The tension’s evolution reflects a broader paradigm shift in cosmology, where inconsistencies in ΛCDM parameters have spurred interdisciplinary efforts to explore alternative frameworks and observational strategies [? ].

The emergence of the Hubble tension has prompted a reevaluation of the methodologies used to measure $H _ { 0 }$ and has sparked discussions regarding the underlying physics that could explain this discrepancy. # 2.1 The Hubble Constant $\left( H _ { 0 } \right)$ and Its Cosmological Significance

The Hubble constant $( H _ { 0 } )$ quantifies the universe’s current expansion rate and is defined as $H _ { 0 } \equiv$ $\dot { a } / a | _ { t = t _ { 0 } }$ , where $a ( t )$ is the scale factor at present cosmic time $t _ { 0 }$ . It establishes the linear HubbleLemaître law at low redshifts $( v \approx H _ { 0 } d )$ and impacts significant cosmological parameters, including the universe’s age $( t _ { 0 } \propto H _ { 0 } ^ { - 1 } ) ,$ ) and critical density $( \rho _ { c } = 3 H _ { 0 } ^ { 2 } / 8 \pi G )$ [? ]. The importance of $H _ { 0 }$ is further highlighted through its determination of the redshift of matter-radiation equality $( z _ { e q } ~ \propto$ $H _ { 0 } ^ { 2 } \Omega _ { m } h ^ { 2 } )$ [? ]. However, a noticeable inconsistency between the $H _ { 0 }$ values obtained from earlyuniverse probes, such as the CMB, and local universe observations challenges the ΛCDM model’s accuracy and has exceeded $5 \sigma$ significance [? ? ? ].

This persistent discrepancy, referred to as the Hubble tension, extends across various observational probes, indicating a possibly deeper cosmological issue. Gravitational wave (GW) astronomy offers independent constraints on $H _ { 0 }$ without relying on traditional cosmic distance ladders [? ]. Modelindependent reconstructions of the universe’s expansion history at different redshifts consistently indicate an underlying tension, hinting at a fundamental issue rather than localized systematic errors [? ]. This tension correlates with other anomalies like the $S _ { 8 }$ discrepancy, suggesting potential inadequacies in the ΛCDM model and motivated reevaluations of current cosmological theories [? ].

New theoretical models seek to address the Hubble tension by proposing novel physics, such as early dark energy and modifications to recombination dynamics, aiming to reconcile early- and lateuniverse constraints with other cosmological evidence [? ]. The resilience of this discrepancy urges the adoption of interdisciplinary approaches that combine gravitational physics, particle cosmology, and astrometric precision to unveil the true nature of cosmic expansion. Being a central parameter, $H _ { 0 }$ remains crucial for testing cosmological models and probing beyond the standard paradigm.

# 2.2 The Cosmic Distance Ladder: Methods and Calibrators

The cosmic distance ladder assembles a sequence of methods to measure extragalactic distances, incorporating geometric methods, standard candles, and cosmological probes. This hierarchical framework begins with parallax measurements, with Gaia’s data providing microarcsecond precision for Milky Way Cepheids [? ]. Such precision supports the period-luminosity (P-L) calibrations of classical Cepheids, anchoring galaxies like the LMC and NGC 4258 [? ].

Intermediate steps employ Cepheids with established distances, refining P-L relations and subsequently anchoring Type Ia supernovae (SNe Ia) as standard candles [? ]. The JWST has notably minimized systematics in crowded stellar photometry, improving SNe Ia calibrations [? ]. While RR Lyrae stars and Type II Cepheids supplement calibrations, they present higher luminosity variation, complicating precision efforts [? ].

The distance ladder culminates with cosmological measurements via SNe Ia, now further calibrated by gravitational wave standard sirens from binary neutron star mergers. This independent approach bypasses traditional ladders, providing direct luminosity distances [? ]. Advances like deep learning with Pantheon SNe data address photometric and extinction errors [? ]. Challenges persist with local inhomogeneities, requiring corrections that minimize induced biases in $H _ { 0 }$ measures [? ].

Systematic errors accrue throughout the ladder’s steps, involving metallicity in Cepheids, SNe environment effects, and photometric uncertainties. Current discrepancies in local $H _ { 0 }$ values linked to these measurements demand ongoing efforts in spectroscopic and infrared methodologies to curtail such uncertainties further [? ? ]. The cosmic distance ladder is indispensable for accurate cosmological parameter estimation, impacting the Hubble tension’s resolution.

# 2.4 Standard Candles and Their Limitations

Standard candles, primarily Type Ia supernovae (SNe Ia) and Cepheid variables, are pivotal for precise $H _ { 0 }$ determination through their well-established luminosity-distance relations. SNe Ia are thoroughly integrated within the cosmic distance ladder, calibrated via reliable anchors like Cepheids or the TRGB method, often yielding $H _ { 0 }$ values exceeding $7 0 ~ \mathrm { k m / s / M p c }$ . Such measurements starkly contrast with lower $H _ { 0 }$ values derived from CMB observations, fueling the Hubble tension, confirmed by additional independent means, suggesting systemic errors in SNe or Cepheid calibrations are not principal contributors [? ? ? ? ].

SNe Ia maintain prominence as late-universe standard candles, achieving precision through empirical peak luminosity calibrations like the Phillips relation. The SH0ES collaboration’s findings at $H _ { 0 } = 7 3 . 0 4 \pm 1 . 0 4 \mathrm { k m / s / M p c }$ highlight effective calibration techniques, despite persistent systematic errors from metallicity dependencies, progenitor environments, and photometric zero offsets [? ? ]. SNe Ia’s applicability is also limited by their sub- $z 1 . 5$ range, barring their use in high-redshift cosmology [? ]. Despite JWST’s advancements in reducing Cepheid photometry errors, calibration inconsistencies across anchors like the LMC and NGC 4258 persist [? ].

Cepheid variables remain vital to the distance ladder, with P-L relations grounded in parallax data. Classical Cepheids (DCEPs) and their pulsation mechanics are influenced by stellar properties, thus influencing distance reliability [? ]. Gaia’s data refine Milky Way Cepheid distances, yet extragalactic blending and crowding hinder accuracy [? ]. The LMC adds significant P-L calibrations for Type II Cepheids (T2Cs), though metallicity and extinction factors restrict precision [? ? ].

Innovations aim to transcend these barriers, enhancing standard candle dependability. Gravitational wave standard sirens, emanating from binary neutron star events, offer independent absolute distances beyond conventional ladders [? ]. Quasars, though variably complex, hold potential as high-redshift candles, bridging applicability gaps in cosmology [? ]. The EPIC method promises improved signal-to-noise ratios, facilitating fainter source accessibility [? ].

## 3.1 Wave Propagation and Material Behavior

### 3.1.1 Topological phononic structures and wave attenuation models
Topological phononic structures represent a class of materials engineered to manipulate sound waves through topological principles, offering robust wave manipulation and bandgap formation. These structures leverage the concept of topological insulators, where phonon modes are protected against disorder and defects, enabling unidirectional wave propagation. The design of such structures typically involves periodic arrangements of materials with specific geometries and material properties, leading to the emergence of topologically protected edge states. These states allow for the confinement and guided transport of phonons, which is critical for applications in noise control, vibration isolation, and acoustic filtering. The interplay between topology and phonon dynamics is a key focus in understanding the unique wave attenuation properties of these systems.

Wave attenuation models in topological phononic structures are essential for predicting and optimizing the performance of such systems. These models often incorporate both analytical and numerical approaches to describe the interaction of phonons with the structural topology. Analytical models, such as the perturbative analytic approach, provide insights into the formation of bandgaps and the behavior of phonon-assisted transitions between different polariton states. These models are validated against full numerical simulations, which account for the complex interactions within the material. The accuracy of these models is crucial for the design of efficient phononic devices, as they enable the prediction of wave propagation characteristics and the identification of optimal structural parameters.

The study of topological phononic structures and their wave attenuation models is a rapidly evolving field with significant implications for both fundamental research and practical applications. By integrating topological concepts with phononics, researchers can develop materials with unprecedented control over sound wave propagation. This integration not only enhances the understanding of wave-matter interactions but also opens new avenues for the design of advanced acoustic devices. Future work in this area will likely focus on improving the predictive capabilities of wave attenuation models and exploring novel topological configurations that can further enhance the performance of phononic systems.

### 3.1.2 Phase structure and piezoelectric property optimization
The phase structure of piezoelectric materials plays a critical role in determining their electromechanical performance. The coexistence of different crystallographic phases, such as tetragonal, rhombohedral, and monoclinic, influences the material's polarization switching behavior, domain wall mobility, and overall piezoelectric response. Optimizing the phase composition through controlled synthesis and processing techniques, such as temperature annealing or chemical doping, enables the enhancement of piezoelectric coefficients. This optimization is particularly important in lead-free piezoelectric ceramics, where phase transitions can be tuned to achieve high electromechanical coupling factors and improved operational stability under varying environmental conditions [1].

Piezoelectric property optimization also involves the manipulation of domain structures within the material. The alignment and density of ferroelectric domains directly affect the material's ability to convert mechanical stress into electrical charge and vice versa. Advanced characterization techniques, such as piezoresponse force microscopy and X-ray diffraction, are employed to analyze domain configurations and their evolution under external stimuli. By tailoring the microstructure through controlled poling processes or epitaxial growth, it is possible to enhance the material's piezoelectric performance. These strategies are crucial for applications in sensors, actuators, and energy harvesting devices, where high sensitivity and efficiency are required.

Furthermore, the interplay between phase structure and material composition is essential for achieving optimal piezoelectric properties. The substitution of elements in the crystal lattice can induce phase transitions that alter the material's functional characteristics. For example, the introduction of A-site or B-site cations can stabilize specific phases and modify the Curie temperature, which is critical for high-temperature applications. Computational modeling and first-principles calculations are increasingly used to predict phase stability and guide the design of new piezoelectric materials. By integrating experimental and theoretical approaches, researchers can systematically optimize the phase structure to achieve superior piezoelectric performance tailored for specific technological needs.

## 3.2 Acoustic-Optical Integration for Cell Analysis

### 3.2.1 Raman spectroscopy and acoustofluidic coupling
Raman spectroscopy has emerged as a powerful analytical tool for probing molecular vibrations and structural properties of materials, often in conjunction with acoustofluidic systems to enhance sensitivity and resolution. The coupling of Raman spectroscopy with acoustofluidics leverages the ability of acoustic waves to manipulate and concentrate particles or molecules within a fluidic environment, thereby increasing the likelihood of molecular interactions and enhancing the Raman signal. This synergy allows for the detection of low-concentration species and the study of dynamic processes in real-time, making it particularly useful in biomedical and chemical sensing applications. The integration of these two techniques requires careful consideration of acoustic field parameters, such as frequency and intensity, to ensure optimal particle manipulation without compromising the integrity of the sample.

Acoustofluidic coupling in Raman spectroscopy involves the use of standing or traveling acoustic waves to generate microfluidic flows that guide particles or molecules toward the laser excitation region. This approach not only enhances the spatial resolution of the Raman signal but also enables the analysis of heterogeneous samples by facilitating controlled mixing and separation. The acoustic forces acting on the particles are influenced by their size, density, and the properties of the surrounding medium, necessitating precise tuning of the acoustofluidic system. Additionally, the interaction between the acoustic field and the optical components must be carefully managed to avoid signal interference or degradation. These challenges highlight the need for advanced design strategies and material selection to achieve reliable and reproducible results in acoustofluidic Raman spectroscopy.

The application of Raman spectroscopy in acoustofluidic systems has opened new avenues for studying complex fluid dynamics and molecular interactions at the microscale. By combining the non-invasive nature of Raman spectroscopy with the precise control offered by acoustofluidics, researchers can investigate a wide range of phenomena, from colloidal suspensions to biological cells. This integration also facilitates the development of compact and portable sensing devices, which are essential for field-deployable applications. Future advancements in this area will likely focus on improving the efficiency of particle manipulation, enhancing signal-to-noise ratios, and expanding the compatibility of acoustofluidic systems with various Raman spectroscopy configurations. In addition to traditional computational approaches, emerging techniques such as machine learning and data-driven modeling are being explored to enhance the efficiency and accuracy of microscale acoustic flow simulations. ### 3.3.2 Acoustic wave attenuation and mixing enhancement
Acoustic wave attenuation and mixing enhancement play a critical role in the behavior of acoustic fields within complex media, particularly in scenarios involving wave propagation through heterogeneous or structured environments. The attenuation of acoustic waves is influenced by factors such as the medium's viscosity, density, and the presence of inclusions or interfaces, which can lead to energy dissipation and modification of wave characteristics. In the context of phonon-assisted transitions, the interaction between acoustic waves and polariton states results in the formation of band gaps and Bragg reflection spectra, which are essential for controlling wave propagation [4]. These phenomena are often analyzed using perturbative analytic approaches that provide insights into the spectral properties of the system, complementing full numerical simulations to validate the accuracy of the theoretical models.

Mixing enhancement in acoustofluidic systems is closely tied to the generation of acoustic streaming flows, which can significantly improve the efficiency of fluid mixing in microscale environments [2]. Acoustic streaming arises from the nonlinear interaction of sound waves with the fluid, leading to the formation of vortices and enhanced convective transport. This effect is particularly useful in applications requiring rapid and uniform mixing, such as in microfluidic devices or nanoparticle dispersion. The efficiency of mixing can be further optimized by controlling the acoustic wave parameters, such as frequency, amplitude, and direction of propagation. By leveraging the interplay between wave attenuation and streaming effects, it is possible to design systems that achieve enhanced mixing while minimizing energy consumption and maintaining precise control over fluid dynamics.

The study of acoustic wave attenuation and mixing enhancement also involves the analysis of nonlinear effects, such as harmonic generation and the formation of standing wave patterns, which can influence both the propagation and interaction of acoustic waves. These effects are particularly relevant in systems where the acoustic field interacts with particles or interfaces, leading to phenomena such as acoustic radiation force and particle manipulation [3]. Theoretical models and numerical simulations are essential tools for understanding these complex interactions, allowing for the prediction and optimization of system performance. By integrating these insights, researchers can develop advanced acoustofluidic techniques that offer improved control over mixing processes, enabling applications in fields ranging from biotechnology to materials science.

Phased-array transducers have emerged as a critical tool in the field of DNA shearing, offering precise control over ultrasonic energy delivery to achieve targeted fragmentation. These transducers consist of multiple individual elements that can be independently controlled to generate complex acoustic wave patterns. By adjusting the phase and amplitude of the signals applied to each element, the resulting acoustic field can be steered and focused in specific regions, enabling localized and controlled DNA cleavage. This capability is particularly valuable in applications requiring uniform or tailored fragment sizes, such as next-generation sequencing and genomic library preparation. The ability to dynamically shape the acoustic field allows for enhanced flexibility and adaptability in DNA shearing processes [5].

The design of phased-array transducers for DNA shearing involves careful consideration of the acoustic properties of the medium and the interaction between the transducer elements and the sample [5]. The transducers are typically fabricated using piezoelectric materials, such as lead zirconate titanate (PZT), which convert electrical signals into mechanical vibrations. These vibrations generate ultrasonic waves that propagate through the sample, inducing shear forces that break the DNA strands. The spatial distribution of these forces can be optimized by varying the excitation patterns of the transducer elements, allowing for precise control over the shearing process. Additionally, the use of phased arrays enables the generation of complex waveforms, such as standing or traveling waves, which can further enhance the efficiency and uniformity of DNA fragmentation.

Recent advancements in phased-array transducer technology have focused on improving the resolution and control of the acoustic field to achieve more consistent and reproducible DNA shearing [5]. This includes the development of high-frequency transducers with fine spatial resolution, as well as the integration of feedback mechanisms to monitor and adjust the shearing process in real time. As research continues, further optimization of these transducers is expected to expand their applications in biotechnology and biomedical research.

## 3.5 Material and Process Innovations

### 3.5.1 Ultrasonic exfoliation of graphene
Ultrasonic exfoliation of graphene is a widely used technique for producing high-quality graphene from graphite [7]. This method relies on the application of high-frequency sound waves to induce cavitation in a liquid medium, which generates intense shear forces that separate individual graphene layers from the bulk material. The process involves dispersing graphite in a suitable solvent and subjecting it to ultrasonic energy, which causes the formation and collapse of microbubbles. These collapsing bubbles create localized high-pressure and high-temperature conditions that facilitate the exfoliation of graphene. The efficiency of this method is influenced by various parameters, including the intensity and duration of the ultrasonic waves, the choice of solvent, and the concentration of the graphite suspension. Optimizing these factors is crucial for achieving a high yield of few-layer or single-layer graphene.

The ultrasonic exfoliation process is particularly advantageous due to its simplicity, scalability, and compatibility with a variety of solvents and surfactants. It allows for the production of graphene in liquid form, which is beneficial for subsequent processing and application in composite materials, coatings, and electronic devices. However, the method also presents challenges, such as the tendency to produce defects and the difficulty in controlling the thickness of the exfoliated graphene. To address these issues, researchers have explored the use of additional heating or the incorporation of functional groups to enhance the exfoliation efficiency and improve the quality of the resulting graphene. These modifications can significantly reduce the time required for exfoliation and increase the overall yield, making the process more suitable for industrial applications.

Recent advancements in ultrasonic exfoliation have focused on optimizing the parameters to achieve better control over the exfoliation process [7]. Studies have shown that the use of low ultrasonic intensity over extended periods can lead to the formation of high-quality graphene with minimal defects. Additionally, the integration of ultrasonic techniques with other methods, such as chemical functionalization or mechanical stirring, has been explored to further enhance the exfoliation efficiency. These combined approaches offer promising pathways for the large-scale production of graphene with tailored properties. As research continues, the development of more efficient and controlled ultrasonic exfoliation techniques will play a critical role in expanding the applications of graphene in various technological fields [7].

### 3.5.2 Patterned backgating for 2D device fabrication
Patterned backgating is a crucial technique in the fabrication of two-dimensional (2D) devices, particularly those involving double quantum well structures or heterointerfaces where precise control over the wavefunction is required [8]. This method enables the application of gate voltages to the backside of a device while maintaining the top surface free from metallization, which is essential for scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) or atomic force microscopy (AFM) studies. The process involves lithographic alignment of the backgate with existing top-side features, ensuring precise spatial registration. This approach allows for the modulation of charge carrier density and the manipulation of electronic properties without disrupting the integrity of the top surface, making it a vital step in the development of advanced 2D heterostructures.

The implementation of patterned backgating typically involves a combination of wet etching and lithographic techniques to thin the sample and align the backgate with high precision. Conventional ultrasonic ball bonders can be utilized to establish electrical contacts on both sides of the device, facilitating the integration of gate electrodes. This technique is particularly advantageous in devices where the top surface must remain unaltered for optical or electronic characterization. By employing a single-sided mask aligner, the process becomes more accessible and cost-effective, as it avoids the need for complex and expensive equipment. The adaptability of this method to various materials and device architectures further enhances its utility in 2D device fabrication.

The significance of patterned backgating extends beyond basic device functionality, as it enables the exploration of novel electronic phenomena at heterointerfaces [8]. By precisely controlling the electrostatic potential, researchers can tailor the properties of 2D materials for specific applications, such as transistors, sensors, and quantum devices. This technique also plays a critical role in the study of surface states and interfacial effects, which are fundamental to understanding the behavior of 2D systems. As the field of 2D materials continues to evolve, the development of robust and scalable patterned backgating methods will be essential in advancing the performance and functionality of next-generation electronic devices [8].

### 3.6.1 Deterministic models for particle orbits
Deterministic models for particle orbits provide a foundational framework for understanding the motion of particles in complex fluid environments, particularly under the influence of acoustic and electromagnetic fields. These models typically rely on the integration of forces such as acoustic radiation, fluid drag, and buoyancy to predict the trajectories of suspended particles. By assuming a well-defined set of initial conditions and governing equations, deterministic approaches enable precise predictions of particle behavior in controlled settings. Such models are essential for applications ranging from particle manipulation in microfluidic devices to the analysis of colloidal suspensions under external perturbations. The accuracy of these models is heavily dependent on the fidelity of the underlying physical assumptions and the numerical methods employed for solving the governing equations.

In the context of ultrasonic resonators, deterministic models are often coupled with acoustic field calculations to determine the spatial distribution and movement of particles [3]. These models incorporate the effects of standing wave patterns, which create regions of high and low pressure that influence particle positioning. The interaction between the particle and the acoustic field is typically modeled using a combination of wave equations and force balance equations, leading to a comprehensive description of particle dynamics. Additionally, the inclusion of fluid flow models allows for the consideration of hydrodynamic effects, such as viscous drag and convective transport, which further refine the prediction of particle orbits. This integration of multiple physical phenomena ensures that deterministic models remain robust and applicable across a wide range of experimental configurations.

Recent advancements in deterministic modeling have focused on improving the resolution and accuracy of particle trajectory predictions, particularly in non-uniform and time-varying fields. Techniques such as high-order finite element methods and adaptive meshing have been employed to capture the intricate details of particle motion. Furthermore, the incorporation of stochastic elements into deterministic frameworks has enabled the study of particle behavior in more realistic, noisy environments. These developments have broadened the applicability of deterministic models, making them indispensable tools for both theoretical analysis and practical applications in fields such as biomedical engineering, materials science, and environmental monitoring.

### 3.6.2 Rotational noise effects on particle distribution
Rotational noise significantly influences the distribution of particles in acoustic and fluidic systems, particularly in environments where both rotational and translational forces are present. This effect arises due to the interaction between the rotational motion of the fluid and the inherent stochasticity of the noise, which can alter the trajectories and spatial organization of particles. In systems where acoustic waves drive fluid motion, such as in acoustic streaming or ultrasonic manipulation, rotational noise can disrupt the ordered patterns typically observed in particle distributions. This disruption is especially pronounced in regions of high shear or vorticity, where the noise-induced fluctuations can lead to deviations from expected particle behavior. Understanding these effects is crucial for applications involving particle separation, concentration, or controlled assembly, where precise spatial control is essential.

The impact of rotational noise on particle distribution is closely tied to the characteristics of the acoustic field and the properties of the particles themselves. For instance, particles with higher inertia or larger size may exhibit different responses compared to smaller or lighter particles, as their motion is less affected by the random fluctuations induced by noise. Additionally, the frequency and intensity of the acoustic waves, as well as the viscosity and density of the surrounding fluid, play a critical role in determining how rotational noise influences particle dynamics. Numerical simulations and experimental studies have shown that rotational noise can lead to the formation of non-uniform particle distributions, with clustering or dispersion depending on the interplay between noise strength and the dominant acoustic forces.

In the context of acoustic manipulation and fluid dynamics, the effects of rotational noise on particle distribution must be carefully considered to ensure the reliability and efficiency of the system. This includes accounting for noise in both the design and control of acoustic devices, as well as in the interpretation of experimental data. By analyzing the statistical properties of particle motion under rotational noise, researchers can develop more robust models for predicting and controlling particle behavior. These insights are essential for advancing applications such as microfluidics, particle sorting, and acoustic levitation, where the interplay between noise and acoustic forces determines the overall performance.

### 3.7.2 Mathematical frameworks for photo-acoustic tomography
Photo-acoustic tomography (PAT) relies on a robust mathematical framework to reconstruct the optical and acoustic properties of biological tissues from measured acoustic signals [9]. The fundamental problem involves solving the wave equation for the acoustic pressure generated by the photo-acoustic effect, which is driven by the absorption of optical energy. The governing equations incorporate the propagation of both light and ultrasound waves, with the latter being influenced by the spatial distribution of the optical absorption coefficient [9]. These equations are typically solved using analytical or numerical methods, with the latter being more prevalent due to the complexity of real-world geometries and heterogeneous media. The mathematical formulation often includes the Helmholtz equation for the optical transport and the wave equation for the acoustic pressure, which are coupled through the photo-acoustic source term.

The reconstruction process in PAT involves inverting the measured acoustic signals to recover the initial pressure distribution, which is proportional to the optical absorption coefficient. This inverse problem is inherently ill-posed and requires regularization techniques to stabilize the solution. Common approaches include time-reversal methods, iterative optimization algorithms, and model-based reconstructions that incorporate prior knowledge of the medium's properties. The choice of mathematical framework depends on the specific application, such as the desired spatial resolution, the type of illumination, and the availability of multi-angle or multi-frequency data. Additionally, the mathematical models must account for the effects of acoustic attenuation, dispersion, and the finite size of the detectors, which can significantly impact the accuracy of the reconstructed images.

Recent advancements in PAT have focused on improving the mathematical models to handle more complex scenarios, such as anisotropic media, non-linear acoustic effects, and multi-modal imaging. These enhancements often involve the use of advanced numerical methods, such as finite element or finite difference schemes, to solve the governing equations with higher accuracy and efficiency. Furthermore, the integration of machine learning techniques has shown promise in accelerating the reconstruction process and improving image quality. The mathematical frameworks continue to evolve to address the challenges posed by clinical applications, where high-resolution and real-time imaging are critical. These developments underscore the importance of a solid theoretical foundation in advancing the capabilities of PAT for biomedical imaging and diagnostics.

## 3.9 Structural and Mechanical Analysis

### 3.9.1 Strain response and protein gel rupture prediction
The strain response of protein gels is a critical factor in understanding their mechanical behavior under external forces. These gels, such as those made of sodium caseinate, exhibit nonlinear mechanical properties that can be analyzed through Fourier analysis of the strain response [12]. This analysis reveals that their mechanical behavior can be scaled onto a single master curve, independent of gel concentration. ## 5.3 Target Detection and Analysis

### 5.3.1 DORT method for multiple scattering suppression
The DORT (Decomposition of the Time Reversed Operator) method is a powerful technique for suppressing multiple scattering effects in imaging and detection applications. It operates by decomposing the response matrix of a wavefield into singular values and vectors, allowing for the isolation of contributions from individual scatterers. By applying DORT to a filtered matrix that has been processed to remove multiple scattering components, the method can effectively detect targets even in complex environments where multiple scattering is prevalent [25]. The key to this approach lies in the identification of the dominant singular value, which is used as a detection criterion to determine the presence of a target. This capability makes DORT particularly useful in scenarios where traditional imaging techniques fail due to the overwhelming influence of multiple scattering [24].

In cases where a target is hidden behind a strongly scattering medium, the direct application of DORT to the raw response matrix may not yield accurate results [25]. This is because multiple scattering can distort the singular value distribution, making it difficult to associate the first singular value with the actual target [25]. To address this issue, the method involves a preprocessing step that separates single- and multiple-scattering contributions from the total response [24]. This separation is based on the properties of the response matrix and allows for a more accurate identification of the target's position and characteristics. The effectiveness of DORT in such scenarios has been demonstrated through various experimental and numerical studies, highlighting its robustness in challenging environments.

The DORT method's ability to isolate and suppress multiple scattering effects has significant implications for the development of advanced imaging and detection systems. As research continues to refine and expand the capabilities of DORT, its role in overcoming the limitations of traditional imaging methods is expected to grow, offering new possibilities for improved detection and analysis in complex media.

### 5.3.2 Single scattering filter for target identification
The single scattering filter (SSF) plays a critical role in target identification by isolating the deterministic coherence of single-scattered signals from the complex wavefield [25]. The filtered matrix, denoted as $\mathbf{K}^{\mathbf{F}}$, allows for the application of advanced techniques such as DORT (Decomposition of the Time Reversal Operator), which can identify and localize targets with greater precision despite the challenging scattering environment.

In scenarios where multiple scattering dominates, direct application of conventional methods like DORT to the original array response matrix $\mathbf{K}$ often fails due to the distortion caused by scattered waves [25]. The SSF not only improves the reliability of detection but also provides insights into the scattering characteristics of the medium, which can be used to validate or refine existing imaging models.

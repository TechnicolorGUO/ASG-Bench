Additive manufacturing (AM) has emerged as a transformative technology, revolutionizing traditional manufacturing by enabling the layer-by-layer fabrication of complex geometries with minimal material waste. This survey paper provides a comprehensive overview of the current state and future directions of AM, focusing on its integration into business models and the technological advancements driving its adoption. The paper examines advanced 3D printing techniques, material innovations, computational methods, and the role of robotics and artificial intelligence in enhancing manufacturing processes. It also addresses critical challenges such as quality control, security, and scalability, which are essential for the widespread implementation of AM in industrial settings. Key findings highlight the potential of deep learning for filament geometry analysis, the importance of optical and imaging technologies in non-destructive evaluation, and the role of multi-layer inkjet printing in creating complex, functional components. The integration of robotics and augmented reality is shown to enhance automation and precision, while cloud-based platforms like Additive MaaS are reshaping business models by enabling on-demand production. This paper underscores the interdisciplinary nature of AM research and emphasizes the need for continued innovation to overcome existing limitations. As AM continues to evolve, its impact on industry, healthcare, and beyond is expected to grow, making it a cornerstone of modern manufacturing.

# 3 Advanced 3D Printing Techniques and Materials

## 3.1 Geometric and Material Innovation in 3D Fabrication

### 3.1.1 Topographical Sensing and Surface Profiling
Topographical sensing and surface profiling play a critical role in non-destructive inspection (NDI) by providing detailed information about the surface geometry and microstructure of materials. These techniques are essential for identifying surface defects, characterizing roughness, and ensuring compliance with manufacturing specifications. Traditional methods such as optical coherence tomography (OCT) and confocal microscopy offer high-resolution surface data but often lack the capability for large-area scans or sub-surface imaging [7]. In contrast, emerging approaches like hyperspectral imaging and structured light scanning provide faster, more scalable solutions, though they typically sacrifice resolution for speed. The integration of these methods into automated manufacturing systems is crucial for real-time quality control and defect detection.

Surface profiling techniques must balance resolution, speed, and adaptability to different material properties. For instance, fiber-based micro-objectives and 3D-printed lenses are being explored to achieve high-NA imaging of microstructures, enabling precise surface mapping. These systems often require careful alignment and calibration to ensure accuracy, particularly when dealing with complex geometries or varying surface reflectivity. Additionally, the use of deep learning algorithms for sensor data processing has shown promise in enhancing the segmentation and classification of surface features, improving the overall reliability of topographical analysis. Such advancements are vital for applications ranging from additive manufacturing to biomedical device inspection.

Despite progress, challenges remain in achieving high-resolution, non-contact surface profiling that is both fast and cost-effective. Current systems often struggle with maintaining accuracy over large areas or in dynamic environments, and the integration of sub-surface imaging remains limited. Future developments must focus on overcoming these limitations through improved sensor design, advanced data processing techniques, and better compatibility with industrial workflows. By addressing these challenges, topographical sensing and surface profiling can become a cornerstone of next-generation non-destructive evaluation and quality assurance in manufacturing.

### 3.1.2 Multi-Layer Inkjet and Structural Control
Multi-layer inkjet printing represents a critical advancement in additive manufacturing, enabling the precise deposition of multiple materials and functional layers within a single fabrication process. This technique allows for the creation of complex, heterogeneous structures by sequentially applying different inks, each tailored to specific mechanical, electrical, or optical properties. Structural control in multi-layer inkjet systems is achieved through careful optimization of parameters such as ink viscosity, nozzle geometry, droplet size, and deposition speed. These factors influence the interlayer adhesion, surface roughness, and overall dimensional accuracy of the printed object. The ability to control these parameters at a microscopic level is essential for achieving the desired performance in applications ranging from flexible electronics to biomedical devices.

Structural control in multi-layer inkjet systems is further enhanced by the integration of real-time monitoring and feedback mechanisms. These systems employ sensors and imaging techniques to detect defects, such as voids or misaligned layers, during the printing process. This capability enables immediate adjustments to the printing parameters, ensuring consistent quality and minimizing the need for post-processing. Additionally, the use of advanced algorithms for path planning and layer-wise optimization allows for the fabrication of intricate internal structures that would be challenging to achieve with conventional methods. By combining precise material deposition with adaptive control strategies, multi-layer inkjet printing offers a powerful approach to fabricate components with tailored functionalities and improved mechanical integrity.

The development of multi-layer inkjet systems also necessitates the design of compatible inks and substrates that can support the required structural and functional properties. This involves the formulation of inks with appropriate rheological characteristics, as well as the selection of substrates that can withstand the printing process without degrading. Furthermore, the integration of multi-layer inkjet with other fabrication techniques, such as 3D printing or thermal drawing, opens new possibilities for creating hybrid structures with enhanced performance. These advancements highlight the importance of interdisciplinary research in optimizing both the hardware and materials used in multi-layer inkjet systems, paving the way for more sophisticated and reliable additive manufacturing solutions.

## 3.2 Optical and Imaging Advancements

### 3.2.1 X-ray and Crystal Optics for High-Speed 3D Imaging
X-ray and crystal optics play a pivotal role in advancing high-speed 3D imaging, particularly in non-destructive inspection (NDI) of materials such as ceramics. Traditional techniques like X-ray computed tomography (XCT) require sample rotation, which limits their applicability in industrial settings and introduces operational complexities due to the use of hazardous radiation. To overcome these limitations, X-ray multi-projection imaging (XMPI) has emerged as a promising approach, leveraging Bragg crystal optics to split the X-ray beam into multiple beamlets. This enables simultaneous multi-angle imaging of the sample without rotation, significantly improving acquisition speed. By eliminating mechanical rotation, XMPI also mitigates shear forces that can disrupt delicate sample dynamics, making it ideal for high-speed 3D imaging at kHz or even MHz rates.

Recent advancements in X-ray sources, such as free-electron lasers (XFELs) and synchrotrons, have further enhanced the potential of high-speed 3D imaging [8]. XFELs, with their high flux per pulse and MHz repetition rates, offer the necessary brightness and temporal resolution for ultra-fast imaging [8]. Crystal optics, including monochromators and beam splitters, are essential for shaping and directing the X-ray beam, ensuring optimal interaction with the sample. These components enable precise control over beam divergence and intensity, which is critical for achieving high-resolution 3D reconstructions. Additionally, the integration of advanced detectors and computational reconstruction algorithms has improved the fidelity and speed of 3D image generation, making it feasible for real-time or near-real-time applications.

The application of X-ray and crystal optics in high-speed 3D imaging is not without challenges. The complexity of optical systems, particularly at high numerical apertures, requires sophisticated designs to maintain beam quality and minimize aberrations. Furthermore, the integration of these components into compact and robust systems remains a significant engineering hurdle. Despite these challenges, ongoing research into novel materials, fabrication techniques, and computational methods continues to push the boundaries of what is possible. As a result, X-ray and crystal optics are poised to become a cornerstone of next-generation high-speed 3D imaging technologies, enabling new capabilities in materials science, industrial inspection, and biomedical imaging.

### 3.2.2 Volumetric and Microstructured Fabrication Techniques
Volumetric and microstructured fabrication techniques have emerged as critical enablers for advanced manufacturing, particularly in applications requiring high-resolution sub-surface imaging and complex geometries. Additive manufacturing (AM) methods, such as stereolithography (SLA) and two-photon polymerization, allow for the creation of intricate microstructures with precise control over material distribution and internal features [2]. These techniques are especially valuable in addressing volume defects that arise during the printing process, such as air pockets and inconsistent material deposition, which can compromise part performance. The integration of non-destructive imaging techniques, such as X-ray or optical coherence tomography, with these fabrication processes enables real-time monitoring and quality control, ensuring adherence to stringent performance specifications.

The development of high-numerical-aperture (NA) 3D-printed micro-objectives has further expanded the capabilities of volumetric fabrication. By employing no-core fibers and optimizing the shape of aspheric lenses, it is possible to achieve NAs up to 0.6, which is essential for efficient coupling of light from quantum dots or other nanoscale sources [9]. These micro-objectives are typically fabricated using single-lens designs, reducing complexity and improving reliability, especially under challenging conditions like cryogenic temperatures. The elimination of gluing steps and the use of flexible fiber and sample mounts enhance the robustness and alignment accuracy of such systems. Additionally, 3D printing-based preform manufacturing allows for the creation of multi-material structures with complex internal geometries, such as hollow cores and small channels, which are difficult to achieve with traditional molding or extrusion techniques [10].

Despite these advancements, challenges remain in achieving fully automated fabrication of 3D geometries with programmable magnetization directions or complex microstructures [11]. Current methods often require manual intervention or are limited in their ability to produce intricate, multi-material components. Innovations in volumetric free-form representations (V-reps) and isogeometric optimization frameworks are addressing these limitations by enabling the design and manufacturing of lightweight, high-performance structures with internal cavities [12]. Similarly, the application of 3D printing to create volumetric knits with programmable mechanical properties demonstrates the potential for new classes of architected materials [13]. These developments highlight the transformative potential of volumetric and microstructured fabrication techniques in enabling next-generation devices and systems.

## 3.3 Computational and Segmentation Approaches

### 3.3.1 Deep Learning for Filament Geometry and Quality Control
Deep learning has emerged as a transformative tool in the realm of filament geometry and quality control within additive manufacturing (AM), particularly in processes such as fused deposition modeling (FDM) and selective continuous composite printing (SC3DP). Traditional methods for assessing filament quality often rely on manual inspection or rule-based algorithms, which are limited in their ability to handle complex, high-resolution data. Deep learning models, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and 3D convolutional architectures, have demonstrated superior performance in segmenting and classifying filament geometries from diverse sensor data sources, including cameras, structured light scanners (SLS), and terrestrial laser scanners (TLS) [1]. These models can detect subtle deviations in filament dimensions, surface irregularities, and internal defects, enabling real-time monitoring and feedback during the printing process.

The integration of deep learning into filament quality control also addresses the challenges posed by sub-surface defects, which are often invisible to surface inspection techniques. By leveraging high-resolution imaging data and advanced feature extraction techniques, deep learning models can infer the internal structure of filaments and predict potential failure points. This capability is critical for ensuring the mechanical integrity and performance of printed components, especially in applications where structural reliability is paramount. Furthermore, the adaptability of deep learning models to different sensor modalities allows for flexible deployment across various AM systems, enhancing their practicality and scalability in industrial settings.

The application of deep learning in this domain also extends to the optimization of printing parameters and the prediction of print outcomes. By training on large datasets of filament geometries and corresponding print quality metrics, these models can identify optimal printing conditions that minimize defects and improve overall part quality. This data-driven approach not only enhances the efficiency of the AM process but also supports the development of autonomous systems capable of self-correcting and adapting to changing conditions. As the field continues to evolve, the synergy between deep learning and AM is expected to drive significant advancements in filament geometry and quality control, paving the way for more reliable and high-performance additive manufacturing solutions.

### 3.3.2 Defect Localization and Image Enhancement Techniques
Defect localization and image enhancement techniques play a critical role in ensuring the quality and reliability of additively manufactured (AM) components, particularly in advanced materials like ceramics. Traditional surface inspection methods often fail to detect sub-surface defects such as air pockets or inconsistent material deposition, which can significantly impact the mechanical and functional performance of the final product. To address this, non-destructive imaging techniques like mid-infrared optical coherence tomography (MIR OCT) have been employed to track defects throughout the entire manufacturing process. These techniques provide high-resolution, sub-surface imaging capabilities, enabling the identification of defects that remain invisible to conventional inspection methods. Such capabilities are essential for achieving the precision required in high-performance applications, such as those in aerospace or biomedical engineering.

Image enhancement techniques are integral to improving the visibility and interpretability of defect features in AM components. These methods often involve advanced signal processing, machine learning algorithms, and multi-modal imaging approaches to enhance contrast, reduce noise, and extract meaningful features from raw data. For instance, in the context of 3D-printed alumina parts, image enhancement can help distinguish between voids, inclusions, and other microstructural anomalies. By integrating these techniques with real-time monitoring systems, it becomes possible to detect and correct defects during the printing process, thereby reducing waste and improving overall manufacturing efficiency. The combination of high-resolution imaging and sophisticated image processing is key to achieving the stringent quality standards demanded by modern AM applications.

The integration of defect localization and image enhancement techniques into the AM workflow requires careful consideration of both hardware and software components. Hardware must support high-resolution, non-contact imaging with sufficient speed to accommodate real-time or near-real-time monitoring. Software, on the other hand, must be capable of processing large datasets and applying advanced algorithms to identify and characterize defects accurately. The development of such systems is crucial for enabling the transition from prototyping to high-volume, high-precision manufacturing. As AM technologies continue to evolve, the refinement of these techniques will be essential in achieving the goal of zero-defect, zero-waste production, particularly in critical applications where material integrity is paramount.

## 3.4 Advanced Material Synthesis and Integration

### 3.4.1 Magnetic Nanoparticles and Programmable Microrobots
Magnetic nanoparticles and programmable microrobots represent a significant advancement in the field of microscale actuation and control, enabling precise manipulation and dynamic reconfiguration in complex environments [11]. These systems leverage the unique properties of magnetic materials, where external magnetic fields can be used to induce motion and shape changes in microrobots composed of magnetic particles embedded in soft polymer matrices [11]. The ability to control the orientation and movement of these particles allows for the creation of highly adaptable microscale devices capable of performing intricate tasks. This approach has been particularly effective in biomedical applications, where microrobots can navigate through biological fluids or tissues with minimal invasiveness, demonstrating the potential for targeted drug delivery and minimally invasive surgery.

The integration of magnetic nanoparticles into programmable microrobots has been facilitated by advances in fabrication techniques, including 3D printing and microfabrication, which allow for the precise placement and alignment of magnetic elements. These methods enable the creation of complex geometries and tailored magnetic properties, enhancing the functionality and performance of microrobots [11]. Additionally, the use of magnetic fields for actuation offers advantages such as non-contact operation, remote control, and the ability to operate in confined or hazardous environments. Recent developments have focused on improving the efficiency, responsiveness, and scalability of these systems, with an emphasis on achieving multi-degree-of-freedom motion and real-time adaptability. These improvements are critical for expanding the application scope of microrobots in areas such as environmental monitoring, micro-manipulation, and advanced manufacturing.

Despite these advancements, challenges remain in the fabrication, control, and integration of magnetic nanoparticle-based microrobots. Issues such as material compatibility, long-term stability, and the need for precise magnetic field control must be addressed to ensure reliable performance. Furthermore, the development of autonomous and intelligent microrobots requires the integration of sensing and feedback mechanisms, which adds complexity to the design and control systems. Ongoing research aims to overcome these challenges through the development of novel materials, advanced fabrication techniques, and sophisticated control algorithms. As these technologies continue to evolve, magnetic nanoparticles and programmable microrobots are poised to play a transformative role in a wide range of scientific and industrial applications.

### 3.4.2 Hybrid 3D Printing and Thermal Drawing for Complex Fibers
The integration of 3D printing and thermal drawing has emerged as a powerful strategy for fabricating complex fibers with tailored optical and mechanical properties [10]. This hybrid approach leverages the precision of 3D printing to create intricate preforms, which are then thermally drawn into fibers with high aspect ratios and complex cross-sectional geometries. By combining these techniques, it becomes possible to incorporate multiple materials with varying thermal expansion coefficients, enabling the design of multifunctional fiber structures. The process allows for the fabrication of no-core fibers and aspheric lenses, which are essential for high-NA optical systems. This synergy between additive manufacturing and thermal processing addresses the limitations of conventional fiber fabrication, offering a pathway to advanced optical components with enhanced performance.

Thermal drawing plays a critical role in maintaining the structural integrity of the preform during the fiber fabrication process [10]. The preform, typically a macroscopic structure with defined cross-sectional features, is heated and drawn into a fiber while preserving its internal geometry. This method enables the creation of fibers with sub-micron features and complex internal architectures that are otherwise unattainable through traditional methods. The ability to control the thermal drawing parameters, such as temperature and drawing speed, allows for precise tuning of the fiber's final dimensions and properties. When combined with 3D printing, this approach facilitates the rapid prototyping of fiber designs, reducing the time and cost associated with iterative development.

The hybrid methodology also opens new possibilities for integrating functional elements directly into the fiber structure. For instance, the use of 3D-printed micro-objectives on the end of single-mode fibers enables compact and efficient light coupling, which is crucial for applications in quantum optics and biomedical sensing. Additionally, the flexibility of the hybrid process allows for the incorporation of magnetic or structural elements, expanding the range of potential applications. By eliminating the need for post-fabrication assembly, this approach enhances the reliability and performance of the final fiber devices. Overall, the combination of 3D printing and thermal drawing represents a significant advancement in the fabrication of complex, multifunctional fibers [10].

# 4 Robotic and AR-Enhanced Manufacturing Systems

## 4.1 Vision and AI-Driven Robotic Systems

### 4.1.1 AR-Based Pose Estimation and Swarm Robotics
AR-based pose estimation has emerged as a critical component in enhancing the perception capabilities of robotic systems, particularly in complex and dynamic environments. By leveraging augmented reality (AR) technologies, robots can overlay digital information onto the physical world, enabling more accurate and context-aware navigation and interaction. This approach is especially valuable in scenarios where traditional sensor-based methods face limitations, such as in low-light conditions or environments with high levels of occlusion. AR-based systems often integrate visual markers, depth sensing, and machine learning algorithms to estimate the relative position and orientation of objects or robots in real time. These advancements have significantly improved the reliability and adaptability of robotic systems, particularly in applications requiring precise spatial awareness.

In the context of swarm robotics, AR-based pose estimation plays a pivotal role in enabling coordinated and autonomous behavior among multiple robots. Swarm systems often rely on distributed sensing and communication to achieve collective tasks, and AR can enhance this by providing shared situational awareness. For instance, AR can be used to visualize the positions and movements of individual robots within a swarm, allowing for real-time adjustments and improved task execution. This is particularly beneficial in applications such as environmental monitoring, search and rescue, and large-scale manufacturing, where the ability to dynamically adapt to changing conditions is crucial. Additionally, AR can facilitate human-robot interaction by offering intuitive visual feedback, making it easier for operators to monitor and control swarm activities.

The integration of AR into swarm robotics also introduces new challenges, such as ensuring robustness in varying environmental conditions and maintaining consistent performance across multiple robots. These challenges necessitate the development of advanced algorithms for real-time data fusion, error correction, and scalable communication protocols. As the field continues to evolve, the synergy between AR-based pose estimation and swarm robotics holds great promise for creating more intelligent, adaptive, and efficient robotic systems. This convergence is expected to drive innovation in a wide range of applications, from industrial automation to autonomous exploration and beyond.

### 4.1.2 Vision Language Models for 3D Assembly and CAD Generation
Vision language models (VLMs) have emerged as a powerful tool for enabling 3D assembly and CAD generation tasks by leveraging their ability to understand and generate text based on visual input [14]. These models, pre-trained on extensive image-text datasets, can recognize complex visual patterns and interpret textual descriptions, making them suitable for tasks that require both visual and semantic understanding. In the context of 3D assembly, VLMs can be used to interpret design specifications, generate assembly instructions, and even predict the feasibility of component arrangements. Their zero-shot capabilities allow them to generalize across diverse object types and assembly scenarios without the need for extensive task-specific training, which is a significant advantage in dynamic and complex manufacturing environments.

Recent advancements have demonstrated the integration of VLMs into robotic systems for automated 3D assembly, where they serve as a critical component in perception and decision-making pipelines. By combining VLMs with physical simulators and robotic platforms, researchers have developed systems capable of generating constructible 3D designs that adhere to physical constraints. These systems use iterative prompting and simulation-based validation to refine assembly plans, ensuring that generated designs are not only visually plausible but also physically stable. Such approaches enable the automation of tasks that traditionally required human expertise, such as component arrangement, alignment, and verification, thereby reducing the time and cost associated with manual design and assembly processes.

In the domain of CAD generation, VLMs offer a novel pathway for transforming textual descriptions into detailed 3D models. This capability is particularly valuable for users who lack extensive CAD experience, as it allows them to generate designs through natural language input. By integrating VLMs with simulation and validation tools, these systems can ensure that generated models meet functional and structural requirements. Furthermore, the use of VLMs in CAD generation supports the democratization of design and manufacturing, enabling a broader range of users to participate in the creation of complex 3D objects. As research continues to advance, the role of VLMs in 3D assembly and CAD generation is expected to expand, driving innovation in automated manufacturing and design workflows.

# 5 Security, Control, and AI-Driven Process Optimization

## 5.1 AI and Machine Learning in Process Monitoring

### 5.1.1 G-code Trajectory Recovery and IP Protection
G-code trajectory recovery involves the reconstruction of the path and motion instructions embedded within G-code files, which are essential for controlling 3D printers. This process is critical for both legitimate manufacturing and potential adversarial activities, as the G-code file contains the digital blueprint of the printed object. Side-channel attacks, such as those leveraging acoustic, power, or thermal signals, have demonstrated the feasibility of recovering G-code trajectories without direct access to the original design files. These attacks exploit the physical characteristics of the printing process, such as the movement of the nozzle or the energy consumption patterns, to infer the underlying G-code instructions. The accuracy of such reconstructions varies, with some methods achieving high precision in trajectory prediction, while others face limitations due to environmental noise or the complexity of the printed geometry.

IP protection in the context of G-code files is a growing concern, particularly as 3D printing becomes more integrated into industrial and commercial applications [21]. The ability to reverse-engineer G-code files through side-channel attacks poses a significant risk to intellectual property, as adversaries can replicate designs without authorization. To mitigate this risk, researchers and industry professionals have proposed various countermeasures, including encryption, watermarking, and secure communication channels. These solutions aim to prevent unauthorized access to G-code files or detect any tampering or theft. Additionally, the use of digital rights management (DRM) systems has been explored to control the distribution and usage of G-code files, ensuring that only authorized parties can execute or modify them. However, the effectiveness of these measures depends on the specific implementation and the level of security required for the application.

The challenge of protecting G-code IP while maintaining the functionality and efficiency of 3D printing processes remains an active area of research [21]. While some methods focus on securing the G-code file itself, others aim to obscure the relationship between the G-code and the final printed object, making it more difficult for attackers to infer the original design [21]. Techniques such as obfuscation, randomization, and dynamic code generation have been proposed to complicate the reverse-engineering process. However, these approaches must balance security with usability, as excessive complexity can hinder the performance of the 3D printing system. As the threat landscape continues to evolve, ongoing research is necessary to develop robust and scalable solutions for G-code trajectory recovery and IP protection in additive manufacturing environments.

### 5.1.2 Few-Shot Learning for Image Inspection
Few-shot learning has emerged as a powerful technique for image inspection, particularly in scenarios where labeled data is scarce or expensive to obtain. This approach enables models to generalize from a small number of examples, making it highly suitable for applications such as quality control in additive manufacturing, where defects may be rare or vary significantly. By leveraging pre-trained models and adapting them to specific tasks with minimal supervision, few-shot learning reduces the dependency on large annotated datasets, which is a critical advantage in industrial settings. Recent studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of methods like meta-learning and contrastive learning in achieving high accuracy with limited training samples, highlighting their potential for real-time inspection systems.

In the context of image inspection, few-shot learning addresses challenges such as class imbalance, domain shifts, and the need for rapid adaptation to new product designs. Techniques like prototypical networks and matching networks have been employed to learn discriminative features that capture the essential characteristics of objects or defects. These methods are particularly useful in environments where the inspection task evolves over time, such as in dynamic manufacturing processes. Additionally, the integration of pre-trained vision models, such as CLIP, has shown promise in improving generalization across different imaging modalities and lighting conditions, further expanding the applicability of few-shot learning in industrial image inspection.

The practical implementation of few-shot learning for image inspection requires careful consideration of data augmentation, feature extraction, and model architecture. While convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been widely used for image analysis, recent approaches have explored point-based representations and transformer-based models to better handle 3D geometries and high-dimensional data [22]. These advancements enable more accurate and robust inspection systems, particularly in complex environments where traditional methods may struggle. Overall, few-shot learning offers a viable and efficient solution for image inspection tasks, bridging the gap between data-limited scenarios and high-performance industrial applications.

## 5.2 Data-Driven and Optimization Strategies

### 5.2.1 Multi-Objective Planning and Scheduling
Multi-objective planning and scheduling in additive manufacturing (AM) involves optimizing multiple conflicting objectives simultaneously, such as minimizing production time, reducing machine idle time, and maximizing resource utilization [1]. Unlike traditional manufacturing, where planning and scheduling are often treated as separate tasks, AM requires an integrated approach due to the interdependencies between part nesting, batch formation, and machine scheduling [3]. This complexity arises from the need to account for varying part geometries, material properties, and machine capabilities, which collectively influence the efficiency and quality of the final product. As a result, multi-objective planning and scheduling in AM is a critical area of research aimed at enhancing operational efficiency and reducing costs.

Recent studies have focused on developing mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) models and heuristic algorithms to address the challenges of multi-objective planning and scheduling. These approaches often incorporate constraints related to machine availability, part compatibility, and production deadlines, while seeking to balance objectives such as makespan minimization, cost reduction, and energy efficiency. The integration of advanced optimization techniques, such as genetic algorithms and particle swarm optimization, has shown promise in handling the combinatorial complexity of AM planning problems. Additionally, the use of machine learning and data-driven methods has enabled more dynamic and adaptive scheduling strategies, allowing for real-time adjustments based on production feedback and changing demand.

Despite significant progress, several challenges remain in the field of multi-objective planning and scheduling for AM. These include the need for more scalable algorithms that can handle large-scale production environments, the development of benchmark datasets to evaluate solution quality, and the integration of real-time data for improved decision-making. Furthermore, the dynamic nature of AM processes, including variable print times and material constraints, necessitates robust and flexible planning frameworks. Addressing these challenges will be essential for advancing the adoption of AM in industrial settings and ensuring its long-term viability as a competitive manufacturing technology [3].

### 5.2.2 Memory-Augmented LLMs for Order Merging
Memory-augmented large language models (LLMs) have emerged as a promising approach for enhancing order merging in 3D printing systems by incorporating persistent memory mechanisms that enable the retention and utilization of historical decision-making data [23]. These models extend traditional LLMs by integrating external memory modules that store relevant information about past orders, device capabilities, and production constraints. This allows the system to dynamically adjust its decision-making process based on accumulated experience, improving the accuracy and efficiency of order-device matching and interference detection. By leveraging memory-augmented architectures, the models can adapt to evolving production scenarios and maintain a consistent understanding of complex operational contexts.

The application of memory-augmented LLMs in order merging involves the creation of standardized prompt templates that encapsulate the functional and structural characteristics of both printing devices and work orders [23]. These templates serve as a common language for the LLM to interpret and process information, enabling the system to generate optimized merging strategies. The integration of memory-based learning strategies further enhances the systems ability to refine its decisions through iterative learning, ensuring that each merging operation builds upon previous experiences. This approach not only improves the adaptability of the system but also reduces the likelihood of conflicts and inefficiencies during the production process.

Furthermore, memory-augmented LLMs facilitate autonomous decision-making and real-time adjustments in order merging, which is critical for managing the dynamic nature of 3D printing operations. By continuously updating and accessing memory stores, the models can respond to changes in order priorities, machine availability, and production constraints with minimal human intervention. This capability is particularly valuable in environments where rapid and accurate order processing is essential for maintaining production efficiency. Overall, the use of memory-augmented LLMs represents a significant advancement in the automation and optimization of order merging in 3D printing systems [23].

## 5.3 Digital Chain and Workflow Integration

### 5.3.1 End-to-End Process Modeling and Control
End-to-end process modeling and control in additive manufacturing (AM) involves the integration of digital design, process execution, and real-time monitoring to ensure consistent quality and efficiency [1]. This approach encompasses the entire workflow, from the initial 3D model to the final printed part, with a focus on optimizing each stage. Advanced modeling techniques, such as height profile prediction, are critical for maintaining the uniformity of printed layers, which directly impacts the performance of 3D-printed products. These models are often combined with control strategies like feedforward and feedback mechanisms to adjust parameters dynamically during the printing process, enhancing accuracy and reliability.

The control of AM processes is further complicated by the need to manage material properties, layer adhesion, and thermal effects, which vary depending on the printing technology and material used. Recent advancements in process modeling have enabled more precise predictions of these factors, allowing for better control over the final product's quality. Additionally, the integration of machine learning and artificial intelligence into process control systems has shown promise in identifying potential failures and optimizing print parameters in real time. This level of automation not only improves the consistency of AM outputs but also reduces the reliance on human expertise, making the process more accessible and scalable.

End-to-end process modeling also plays a vital role in the scalability and reproducibility of AM, particularly in industries such as pharmaceuticals and aerospace, where precision is paramount. By establishing standardized workflows and leveraging digital twins, manufacturers can replicate processes across different machines and locations, ensuring uniform quality. Furthermore, the ability to monitor and adjust processes in real time supports the transition from prototyping to series production, aligning AM with traditional manufacturing paradigms. As the technology continues to evolve, the development of robust end-to-end control systems will be essential for realizing the full potential of AM in industrial applications.

### 5.3.2 Branch-and-Cut for Integrated Additive Planning
Branch-and-Cut is a powerful exact algorithmic technique that has been increasingly applied to solve complex optimization problems in additive manufacturing (AM) planning. When integrated with AM processes, this method addresses the challenges of scheduling, resource allocation, and order merging by decomposing the problem into manageable subproblems. The approach leverages the strengths of both branch-and-bound and cutting-plane methods, enabling the exploration of the solution space while tightening the problem formulation through the addition of valid inequalities. This integration is particularly beneficial in AM environments where heterogeneous machines with varying build spaces and processing times must be coordinated efficiently to minimize makespan and maximize resource utilization.

In the context of integrated additive planning, Branch-and-Cut is employed to solve mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) models that capture the intricacies of AM production. These models incorporate constraints related to machine availability, job precedence, and material compatibility, ensuring that the generated plans are both feasible and optimal. The algorithm's ability to iteratively refine the solution space through cutting planes allows for the elimination of non-optimal solutions early in the search process, significantly improving computational efficiency. Furthermore, the method's adaptability makes it suitable for handling dynamic and uncertain conditions often encountered in AM environments, such as machine breakdowns or changing order priorities.

The application of Branch-and-Cut in integrated additive planning also facilitates the development of benchmark instances and performance evaluation frameworks, which are essential for comparing different solution approaches. By providing a structured and rigorous methodology, this technique supports the advancement of AM planning systems, enabling more accurate and efficient decision-making. As the complexity of AM operations continues to grow, the use of Branch-and-Cut offers a promising avenue for achieving optimal planning outcomes in both industrial and service-oriented AM settings.

## 5.4 Security and Vulnerability Analysis

### 5.4.1 Security Awareness and Industry Practices
Security awareness in the context of additive manufacturing (AM) has become a critical concern as the technology's integration into industrial ecosystems expands. The emergence of side-channel attacks that can steal design data from 3D printers highlights the need for heightened vigilance and proactive security measures [2]. These attacks exploit physical or operational characteristics of the printing process, such as visual monitoring through cameras or data transmission vulnerabilities, to extract sensitive information. As a result, industry stakeholders must recognize the potential risks associated with the digital chain and implement robust safeguards to protect intellectual property and maintain operational integrity.

Industry practices have evolved to address these security challenges through a combination of technological and procedural solutions. Commercial solutions such as digital rights management (DRM) architectures, direct streaming of design files, and the integration of trusted hardware components like Trusted Platform Modules (TPMs) have been developed to secure the AM workflow. Additionally, companies like Identify3D and Assembrix offer access control mechanisms that restrict unauthorized use of design data. These measures are essential in mitigating risks, particularly in outsourcing scenarios where digital design files are shared with third parties. However, the effectiveness of these practices depends on consistent implementation and ongoing updates to counter emerging threats.

Despite these efforts, the security landscape for AM remains dynamic, requiring continuous research and adaptation. The complexity of AM systems, which involve multiple software and hardware components, introduces numerous potential vulnerabilities that must be systematically addressed. As the industry moves toward more integrated and automated production models, such as Factory-as-a-Service, the need for standardized security protocols becomes even more pressing. Future developments should focus on enhancing transparency, improving threat detection, and fostering collaboration between academia and industry to establish comprehensive security frameworks for AM technologies.

### 5.4.2 Side-Channel Attacks on 3D Printing Systems
Side-channel attacks on 3D printing systems represent a critical vulnerability in the additive manufacturing (AM) ecosystem, particularly as the technology becomes more integrated into industrial and commercial applications [2]. These attacks exploit indirect information leakage from the printing process, such as acoustic emissions, power consumption, or visual data, to infer sensitive information like the digital design of printed objects. Recent studies have demonstrated that such attacks can successfully reconstruct design features with varying degrees of accuracy, raising serious concerns about intellectual property (IP) security and the potential for industrial espionage. The ability to extract design data without direct access to the original CAD or G-code files highlights the need for robust countermeasures in secure AM environments.

Most existing side-channel attacks on 3D printing systems have focused on Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) printers, which are widely used in consumer and small-scale industrial settings [2]. These attacks typically rely on acoustic or power-based signals to reverse-engineer the geometry of printed parts. While early results showed limited accuracy, recent advancements have significantly improved the effectiveness of such techniques, particularly when using power consumption data. However, the industrial-grade AM sector, which often involves more complex and secure systems, remains underexplored in this context. This gap underscores the importance of extending research to more sophisticated printing technologies and evaluating their susceptibility to side-channel exploitation.

The implications of side-channel attacks on 3D printing systems are profound, especially for industries that rely on outsourced manufacturing [2]. The potential for design theft or unauthorized replication of proprietary components poses a significant threat to innovation and competitive advantage. As 3D printing continues to evolve, so too must the security frameworks protecting its digital and physical processes. Future research should focus on developing secure communication protocols, enhancing hardware-level encryption, and implementing real-time monitoring systems to detect and mitigate side-channel vulnerabilities. Addressing these challenges is essential to ensuring the long-term trust and adoption of 3D printing in critical industrial applications [18].

# 6 Future Directions

The proposed future work has the potential to significantly advance the field of additive manufacturing by addressing critical bottlenecks in quality control, automation, and security. Finally, the integration of advanced AI and optimization techniques would enhance the efficiency and adaptability of AM systems, supporting the transition from prototyping to large-scale, high-volume manufacturing.
